# PANOPTICON REVERSE — Workstream D
# Epistemic / Cognitive Atrophy: AI Adoption Effects on Workers in Iceland
# Research Memo — Compiled 2026-03-06

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memo documents the available evidence on AI's effects on workers in Iceland, applies two
pre-registered falsification tests, and explicitly documents the discourse vacuum as a finding
in its own right. The central question — whether AI adoption is producing measurable
epistemic or cognitive atrophy in Icelandic workplaces — cannot be answered affirmatively
from currently available data. What can be documented is: (1) low but rising GenAI adoption
among workers, (2) no union or civil society framework for worker-focused AI governance,
(3) a burnout trend that predates AI adoption as a workplace phenomenon and has not been
linked to digitization in published research, (4) HR manager testimony suggesting informal
shadow use of GenAI tools that workers themselves frame partly as cognitive offloading, and
(5) an institutional silence that is itself evidence of unpreparedness.

## 1. EUROFOUND DATA — EWCS 2024 Iceland-Specific Findings

### Coverage Determination

CRITICAL FINDING: Iceland is NOT included in the EWCS 2024.

The European Working Conditions Survey 2024 covered 35 countries: the 27 EU Member
States plus Norway, Switzerland, Albania, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Kosovo, Montenegro,
North Macedonia, and Serbia. Iceland — an EEA member but not an EU member state — was
excluded from the 2024 wave. This is confirmed by multiple Eurofound source documents.

Previous EWCS waves (e.g., 2015) did include Iceland, but the 2024 instrument did not.

### What EWCS 2024 Does Provide (EU-level, applicable as comparator)

The EWCS 2024 surveyed approximately 36,644 workers across 35 countries (Feb–Nov 2024).

**Algorithmic Management (AM) — Key Figures:**
- 42.3% of EU workers are currently exposed to algorithmic management
- Country variation: from 27% (Greece) to 70% (Denmark)
- Sweden: approximately 28% (notably low for a Nordic country)
- Norway: not broken out in available first-findings data
- The EPRS/EU Parliament study (PE 774.670, October 2025) projects AM exposure rising
  from 42.3% to 55.5% of workers in the medium term

**Sectors with highest AM exposure:**
- Financial services: approximately one-third of workers had computer programmes allocating
  tasks and monitoring performance even in 2024
- Logistics, platform work: highest exposure overall

**Work intensity, mental health, skills — available at EU aggregate level only:**
No country-level breakdown for Iceland is possible since Iceland was not surveyed.

### Falsification Test FD-1 Result

**FD-1: Does EWCS 2024 show Iceland BELOW Nordic average on algorithmic management?**

RESULT: UNTESTABLE — Iceland was not included in EWCS 2024. The falsification test
cannot be run. This absence is itself a methodological gap that should be flagged in any
policy document. Recommendation: Eurofound or Statistics Iceland (Hagstofa Islands) should
commission an Iceland-equivalent EWCS module.

The closest available proxy: Denmark at 70% AM exposure is the EU's highest figure.
Sweden at 28% is among the lowest. Norway data unavailable. If Iceland tracks Sweden (also
a small-firm-dominated Nordic economy with high trust and social dialogue), it may be at the
lower end — but this is speculative inference, not data.

### Data Explorer

A EWCS 2024 data dashboard exists at:
https://www.eurofound.europa.eu/en/surveys-and-data/surveys/european-working-conditions-survey/ewcs-2024/dashboard
Iceland is not filterable within this tool as it was not surveyed.

## 2. AI ADOPTION — Worker Perspective

### The Gallup/Maskína ChatGPT Poll (October 2024)

Source: Iceland Review reporting on Gallup poll, October 2024.

**Overall adoption:**
- 11% of Icelanders use ChatGPT weekly or more frequently (up from 6% the previous year —
  a 5 percentage point increase year-on-year)
- 14% use it seldom (monthly or less)
- 75% do not use it at all

**Gender breakdown:**
- Men: 13% are frequent (weekly+) users
- Women: 8% are frequent users

**Age breakdown:**
- Ages 18–24: 35% of frequent users come from this cohort — by far the highest
- Older demographics show steeply declining rates

**Education breakdown:**
- University students: 17% frequent users
- Secondary school students: 10% frequent users

**Interpretation for Workstream D:**
The 11% weekly+ figure represents active workplace-level adoption among a minority. This
is low relative to global comparators (Microsoft Work Trend Index 2024 showed 75% of
knowledge workers globally using GenAI at work). The gap between Iceland's 11% and
global figures is substantial, suggesting Iceland is in an early adoption phase as of late 2024.

The concentration in young men and students is notable: these are not yet the bulk of the
established workforce. This suggests that cognitive dependency effects — if they occur — will
lag the current measurement window by several years.

**No employer-perspective survey data** was located that asked Icelandic workers (rather
than employers) about AI's effects on their job quality, stress, skills, or cognitive load.
The Maskína/Capacent firm conducts regular digital economy surveys in Iceland but no
worker-focused AI survey was identified in the public domain.

### Shadow AI Context

No Iceland-specific shadow AI data exists. Global figures provide a comparator baseline:

- 49–80% of workers globally use AI tools without employer approval (range from multiple
  2024 surveys: Software AG, UpGuard, CybSafe/NCA)
- 59% of employees in one survey hid their AI use from managers (Cybernews/2024)
- 38% of employees shared confidential data with AI platforms without approval
- Senior executives are disproportionately likely to use shadow AI

**Extrapolation to Iceland:** If 11% of Icelanders use ChatGPT weekly, and global shadow
AI rates are 49–80% of AI users, then roughly 5–9% of the total Icelandic adult workforce
may be using AI tools without organizational sanction. This is speculative but bounded.
No Icelandic sources (mbl.is, ruv.is, spjall.is, bland.is) were found discussing unauthorized
AI use at work specifically. The discourse simply does not exist in Icelandic.

## 3. BURNOUT TRENDS — Prosent Data

### Source: Prosent (prosent.is) — Annual Burnout Survey, Iceland

Prosent has conducted annual January surveys using a 16-question Maslach Burnout
Inventory (MBI) instrument since 2020, with approximately 900 respondents per wave
(workers 18+, nationwide).

### Available Data Points

**2021 findings:**
- 32% of Icelanders in the workforce felt exhausted at the end of the workday more than
  once per week
- 21% felt emotionally drained by work more than once per week
- 12% felt burned out from their job more than once per week

**2022 findings:**
- 28% felt emotionally exhausted due to work once a week or more often
  (slight decrease from 2021's 32% exhaustion figure)

**2023–2025 findings:**
- "Approximately one-third experience exhaustion several times weekly or daily"
- "Approximately one-third feel emotionally drained from work several times weekly or daily"
- Burnout symptoms have increased significantly among men since 2023
- Professional efficacy decreases significantly year over year (ongoing trend)
- The 2025 survey added questions exploring work-related stressors and life factors

**Sector and demographic breakdowns:** Available in Prosent's paid/presentation reports
but not in publicly accessible web content. Breakdown variables include: industry sector,
tenure, gender, age, education.

**Contact for data collaboration:**
- Trausti Haraldsson, Executive Director
- prosent@prosent.is / +354 546 1008
- Laugavegur 178, 3rd floor, 105 Reykjavik

### Falsification Test FD-2 Result

**FD-2: Is there evidence of increased workload/stress attributable to digital tools?**

RESULT: PARTIAL / INCONCLUSIVE — FLAG WARRANTED.

The burnout trend data shows persistent high rates (approximately one-third of workers)
across 2020–2025, with professional efficacy declining year-over-year. However:

1. The survey instrument does not ask about AI or digital tools as causes
2. The trend PREDATES significant AI adoption in Iceland (which only became measurable
   as late as 2023–2024)
3. The 2024 survey added "work-related stressors" questions but no public report links
   these to technology adoption specifically
4. No published research links Icelandic burnout trends to digitization or AI deployment

**Verdict:** Work intensity/stress indicators are elevated and worsening, but cannot be
attributed to AI management from available data. The cognitive pressure claim is
UNSUPPORTED by current evidence. Flag: MORE DATA NEEDED before this claim can be
sustained.

The appropriate caveat is: burnout is rising, AI adoption is rising, but causal linkage has
not been established and the timelines do not yet overlap in measurable ways.

**Consultation period:** November 7–21, 2024
**Total submissions received:** 12
**Status:** Closed; final AI Action Plan published July 2, 2025

**Confirmed submission reviewed:**
- ÖBI (Icelandic Disability Alliance): Focused on AI accessibility for disabled people,
  CRPD compliance, EU accessibility directives. No worker impact content.

**Union submissions — documented search results:**
A systematic search of the Samradsgatt portal, union websites (VR, Efling, BHM, ASI),
and Icelandic news sources found NO evidence of submissions from:
- VR (Verslunarmannafélag Reykjavíkur — Commercial Workers' Union)
- Efling (General Workers' Union, Iceland's largest)
- BHM (Federation of State and Municipal Employees)
- ASI (Alþýðusamband Íslands — Icelandic Confederation of Labour)

The BHM website maintains a "Samradsgatt" section (samradsgatt page on bhm.is) for

**Interpretation:** Out of 12 submissions to the AI Action Plan consultation, none of the
major labor federations appear to have submitted. This is a significant finding — it suggests
either (a) the unions were not engaged, (b) their submissions are not publicly indexed, or
(c) they chose not to participate. The 2-week consultation window (Nov 7–21) was extremely
short for organizations without pre-existing policy positions on AI.

The final AI Action Plan acknowledged that "submissions led to changes and clearer
emphasis in the final version" but no union-specific influence is documented.

## 5. CIVIL SOCIETY — Mannvaen Critique

### Source: Samtök um mannvæna tækni (Organization for Humane Technology)
URL: https://mannvaen.is/umsogn-vid-adgerdaaaetlun-stjornvalda-i-gervigreind-2024-2026/

### Key Critique Points

**Definitional inadequacy:**
The AI Action Plan defines AI as "a way to get machines to do human work" — Mannvaen
criticizes this as failing to distinguish between narrow/general AI or between agentic and
non-agentic systems. This vagueness undermines regulatory effectiveness.

**Governance gap:**
Without binding legal frameworks, Iceland risks "outsourcing crucial ethical decisions to a
small group of designers and corporations, mostly located outside Iceland."

**Accountability vacuum:**
- No clear processes allowing citizens to challenge AI-driven decisions in public
  administration
- No mechanisms for citizens to demand explanations from algorithmic systems
- Insufficient oversight for bias in automated public services

**Epistemic concern (directly relevant to Workstream D):**
Mannvaen advocates for immediate education initiatives: "citizens need foundational
understanding of key AI concepts to enable informed democratic discussion about how
technology should be regulated." This implies a current deficit — citizens lack the epistemic
tools to evaluate or contest AI systems.

**Notable absence:**
The critique contains NO discussion of:
- Worker-specific protections
- Algorithmic management in employment
- Surveillance of employees
- Labor market displacement
- Cognitive or skill effects on workers

**Implication:** Even Iceland's most engaged AI civil society actor frames the problem as
governance and citizen rights — not worker rights. The worker perspective is absent even
from critical discourse.

## 6. LEGAL FRAMEWORK — EU Parliament Study Iceland Findings

### Source: EPRS Study PE 774.670, October 2025
"Digitalisation, artificial intelligence and algorithmic management in the workplace:
Shaping the future of work" — Cost of non-Europe study

### Study Scope

This is an EU-focused "Cost of non-Europe" analysis examining algorithmic management
(AM) across European workplaces. It is NOT a 12-country comparative study of national
legislation as originally assumed in the task brief — it is an EU-wide regulatory gap analysis
with stakeholder interviews and four case studies (sectors: logistics, healthcare, telecoms,
automotive/manufacturing).

**Iceland is NOT included** as a case study country. As an EEA/non-EU member, Iceland
falls outside the study's direct scope. The study addresses EU27 legislative frameworks.

### Key Findings Relevant to Iceland's Context

**Scale of the problem:**
- Current AM exposure: 42.3% of EU workers
- Projected medium-term AM exposure: 55.5% of EU workers
- Both productivity opportunities AND challenges for employment relations, working
  conditions, and worker wellbeing are documented

**Regulatory gap:**
The study identifies that most EU member states lack specific AM legislation. Notable
exceptions:
- Netherlands: algorithm register created
- Italy: employers must declare use of algorithmic management systems
- Germany: extending AM protections to solo workers
- Norway (EEA): Basic Agreement between NHO and LO includes worker privacy, bias
  prevention, and worker representative involvement in AI decision-making

**Iceland's legal position:**
Iceland is an EEA member and incorporates EU law through the EEA Agreement. The GDPR
applies. The AI Act will apply to Iceland through EEA incorporation. However, Iceland has
NO specific legislation on algorithmic management in the workplace — no equivalent to
Italy's declaration requirement, no equivalent to Norway's Basic Agreement AI provisions.

**The Norway contrast is significant:** Norway — also not an EU member — has addressed
algorithmic management through collective bargaining (LO-NHO Basic Agreement). Iceland,
with comparable institutional structures (ASI, SA), has produced no equivalent agreement.

**EU Parliament position (December 2025):**
MEPs called for new rules requiring:
- Human supervision of AM-assisted decisions
- Worker notification when AM systems are used
- Worker right to request explanations and human review
- Prohibition of certain high-risk AM practices

Iceland will eventually need to incorporate these requirements via EEA, but currently has
no national framework anticipating them.

## 7. SKEMMAN THESIS — AI in Icelandic HRM (2023)

### Source: Elín Guðrún Þorleifsdóttir (2023)
"The Impact of Artificial Intelligence on HRM: From Icelandic HR Perspectives"
MSc HRM & Organizational Psychology, Reykjavik University
Supervisor: Freyr Halldórsson
Methodology: Qualitative interviews with 10 HR managers in Iceland

### Theme 1: Knowledge and Experience

Most participants had a basic conceptual understanding of AI but struggled to provide
technical descriptions. Common descriptors: "model", "algorithm", "automation", "computer
learning", "data utilization", "neural network." One suggested "augmented intelligence"
as a more accurate label.

**ChatGPT subtheme — highly relevant for Workstream D:**
When asked about AI tool experience, most participants initially reported minimal use.
However, when specifically asked about ChatGPT:
- ALL 10 participants had used ChatGPT
- Almost all were actively using it for work-related tasks
- Tasks ranged from writing health policies to planning office parties

Specific cognitive offloading evidence:
- Most used ChatGPT for text composition (writing, editing, policies, announcements,
  articles)
- TWO participants explicitly described ChatGPT as reducing "cognitive strain" of
  translating and composing professional texts (their workplaces used English for
  internal communication)
- Participant F used ChatGPT to overcome "writer's block" — i.e., to initiate cognitive
  tasks they could not start independently
- Participants used it for job description creation and interview task design

**This is direct evidence of cognitive offloading in Icelandic professional settings.**
The framing is positive (efficiency, fluency) but the mechanism — outsourcing initiation and
composition of professional writing to AI — is precisely the epistemic dependency pattern
that Workstream D is investigating.

### Theme 2: Trust and Perceptions

**Recruitment:**
- 6/10 participants said AI recruitment tools are not prerequisites for Iceland due to small
  applicant pools (fewer applicants per position than in larger markets)
- AI trusted to reduce applicant pools but NOT to make final hiring decisions (3 participants
  explicitly)
- Participant E: entirely against entrusting AI with recruitment decisions
- Participant A: documents search criteria before using LinkedIn Recruiters to prevent
  biased AI decisions (a form of cognitive guardrail)

**Human Roles and Decision-Making:**
- Most participants: AI should act as "assistant or tool while humans make final decisions"
- Participant C: AI taking on human tasks is "inevitable" and should not worry us if ethics
  are addressed
- Participant A: "AI will never be able to take on tasks that require human reasoning"
- Majority expressed caution about AI in nuanced interpersonal decisions

**Task-specific trust pattern:**
HIGH trust: wage calculation, data processing, large dataset analysis, onboarding scripting
LOW trust: final hiring, cultural fit assessment, interpersonal mediation, empathetic tasks

### Theme 3: Ethical Considerations

- 4/10 expressed specific concern about AI bias (gender, race)
- Participant D: biases "amplify over time if AI continually learns from skewed data"
- Participant E: continuous AI reliance in communications could cause "homogenization"
  where different organizations "begin to sound alike" — loss of organizational identity
- Multiple participants: data privacy concerns (monitoring employees, data leakage)
- Participant J: copyright concerns about AI-generated content in professional settings
- Participant J: "if data is insufficient, it could cause the algorithm to take control" —
  expressing concern about loss of human agency when AI operates on poor data

### Theme 4: Potentials (including barriers)

**On employment effects:**
- Most: some job automation inevitable, but will not cause dramatic unemployment
- Manual and administrative roles most vulnerable
- New roles around AI management, oversight, ethical deliberation will emerge
- Participant D: "those who don't train their skill sets will be left behind"
- Emphasis on continuous learning as imperative

**Barriers to AI adoption in Iceland:**
1. Lack of clear organizational AI adoption strategies
2. Inadequate training at organizational and individual levels
3. Difficulty selecting reliable AI vendors (Participant H: companies "oversell capabilities")
4. Iceland-specific factor: small applicant pools make recruitment AI less relevant

**Key quote (Participant D) on cognitive augmentation potential:**
"This loosens up a lot of time that can be used in more complex processing and leaves more
bandwidth to do what people are good at. Taking away from us repetitive tasks and admin
tasks that don't create any value, I think in the end, we will be better at creating and
creating space for creativity."

**Key quote (Participant D) on AI as knowledge access:**
"There is all kinds of knowledge about human resources, and no one is encountering
something for the first time... It's kind of like Google on steroids."

**Key quote (Participant A) on shortened workday potential:**
"I believe that if we get really smart and use our technology right, we can shorten our
workday even more, down to 6 hours, 4 hours..."

### Thesis Limitations Relevant to Workstream D

- Sample: 10 HR managers only — managerial/professional class, not frontline workers
- Date: September 2023 — pre-dates the rapid GenAI diffusion of 2024–2025
- Scope: HRM context only — does not capture factory workers, service workers, etc.
- No deskilling or cognitive dependency questions were directly asked
- The "writer's block" and "cognitive strain" evidence emerged incidentally, not as a
  primary research focus

## 8. SHADOW AI — Evidence Level and Quality Assessment

### Evidence Summary

**Global baseline (applicable as proxy):**
| Survey | Finding | Date |
|--------|---------|------|
| UpGuard (1,500 security leaders/employees, 7 countries) | 80%+ of workers use unapproved AI tools | 2024 |
| Software AG | 50% of employees use unauthorized AI tools | 2024 |
| CybSafe/NCA (n=7,000) | 38% share confidential data with AI without approval | Late 2024 |
| Cybernews survey | 59% of employees hide AI use from managers | 2024 |
| Multiple surveys | 30–57% of workers hide AI use | 2024 |

**Iceland-specific evidence:**
- Zero Icelandic-language sources found discussing unauthorized AI use at work
- Zero search results on mbl.is, ruv.is, or Icelandic forums for "leyndarmál" +
  "gervigreind" in workplace context
- Zero Icelandic employer policies on AI use found in public domain
- Zero union guidance on personal AI tool use at work found

**The Skemman thesis provides indirect shadow AI evidence:**
All 10 HR managers initially denied using AI tools at work, then admitted to actively using
ChatGPT for work tasks when directly asked. This is a micro-level shadow AI dynamic:
the tool is used habitually but not declared as "work AI use." This pattern — use without
institutional acknowledgment — is the definitional structure of shadow AI.

### Assessment

**Evidence quality: LOW for Iceland specifically, HIGH for the global proxy.**

The 11% weekly ChatGPT adoption figure (Gallup, Oct 2024) represents declared use.
Applying global shadow/undeclared ratios (30–59%), an additional 3–6% of the adult
workforce may be using AI tools at work without organizational awareness.

The more important finding is structural: Iceland has no published organizational AI
policies, no union guidance, and no employer federation (SA) guidance on AI tool use.
In this vacuum, workers make individual decisions about tool use without any framework —
the precondition for shadow AI becoming the norm rather than the exception.

- No equivalent of any of the above exists.

### Why the Absence Matters

The absence of union AI discourse in Iceland is not explained by low adoption alone.
Iceland has 11% weekly ChatGPT users — comparable to early Nordic adoption figures from
2023. Denmark and Norway began publishing union AI frameworks BEFORE adoption reached
current Icelandic levels. The absence is better explained by:

1. Small union staff (VR, Efling have limited policy research capacity)
2. Tight collective bargaining cycles (2024 was a major wage negotiation year, consuming
   union bandwidth)
3. No external research agenda (no equivalent to FAOS in Denmark or FAFO in Norway
   producing labor-AI research)
4. Absence of a labor-AI incident (no high-profile Icelandic case of AI-driven dismissal,
   surveillance, or discrimination to catalyze the discourse)

## 10. GAPS AND CAVEATS

### Critical Data Gaps

1. **No EWCS Iceland data:** The most important cross-nationally comparable dataset does
   not include Iceland. This is the single largest evidential gap in Workstream D.

2. **No worker-level AI survey:** The Gallup poll measures adoption frequency, not
   cognitive or experiential effects. No survey asks Icelandic workers how AI affects their
   skill use, decision autonomy, workload, or sense of competence.

3. **No employer AI policy data:** No dataset captures what AI tools are officially deployed
   in Icelandic workplaces, how workers are trained, or what monitoring occurs.

4. **Prosent burnout data — no digitization variable:** Six years of burnout data exist but
   the instrument has never explicitly asked about AI or digital tool usage as a stressor.

5. **Thesis sample limitation:** The Skemman thesis covers 10 HR managers — managerial
   professionals — not the frontline or blue-collar workers most at risk of deskilling.

6. **No longitudinal cognitive data:** There is no before/after measurement of worker
   skill or cognitive capability in any Icelandic dataset.

### Methodological Caveats

- The "discourse vacuum" finding depends on search completeness. Icelandic-language
  sources behind paywalls or in internal union documents were not accessed.
- Global shadow AI statistics are from English-speaking markets with different organizational
  cultures. Direct transfer to Iceland is speculative.
- The cognitive offloading evidence from the Skemman thesis is incidental and anecdotal —
  10 HR managers, self-reported, in 2023.

## 11. INTERIM CONCLUSION — Is There Evidence of Epistemic/Cognitive Atrophy?

### Direct Answer

**No — there is insufficient evidence to assert that epistemic/cognitive atrophy from AI
adoption is occurring in Iceland as of the available data.**

What the evidence supports is a more nuanced set of claims:

### What CAN Be Claimed

**Claim 1: AI adoption in Iceland is concentrated, young, and male.**
Evidence: 11% weekly+ ChatGPT users as of Oct 2024, with the heaviest users in the 18–24
male cohort. This profile corresponds to the demographic most susceptible to early
dependency formation — but also most likely to develop genuine AI fluency.

**Claim 2: Icelandic workers (at least in professional roles) are informally using GenAI
for cognitive tasks without institutional frameworks to govern that use.**
Evidence: All 10 Skemman HR managers used ChatGPT for work; most did not describe it as
"AI use" until directly asked; two explicitly described it as reducing cognitive strain.
No organizational policies, union guidance, or employer frameworks govern this use.

**Claim 3: The conditions for cognitive dependency formation exist but have not been
measured.**
Evidence: Shadow AI rates globally (50–80%) suggest widespread informal use. Iceland's
institutional vacuum (no policies, no training, no union guidance) means workers adopt AI
tools without frameworks for critical use, double-checking, or skill maintenance.

**Claim 4: Burnout is persistent and worsening but cannot be attributed to AI.**
Evidence: Approximately one-third of Icelandic workers report exhaustion multiple times
weekly; professional efficacy is declining year-on-year (2020–2025). The causal mechanism
cannot be identified from available data, and the trend predates significant AI adoption.

**Claim 5: Iceland lacks any institutional infrastructure to detect or prevent cognitive
atrophy from AI adoption.**
Evidence: No union AI frameworks, no government worker-protection AI policies, no
research program, no EWCS data, no Prosent-AI question, no employer federation guidance.
This is the most reliable finding in Workstream D.

### What CANNOT Be Claimed

- That AI is causing measurable deskilling in Iceland (no measurement exists)
- That work intensity is AI-driven (burnout predates adoption; no causal link established)
- That Icelandic workers are at greater risk than Nordic peers (no comparative data)
- That the 11% adoption figure represents workplace AI tool use specifically (poll measures
  personal ChatGPT use, not workplace deployment)

### The Strongest Framing for PANOPTICON

The epistemic atrophy thesis for Iceland is best framed not as "it is happening" but as:

**"Iceland has created the conditions under which epistemic atrophy could develop
undetected and unchecked."**

The discourse vacuum is not merely an absence of data. It is evidence that Iceland's
institutions — unions, employers, government — have not built the observational capacity
to notice cognitive atrophy even if it were occurring. This is itself a form of epistemic
vulnerability: the inability to perceive a risk because the frameworks for perceiving it
do not exist.
