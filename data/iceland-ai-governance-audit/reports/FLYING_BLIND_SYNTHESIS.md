## Executive Summary

Iceland has adopted an AI strategy affecting 130,000 workers while lacking every
measurement instrument needed to assess AI's actual impact on its labour market,
welfare system, and workforce. This report compiles the evidence across six pillars,
drawing on 50+ academic sources with verified DOIs, 25 corporate investigations,
10 government self-contradictions with direct Icelandic quotes, a complete
Nordic welfare-data infrastructure comparison, and a six-dimension Nordic
measurement matrix showing Iceland as the systematic outlier on every axis.

The central finding: **Iceland governs AI with borrowed statistics, no feedback
loop, and no capacity to detect harm.** Every Nordic neighbour can trace displaced
workers from prior occupation to welfare entry. Iceland cannot. This is not a
data quality issue — it is a structural governance failure.

## Pillar 1: The Measurement Vacuum

*Iceland cannot measure what AI is doing to its workforce.*

### 1.1 The Eurostat Exclusion — Updated April 2026

**Original finding (March 2026):** Iceland was described as definitively excluded from Eurostat's `isoc_eb_ai` — the EU's primary enterprise AI adoption survey.

**Correction (15. apríl 2026):** Hagstofa Íslands conducted the `isoc_e` survey in 2025, including the AI adoption component, after a multi-year hiatus. The AI results were published on hagstofa.is in **March 2026** — the first official domestic measurement of enterprise AI adoption in Iceland. Source: Hagstofa Íslands (personal communication, 15. apríl 2026).

**The revised finding is more precise and more damning:** Iceland collected this data and has not submitted it to Eurostat. Iceland does not appear in comparative international tables — not because it lacks capacity, but because the data pipeline to European statistical infrastructure is not functioning. This is an institutional governance failure, not a measurement capacity gap.

The survey methodology (NACE Rev. 2 sections C-J, L-N, enterprises with 10+
employees) also systematically excludes micro-enterprises — dominant in Iceland's
economy — meaning even submission would undercount AI penetration.

Iceland remains excluded from the Eurofound European Working Conditions Survey
(EWCS) 2024, which for the first time measured generative AI use at work (12% of
EU27 workers). Even the European Parliament's own analysis acknowledges that
"establishing a clear benchmark for the prevalence of algorithmic management
across the EU is not feasible based on the available survey evidence."

**Source:** Eurostat Statistics Explained; Eurofound EWCS 2024 First Findings; Hagstofa Íslands (personal communication, 15. apríl 2026).

### 1.2 The 55% Claim — Unattributed

The AI Action Plan's flagship claim — "um 55% starfa a Islandi, eda um 130 thusund
manns" — contains **zero source citations** in official press releases. The November
7, 2024 press release states "Althjodlegar rannsoknir hafa thannig synt fram a"
("international research has shown") without naming a single study, institution,
or methodology.

The same plan projects GDP growth of 0.8-6% annually through 2029 (174-1,450
billion kronur) without identifying the analytical source or methodology.

Simultaneously, the plan includes **Action B.1** — commissioning a baseline economic
impact study — explicitly acknowledging that the domestic data does not exist.

**The contradiction:** Projections recycled as established facts, while internally
acknowledging the absence of Icelandic-specific data. The government cited 130,000
affected workers as fact while committing to study whether that number is correct.

**Methodology now documented (April 2026):** The joint Hagstofa/Ministry report "Efnahagsleg tækifæri gervigreindar á Íslandi" (Nov 2024) reveals the methodology on pp. 32–33 (Aðferð II). The 55%/127,000 figure is derived from:
1. AI occupational exposure scores from **Felten, Raj & Seamans (2021)** — a US-based dataset
2. Crosswalked US SOC → ISCO-08 via U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics mapping tables
3. Applied to Iceland's Labour Force Survey data (Hagstofa, 2009–2024)

The report itself states: *"Athugið að þessi skipting í fimm flokka er ekki byggð á empirískum mælingum"* ("Note that this classification into five categories is not based on empirical measurements"). The 130,000 figure is a theoretical occupational exposure estimate from American data — not a measurement of Icelandic AI adoption or impact.

**Source:** stjornarradid.is press release Nov 7, 2024; OBI submission referencing Action B.1; Samradsgatt mal/3862; Felten, Raj & Seamans (2021), Strategic Management Journal 42(12):2195–2217; Hagstofa reply 15. apríl 2026.

### 1.3 The 17% Unemployment Measurement Gap

Rikisendurskodun (National Audit Office, December 2024) identified a structural
conflict between Iceland's two principal labour market institutions:

- **Hagstofa Islands** (Statistics Iceland): Uses ILO/Eurostat international
  definitions — enabling cross-country comparison
- **Vinnumalastofnun** (VMST): Tracks those meeting eligibility criteria for
  temporary unemployment financial assistance — an administrative register

These produce different measurements of both the labour force and unemployment
rates. The audit found the most recent comprehensive study of outcomes for
unemployment benefit exhaustion dates to **2014** — over a decade without current
data on what happens to people who exhaust unemployment benefits.

**Source:** Rikisendurskodun report via Althingi thingskjal 350/156, April 3, 2025.

### 1.4 No Domestic AI Survey Equivalent

No Icelandic institution — not Hagstofa, not VMST, not the Ministry — produces
a domestic equivalent of the Eurostat enterprise AI survey. The Varda 2026 report
(n=25,000) is the first quantitative measurement of worker experience with
technology, but it measures worker perception, not enterprise adoption or
deployment.

A six-dimension comparison reveals Iceland as the systematic outlier among Nordic
nations on every measurement axis:

The adoption rates above (DK 42.03% to NO 28.89%) define the Nordic range.
Iceland's position within this range is literally unknowable.

## Pillar 2: What You Find When You Actually Look

*The ICT Trifecta — a case study in invisible displacement.*

### 2.1 The Signal

When the Panopticon investigation manually combined three separate Hagstofa
tables that no institution had combined before, a clear pattern emerged in the
ICT sector (NACE J), 2022-2025:

| Signal | Change | Source Table |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Employment | -1,300 jobs (-11.4%) | VIN01110 |
| Revenue (J62 computer services) | +74.5% (2019→2024) | FYR04101 |
| Vacancy rate | 5.4% to 0.3% (-94%) | JVS00001 |

### 2.2 Counter-Cyclical to All of Europe

This signal is **structurally anomalous** against European baselines:

- EU-wide ICT specialist employment grew +4.8% (2023-2024). Iceland fell -11.4%.
- 57.5% of EU businesses cannot recruit all needed ICT specialists.
- Nordic peers face severe shortages: Sweden projects 70,000 developer deficit,
  Denmark 13,500, Norway 10,000.
- Iceland moved from acute ICT shortage (5.4% vacancy) to near-zero demand (0.3%)
  while the rest of Europe is desperate for workers.

Iceland's ICT decline cannot be explained by a continental downturn. It is
locally specific.

**Source:** Eurostat ICT Specialists in Employment (2024); Euronews Jan 2025.

### 2.3 Revenue Is Real

After adjusting for Iceland's approximately 20-25% cumulative inflation over the
2022-2025 period, the +72% nominal J62 revenue growth is substantially real (note: verified 2019→2024 baseline gives +74.5%) —
not merely a price effect. The revenue growth would require ~72% cumulative
inflation to be entirely nominal, roughly 3x Iceland's actual CPI increase.

### 2.4 Alternative Explanations Ruled Out

| Alternative | Fits All 3 Signals? | Verdict |
|-------------|---------------------|---------|
| Post-COVID correction | No (revenue grew; Iceland SMEs, not Big Tech) | Weak |
| Offshoring | No (domestic revenue grew +72%) | Implausible |
| Reclassification | No (can't explain vacancy collapse) | Partial noise |
| Skills mismatch | No (vacancies would rise, not collapse) | Definitively ruled out |
| Productivity displacement | Yes (all three signals) | Strongly supported |

The skills mismatch explanation is **definitively ruled out** by the vacancy data.
A 0.3% vacancy rate indicates the sector is not looking for workers — this is
demand-side contraction, not supply-side shortage.

**Source:** Acemoglu & Restrepo (2019, JEP); Autor & Salomons (2018, Brookings);
Dallas Fed (2024); Eurofound tech redundancy wave analysis (2024).

### 2.5 Theoretical Grounding

The "revenue up, employment down" signature is textbook Acemoglu-Restrepo
displacement. Their task-based model (2019) identifies the mechanism:

> "Automation always reduces the labor share in value added and may reduce labor
> demand even as it raises productivity."

The ICT sector is the sector most exposed to its own automation tools. AI
coding assistants write ~30% of new software functions by end of 2024 (Cisco
ICT Workforce Consortium). Productivity gains of +26-39% for junior developers
mean fewer developers needed per unit of revenue.

### 2.6 The Trifecta Extended: Six Supporting Legs

Direct queries to five additional Hagstofa tables reveal that the ICT trifecta
is supported by at least six converging signals:

| Signal | Data | Source Table | Implication |
|--------|------|-------------|-------------|
| Productivity surge | +10.5% in 2025 (highest of any sector) | THJ11011 | Firms producing more per worker |
| Wage stagnation | ICT wages 3.1% below economy average by 2024Q4 | LAU04109 | No scarcity premium = no shortage |
| Labour share shrinking | Personnel costs +14% vs value added +28% | FYR08001 | Capital capturing productivity gains |
| Enterprise count growing | 972 to 1,119 ICT firms | FYR08001 | Healthy sector shedding workers, not dying sector |
| Hours declining | Aggregate ICT hours falling alongside headcount | VIN01110 | Not reclassification — actual work reduction |
| Vacancy sector-specific | Construction 6-10%, ICT 0.4% | JVS00001 | Not macro effect — ICT-specific demand collapse |

The enterprise count finding is particularly striking: the number of ICT firms
is **growing** while employment is falling. This rules out the "sector in
decline" narrative entirely. The ICT sector is healthy, profitable, and
expanding — while employing fewer people.

The wage finding rules out a shortage explanation from a different angle: if ICT
workers were scarce, wages would rise. Instead, ICT wages have fallen below the
economy-wide average — consistent with reduced demand for ICT labour, not
insufficient supply.

The productivity surge (+10.5%, highest of any sector in 2025) combined with
employment decline (-11.4%) is the direct empirical signature of technology-
driven labour productivity displacement.

**Source:** Hagstofa PxWeb tables THJ11011, LAU04109, FYR08001, JVS00001.

### 2.7 Mass Layoffs: The Dog That Didn't Bark

The VMST mass layoff register (Feb 2026) shows only two ICT-adjacent mass
layoffs: Alvotech (24 IT positions) and Islensk erfðagreining (45 research
positions). Both are pharma/biotech restructurings, not pure ICT.

This is itself evidence: the ICT employment decline of 1,300 jobs is happening
through **gradual attrition and non-replacement** rather than dramatic layoffs.
Workers leave or contracts end, and positions are not refilled. This mechanism
is inherently harder to detect than mass layoffs — it produces no headlines,
no union notifications, and no VMST register entries.

The absence of mass layoffs in the VMST register does not contradict the ICT
trifecta. It explains why no one noticed.

### 2.8 Knowledge-Occupation Polarization

The broader pattern: knowledge occupations -9,300 (-6.9%) while physical/service
occupations +6,900 (+7.4%) in a single year (2024-2025). This is not classical
polarization (middle hollowing out) — it is a top-to-bottom shift. High-skill,
non-routine cognitive occupations, predicted by classical models to be protected
from automation, are declining first.

This is consistent with the AI-era revision of the polarization hypothesis. The
IMF's Gen-AI Staff Discussion Note (2024) finds that "unlike previous
technological waves, AI differs from traditional automation by potentially
affecting jobs of workers throughout the income distribution."

**Source:** Goos, Manning & Salomons (2014, AER); IMF SDN/2024/001; Acemoglu &
Restrepo (2020, Cambridge JRES).

The discourse inflates the technological reality.

## Pillar 6: The Welfare Black Box

*The welfare system cannot trace whether displaced workers are entering it.*

### 6.1 Iceland's Data Gap

Tryggingastofnun (TR) publishes disability/rehabilitation data in exactly five
dimensions: time period, age, place of residence, gender, and medical assessment
category. Occupation is not among them.

TR does not publish — and may not collect — the prior occupation or employment
sector of disability claimants. Statistics Iceland's 2024 experimental statistics
on disabled people cover current employment status but explicitly lack
"historical employment data," "transition data," and "career pathway analysis."

**Critical admission:** "Hagstofa Islands does not appear to be mandated to collect
comprehensive disability information" — University of Iceland Social Science
Research Institute.

The two agencies responsible for welfare data (TR) and labour market data
(Hagstofa) are not systematically linked for research purposes.

### 6.2 What Neighbours Can Do

| Country | Can trace: "ICT worker -> lost job -> disability with psychiatric diagnosis"? | Register | Since |
|---------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|----------|-------|
| Denmark | YES — weekly granularity, individual-level | DREAM + IDA + CPR linkage | 1991 |
| Norway | YES — published as routine SSB statistics | FD-Trygd + SSB employment register | 1992 |
| Sweden | YES — 500 variables, 95% occupation completeness | LISA | 1990 |
| Finland | YES — via Findata single-application system | Kela + ETK + FOLK | Active |
| **Iceland** | **NO** | **Nothing** | **--** |

### 6.3 Norway: The Devastating Comparison

Statistics Norway (SSB) publishes a dedicated statistical report titled **"Previous
occupations and industries among new recipients of disability benefit and work
assessment allowance."** Methodology: last registered occupation over a ten-year
period preceding reception of disability benefit.

Finding: Sales and service workers are overrepresented by approximately 13
percentage points relative to their share among all wage earners. Professionals
are underrepresented.

**This exact question — "what occupation were disability claimants in before
claiming?" — is routinely answered in Norway as official statistics. In Iceland,
it cannot be answered at all.**

**Source:** ssb.no/en/sosiale-forhold-og-kriminalitet/trygd-og-stonad/artikler/
previous-occupations-and-industries-among-new-recipients-of-disability-benefit-
and-work-asessment-allowance

### 6.4 Denmark: What Enabled Humlum & Vestergaard (2025)

The NBER paper "Large Language Models, Small Labor Market Effects" (WP 33777)
used DREAM (109 weekly welfare codes since 1991) + IDA (200+ variables,
occupation since 1980) + CPR linkage to study 25,000 workers across 11
AI-exposed occupations. They found null effects on earnings and hours with
high statistical precision (<2% effect size).

**This study is structurally impossible to replicate in Iceland** because:
(a) No matched employer-employee database of comparable scope exists
(b) No universal digital survey distribution linked to admin records exists
(c) Occupation classification is at coarser granularity

The null finding is itself data infrastructure-dependent. Denmark can produce
confident null results. Iceland cannot produce results at all.
