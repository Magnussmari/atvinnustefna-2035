## 3. EUROSTAT ADOPTION RATES — Iceland vs Nordic vs EU

### Critical Finding: Iceland's AI Adoption Data Exists But Is Not in Eurostat

**Update — 15. apríl 2026 (Hagstofa reply):** Iceland conducted the `isoc_e` enterprise ICT survey in 2025, including the AI adoption component (`isoc_eb_ai`). Results were published on hagstofa.is in March 2026. However, the data has **not been submitted to Eurostat** — meaning Iceland remains invisible in all international comparative tables.

Iceland is therefore not "excluded" in a structural sense — it is **self-excluded from international benchmarking** by not submitting data it has collected. This is a governance choice, not a capacity gap. The revised framing: Iceland has domestic AI adoption data for the first time, but the data is not integrated into the European statistical infrastructure that policymakers use for comparison.

## 4. AI ACTION PLAN ANALYSIS — Critical Review of Government Claims

### Document: "Aðgerðaáætlun um gervigreind" (November 2024)
Source: Háskóla-, iðnaðar- og nýsköpunarráðuneytið (Ministry of Higher Education, Industry and Innovation)
Published: November 2024 (initial); Final version July 2, 2025

### The "55% of Jobs Affected" Claim

**Claim:** "About 55% of jobs in Iceland, or about 130 thousand people, are considered likely to be greatly affected by artificial intelligence; in total about 75% of jobs will be affected to some extent."

**Source Attribution:** Joint report by the Ministry and Statistics Iceland (Hagstofa). The report is Action B.1 in the Action Plan — meaning the "Skýrsla um efnahagsleg tækifæri gervigreindar á Íslandi" (Report on economic opportunities of AI in Iceland) is itself an action item, suggesting it was produced to support the action plan rather than independently validated beforehand.

**Methodological Concern:** The 55% figure appears to apply international task-exposure frameworks (OECD, McKinsey Global Institute, or Accenture methodology) to the Icelandic occupational distribution. The Action Plan PDF text does not contain footnotes or external citations visible in the extracted text. The claim is a projection/exposure estimate, NOT a measurement of observed displacement.

**The "30% Time Saving" Claim**

**Claim:** "Public employees spend an average of 30% of their working time on tasks that could be automated with the help of AI."

**Source:** This is cited as coming from "international analyses." The Action Plan text does not specify which international study. The 30% figure closely matches McKinsey Global Institute's 2023 "A new future of work" report, which estimated 28-30% of work activities could be automated across knowledge workers. Iceland appears to have applied this international estimate to the public sector without an Iceland-specific study.

**The "8% Feel Adequately Trained" Claim**

**Claim:** Only 8% of Icelandic workers feel adequately trained to use AI tools.

**Source:** Multiple secondary sources cite this figure in the context of the AI Action Plan, but no primary survey was identifiable. This appears to come from a specific Icelandic survey conducted for or referenced in the Action Plan. It was not found in the November 2024 PDF text extracted — likely in the separately published economic opportunities report (Action B.1).

### Government Spending Commitments

**Finding:** The July 2025 final Action Plan announcement states "financing for all measures is already in place" but discloses no specific budget amounts. The November 2024 draft contains no monetary figures for overall investment. Individual actions are assigned to ministries without budget allocation.

**Specific Financial Mechanisms Identified:**
- Action D.3: Grant fund for AI implementation in public agencies (amount unspecified)
- Action E.1: Grants for AI solutions in healthcare (amount unspecified)
- Action B.2: Support for continuing education in AI/data literacy (unspecified)
- Participation in Digital Europe Programme (EU co-funded)

### Labor Market Mitigation Measures

The Action Plan explicitly includes:
- Action B.2: "Continuing education and data literacy support for labor market participants; special emphasis on reaching groups likely to face negative impacts from AI"
- Action A.4: Impact analysis of AI on different social groups (elderly, disabled, immigrants, low-income, unemployed)
- The plan notes the goal is "skill transformation" not displacement prevention

**Critical Gap:** The Action Plan contains no binding wage protection, retraining entitlement, or transition fund for displaced workers. All labor market measures are framed as education/upskilling rather than economic safety nets.

## 6. TEMPORAL SEQUENCE ANALYSIS

### Pattern: AI Deployment → Efficiency Gain → FTE Reduction

#### Landsbankinn — STRONGEST EVIDENCE

```
Timeline:
2022-2023: Chatbot "Elli" deployed, scaled to 60% query resolution
2023:      849 average FTE
2024:      811 average FTE (-38 positions)
2024:      Cost-to-income 32.4% — "never been lower"
2024:      Annual report explicitly links FTE decline to "technological advancements"
```

**Assessment:** This is a confirmed temporal sequence (AI deployment → FTE reduction) with explicit causal language from the company itself. However, the reduction is modest (-4.5% of average FTE, ~38 positions), and it is unclear what portion of the reduction is directly attributable to the chatbot versus other factors (branch network restructuring via "the Hub," general efficiency programs).

#### Íslandsbanki — PARTIAL EVIDENCE

```
Timeline:
2024 (Q2-Q3): "Sam" AI assistant deployed for employees
2024 (Q3-Q4): Microsoft Copilot integration in progress
2024 (Q4):    20-person restructuring announced — stated reason: "regulatory changes and rapid technological development"
Year-end 2024: 733 employees
```

**Assessment:** The 20-person reduction coincides temporally with AI tool deployment. Management cited "rapid technological development" as a co-reason. However, the primary stated reason was regulatory compliance restructuring, and the bank reorganized into new divisions (Individuals, Commercial Banking, Corporate & Investors). Causal attribution to AI is partial, not definitive.

#### Arion Bank — COUNTER-EVIDENCE

```
Timeline:
2024: Active digital/app expansion
2024: Statement: "AI will increase efficiency in coming years"
2024: Employees: 858 (up from 822)
```

**Assessment:** Arion Bank increased headcount despite AI investment. This directly contradicts a simple AI→displacement model for the banking sector. Their AI statements are forward-looking, not describing current automation of roles.

### Cross-Sector Pattern

**Banking sector summary (all three banks combined):**
- Landsbankinn: avg FTE -38 (confirmed tech link)
- Íslandsbanki: -~20 (partial tech link)
- Arion: +36

**Net banking sector headcount change: approximately -22 positions** across three institutions covering ~2,600 total employees. This is a ~0.8% reduction — modest and non-dramatic.

**Tech sector (per Statistics Iceland 2024):** Turnover increased most among 12 major industries. Tech sector employment GROWING in 2024.

**Vinnumálastofnun mass layoffs 2024-2026:** No mass layoffs in IT/tech sector were recorded in 2024 or through mid-2025. August 2025: services, silica, food. December 2025: cinema, wholesale fish. October 2025: fish processing. January 2026: aquaculture. February 2026: **24 IT employees** — first IT-sector mass layoff recorded.

## 7. FALSIFICATION TEST RESULTS

### FC-1: Can you establish a temporal sequence AI_announcement → headcount_reduction for ANY major Icelandic employer?

**RESULT: PARTIALLY CONFIRMED — FLAG RAISED FOR CAUSATION**

**Evidence FOR:**

- Íslandsbanki: 20-person reduction coinciding with AI tool deployment; "rapid technological development" cited as factor

**Evidence AGAINST full causal claim:**
- Arion Bank: +36 FTE despite AI investment
- Reductions are small-scale (sub-50 per institution)
- Confounding factors present in both cases (Íslandsbanki had regulatory restructuring; Landsbankinn had branch network Hub restructuring)
- No company explicitly stated "we reduced staff because of AI"

**Assessment:** A temporal correlation exists for Landsbankinn (strongest case) and Íslandsbanki (weaker case). However, the causal chain cannot be cleanly isolated from concurrent restructuring drivers. The corporate language at Landsbankinn ("decreased in tandem with technological advancements") is the strongest available evidence of intentional linkage.

**FLAG: CAUSAL CHAIN WEAKLY SUPPORTED — correlational evidence exists but causation is unconfirmed and scale is modest.**

## 8. GAPS AND CAVEATS

### Data Gaps

1. **No Iceland-specific Eurostat AI adoption data** — Iceland excluded from isoc_eb_ai survey. The most authoritative comparative data simply does not exist.

2. **TED procurement inaccessible** — Direct contract-level data requires TED database query or Ríkiskaup/FMA direct inquiry. Cannot confirm or deny AI procurement spending growth.

3. **Hagstofa enterprise ICT survey** — Statistics Iceland publishes ICT usage surveys but the 2024 AI-specific data was not accessible through web research. The joint Ministry/Hagstofa economic opportunities report (Action B.1) may contain the most reliable Iceland-specific data but was not publicly available.

4. **Íslandsbanki full annual report** — PDF too large for WebFetch (>10MB). Employee trend data reconstructed from Q4 2024 press release and web summaries.

5. **Marel, Eimskip, Icelandair, Siminn, Vodafone** — No specific AI adoption announcements found for any of these major employers. Either they have not made public announcements or their AI adoption is not being publicly reported in accessible sources.

6. **"8% feel adequately trained" source** — Not locatable in the AI Action Plan PDF or accessible secondary sources. Likely from a survey conducted for the separately published economic opportunities report.

7. **30% time saving — exact source** — Cited as "international analyses" in secondary sources; closest match is McKinsey Global Institute estimates but no confirmed direct citation found.

8. **Vinnumálastofnun 2024 monthly data** — Only recent months accessed. Full 2024 breakdown by sector not available through web search.

### Methodological Caveats

- The AI Action Plan's statistical claims (55%, 30%) appear to apply international estimates to Iceland rather than measuring Iceland specifically. The commissioned report (B.1) suggests Iceland's government was aware of this gap.
- Landsbankinn's FTE decline is the strongest temporal evidence available, but the absolute numbers (~38 positions from a base of 849) represent modest displacement relative to the bank's scale.
- Arion Bank's headcount growth serves as an important counter-example that should not be ignored.
- The tech sector's growth (Statistics Iceland 2024: turnover up most among 12 industries) suggests net positive employment effects from digitization in the sector producing AI tools, partially offsetting displacement in user sectors.

## 9. INTERIM CONCLUSION

### Is there evidence of corporate AI adoption driving labor displacement in Iceland?

**Short answer: Weak correlational evidence exists; definitive causal evidence does not.**

**What the evidence supports:**

1. **AI tools are genuinely being deployed** in Iceland's banking sector. Landsbankinn's Elli chatbot, Íslandsbanki's "Sam" and Copilot integration, and Arion Bank's digital-first strategy are confirmed real deployments, not aspirational claims.

2. **A modest temporal correlation exists** between AI tool deployment and FTE reduction at Landsbankinn (the clearest case): average FTE dropped 38 positions (4.5%) in 2024 while explicitly citing technology as a factor.

3. **The government's "55% affected" projection** is based on applied international methodology (job exposure analysis), not observed displacement in Iceland. It is a forward-looking risk assessment, not a current measurement. The source is a government-commissioned report, not an independent study.

4. **Corporate AI adoption and labor displacement are occurring simultaneously but not at alarming scale** — the banking sector's net headcount change across three institutions is approximately -22 positions (with Arion Bank actually growing).

**What the evidence does not support:**

1. There is **no evidence of mass AI-driven layoffs** in Iceland through 2025. Vinnumálastofnun records show mass layoffs concentrated in fish processing, food industry, and services — not technology-driven displacement.

2. The **causal chain is confounded**: restructuring at both banks coincided with regulatory changes, not only AI adoption.

3. Iceland's enterprise AI adoption rate **cannot be compared to EU average** due to the Eurostat gap. The adoption premise of the investigation cannot be falsified or confirmed with current data.

4. **No procurement evidence** was found to confirm or deny public sector AI spending growth.

**For the PANOPTICON REVERSE investigation:**

The corporate accountability track finds that Iceland's AI adoption narrative is real but modest in its current labor market effects. The most significant finding is Landsbankinn's explicit corporate acknowledgment that headcount declined in tandem with technological advancement — this is a documented causal claim from a primary source and represents the clearest evidence available for the AI → labor change sequence.

The government's statistical claims in the AI Action Plan require methodological scrutiny: the 55% and 30% figures appear to be international estimates applied to Iceland without Iceland-specific validation. The government was itself commissioning the baseline study (Action B.1) as part of the action plan — acknowledging the evidential gap while simultaneously citing the projections as established fact.
