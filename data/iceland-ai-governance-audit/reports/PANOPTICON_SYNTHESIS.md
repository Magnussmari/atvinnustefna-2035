## VERDICT: QUALIFIED

The AI displacement hypothesis is **neither sustained nor falsified**. The evidence supports a qualified intermediate finding: Iceland's labour market exhibits a real and unusual polarization pattern consistent with technology-driven restructuring, but the causal chain from AI adoption to job loss to welfare entry cannot be closed from available data. The hypothesis survives contact with the data as a candidate explanation — one of several — for observed phenomena that are themselves genuine and concerning.

**In plain terms:** Something happened to knowledge-occupation employment in Iceland in 2024-2025 that is not explained by seasonal cycles, demographic aging, or the physical-sector construction/tourism boom. AI displacement is consistent with the pattern. So are at least four other explanations. The data cannot distinguish between them.

---

## 1. WHAT CONVERGES ACROSS WORKSTREAMS

### 1.1 The Polarization Signal (A + C)

The strongest finding in the investigation is the simultaneous contraction of knowledge-intensive occupations and expansion of physical/service occupations in 2024-2025.

**Workstream A established:**
- Knowledge occupations (Professionals, Managers, Associate Professionals, Clerks): **-9,300 jobs** in 2024→2025 (-6.9%)
- Physical/service occupations: **+6,900 jobs** (+7.4%)
- ICT sector (NACE J): **-1,300 jobs** 2023→2025 (-11.4%)
- Professional/scientific/technical (NACE M): **-900 jobs** 2023→2025 (-6.9%)

**Workstream C established:**
- Landsbankinn: avg FTE -38 (-4.5%) with explicit corporate statement linking decline to "technological advancements"
- Islandsbanki: -20 positions, citing "rapid technological development" as co-factor
- Arion Bank: **+36 FTE** (counter-evidence)
- Net documented banking displacement: ~22 positions

**The gap between these findings is the investigation's central puzzle.** Workstream A identifies a macro signal of 9,300 knowledge-occupation jobs disappearing. Workstream C can attribute approximately 22 of these to documented corporate AI adoption. The remaining ~9,278 are unexplained at the firm level. Either:

(a) The AI-to-displacement chain is operating in hundreds of firms making small, undocumented reductions (plausible but unverifiable);
(b) The 9,300 figure reflects other restructuring dynamics — fiscal tightening, public sector hiring pauses, or reclassification effects in the LFS; or
(c) The occupation-level data has sampling noise at this magnitude (~4% of the 225,000 workforce, within LFS confidence intervals for some cells).

**Assessment:** The polarization is real at the level of macro statistics. Whether it is AI-driven cannot be determined from available evidence. The scale mismatch between macro signal (~9,300) and firm-level evidence (~22) is a factor of 400x.

### 1.2 The Psychiatric Welfare Acceleration (B + A)

**Workstream B established:**
- Disability/rehabilitation recipients: 6.3% → 9.4% of labour market (2000-2024), a 49% relative increase
- Psychiatric/stress diagnoses: **179% growth** vs 26% for other categories (2000-2024)
- Younger cohort (18-49): **+57% prevalence increase**, women 18-49: **+79%**
- Rehabilitation expenditure: **+40% YoY** (2023→2024) — the sharpest acceleration signal

**Workstream A established:**
- Prime-age (25-54) unemployment reached **4.9% in Q4 2025** — highest in post-COVID dataset
- Female 25-54 unemployment **exceeded male** for first time in Q4 2025 (5.0% vs 4.9%)
- Q4 2025 broke the normal seasonal recovery pattern

**The convergence:** Both workstreams show accelerating stress on the younger working-age cohort, with women disproportionately affected. The welfare system shows psychiatric diagnoses dominating new entrants; the labour market shows knowledge occupations contracting. If there were occupation-level data for welfare claimants, these two signals could be tested for overlap. That data does not exist publicly.

**The divergence:** The welfare trend is a 35-year secular phenomenon. Psychiatric diagnoses have been the dominant growth driver since at least 1990 (Thorlacius et al. 2010). The 2024-2025 labour market shift is a 1-2 year phenomenon. The timescales do not match cleanly. The welfare acceleration cannot be attributed to AI displacement without flow-timing data showing recent-onset claimants coming from AI-affected sectors — and that data is the critical gap this investigation cannot fill.

### 1.3 The Institutional Vacuum (C + D)

**Workstream C established:**
- Iceland **excluded from Eurostat enterprise AI survey** (isoc_eb_ai) — adoption rate unknown
- Government's "55% of jobs affected" claim borrowed from international estimates, not locally measured
- Government commissioned its own baseline study (Action B.1) as part of the AI Action Plan — acknowledging the measurement gap while citing projections as established fact

**Workstream D established:**
- Iceland **excluded from EWCS 2024** — no worker-conditions data
- Zero union AI publications (VR, Efling, BHM, ASI)
- Zero Icelandic-language search results for "reikniritsstjornun" (algorithmic management)
- Zero consultation submissions from labour federations on the AI Action Plan
- Norway has LO-NHO Basic Agreement AI provisions; Denmark has Hilfr2; Iceland has nothing

**The convergence:** Iceland is simultaneously implementing AI policy and operating without the measurement infrastructure to evaluate its effects. The country is excluded from the two most important European survey instruments (Eurostat AI adoption + EWCS worker conditions). It has no domestic equivalent. Unions have produced no AI frameworks. The government's statistical claims are international projections applied to Iceland without local validation.

**This is itself a finding.** The Panopticon investigation reveals that Iceland has created the conditions under which AI displacement could occur undetected: no enterprise AI adoption measurement, no worker-conditions survey, no union monitoring, no occupation-level welfare data linkage. The absence of evidence is not evidence of absence — it is evidence of institutional blindness.

## 2. WHAT CONTRADICTS THE HYPOTHESIS

### 2.1 The Denmark Null Result

Humlum & Vestergaard (2025), the strongest comparable study, used Danish administrative registers and difference-in-differences to measure LLM adoption effects. **Finding: null effects on earnings and hours.** Denmark is the most comparable Nordic economy with the highest enterprise AI adoption rate in Europe (42% in 2025). If AI displacement is not detectable in Denmark with gold-standard data, the prior probability for Iceland — smaller, less AI-adopted, and measured with inferior data — should be low.

**Counter-argument:** Denmark has the world's most developed flexicurity system. Active labour market policies, generous unemployment insurance, and rapid retraining may absorb displacement that would manifest as welfare entry in Iceland's less flexible system. The null result in Denmark does not preclude non-null results in Iceland if the institutional response differs.

**Assessment:** The Denmark result is the single strongest piece of evidence against the displacement hypothesis. It should anchor expectations toward null. But institutional differences make it a comparator, not a definitive falsification.

### 2.2 Arion Bank Counter-Evidence

Arion Bank added 36 FTE in 2024 despite active digital/AI investment. This directly contradicts a simple AI→displacement model for banking and demonstrates that AI adoption and headcount growth can coexist.

### 2.3 Physical-Sector Dominance

The dominant labour market story of 2024-2025 is construction boom (+3,300 jobs), tourism expansion (+2,200), and public sector growth (+4,500). These physical and service sectors are growing vigorously and absorbing workers. The economy is not in recession. The knowledge-sector contraction is a secondary phenomenon within a broadly expanding economy.

### 2.4 Confounding Factors

Multiple alternative explanations compete with AI displacement:

| Confound | Scale | Evidence |
|----------|-------|----------|
| **Play airline collapse** (Sept 29, 2025) | ~420+ direct jobs | Tourism/aviation, not AI — but contributes to Q4 2025 unemployment anomaly |
| **Foreign national unemployment** | 55% of registered unemployed | 8.9% rate vs 2.3% Icelandic — drives headline figures without knowledge-worker implications |
| **17% measurement gap** (Hagstofa vs VMST) | 34,000 persons | Systematic confound in all unemployment analysis |
| **Post-pandemic mental health** | COVID sequelae explicitly in welfare growth cluster | Inseparable from AI-era trends in available data |
| **35-year secular diagnostic trend** | Psychiatric diagnoses rising since 1990 | Predates AI by decades |
| **Neurodevelopmental expansion** | ADHD/autism rates 2-3x comparable populations | Diagnostic practice, not labour market |
| **LFS sampling variance** | ~5,200 respondents | Some age×sex×quarter cells have wide confidence intervals |

## 3. FALSIFICATION GATE ASSESSMENT

The mission plan specified: "IF FA-1 AND FA-2 both trigger, the hypothesis is effectively dead."

### FA-1 (Physical vs Knowledge sector concentration): **DID NOT TRIGGER**

Job losses do NOT concentrate exclusively in physical sectors. The ICT sector lost 1,300 jobs and the Professional/Scientific sector lost 900 — both knowledge sectors. At the occupation level, knowledge roles contracted by 9,300. FA-1 did not trigger; the hypothesis survives this gate.

However, FA-1 did not cleanly confirm the hypothesis either. Physical sectors are growing faster than knowledge sectors are contracting. The net economy added jobs 2023→2025. The knowledge-sector loss is real but is a minority phenomenon within an expanding economy.

### FA-2 (Seasonality explanation): **PARTIALLY TRIGGERED**

Q1 2025 had a strong seasonal component. But Q4 2025 at 5.0% — matching the seasonal peak — broke the pattern. Seasonality explains part of the 2025 picture, not all of it. The structural floor appears to have risen ~1.5-2pp.

### FA-3 (Age distribution): **DID NOT CLEANLY TRIGGER**

Youth dominates rate increases (16-24 at 12.2% in Q1 2025). But prime-age (25-54) reached 4.9% in Q4 2025 — unprecedented in the dataset. The age distribution evidence is ambiguous: it weakens the hypothesis on rate grounds but supports it on absolute-number grounds.

### Global Gate: **NOT TRIGGERED.** The investigation proceeds to full synthesis because FA-1 did not trigger. The hypothesis is weakened but alive.

### Full Falsification Scorecard

| Test | Result | Strength for Hypothesis |
|------|--------|------------------------|
| FA-1 | MIXED | Partially supports (knowledge occupations -9,300) |
| FA-2 | PARTIAL | Weakens (seasonal component) but Q4 anomaly supports |
| FA-3 | MIXED | Weakens (youth dominates) but prime-age rising |
| FB-1 | CONFIRMED | Supports (psychiatric 179% vs 26%) |
| FB-2 | PARTIALLY FALSIFIED | Supports (younger cohort growing faster) |
| FB-3 | CONFIRMED DISCONTINUITY | Neutral (measurement artifact acknowledged) |
| FC-1 | PARTIALLY CONFIRMED | Supports (Landsbankinn temporal sequence) |
| FC-2 | INDETERMINATE | No evidence either way |
| FC-3 | UNKNOWN | Iceland not in Eurostat survey |
| FD-1 | UNTESTABLE | Iceland excluded from EWCS 2024 |
| FD-2 | PARTIAL FLAG | Cannot attribute burnout to AI |

**Score: 3 support, 3 mixed/partial, 3 indeterminate/untestable, 2 weaken.**

The falsification framework was designed to kill the hypothesis if the data demanded it. The data did not demand it — but it also did not confirm it. The hypothesis persists in a qualified state.

## 4. THE CAUSAL CHAIN ASSESSMENT

The mission brief's terminal query asks for: **AI Integration → sector job loss → psychological disability claim from same demographic cohort, within 6-12 months.**

### Link 1: AI Integration → Sector Job Loss

**Established for:** Landsbankinn (avg FTE -38, explicit tech citation). Partially established for Islandsbanki (-20, tech co-cited). Not established for any other firm.

**At macro level:** ICT sector -1,300 jobs coincides temporally with AI tool deployment period (2023-2025). But correlation ≠ causation. The ICT sector decline could reflect offshoring, project completion cycles, or fiscal consolidation.

**Strength: WEAK.** One firm with explicit evidence, one with partial, and a macro correlation without micro-level confirmation for 97% of the signal.

### Link 2: Sector Job Loss → Welfare Entry

**NOT ESTABLISHED.** No data links unemployment by sector or occupation to subsequent disability/rehabilitation claims. The data exists (TR admin records + tax/labour registry linkage) but requires formal research approval and is not publicly accessible.

**What can be said:** The welfare system is absorbing more people with psychiatric diagnoses, and the labour market is shedding knowledge workers. Both trends are real. Whether the people are the same people is unknown.

**Strength: ABSENT.** This is the missing link the mission plan identified in Phase 1.

### Link 3: Same Demographic Cohort

**PARTIALLY SUPPORTED by indirect evidence.** Both the labour market contraction and the welfare acceleration disproportionately affect:
- The 25-54 / 18-49 age bands (prime working age)
- Women (female 25-54 unemployment at 5.0% in Q4 2025; women 18-49 welfare prevalence +79%)

The demographic overlap is suggestive but not probative. These are the largest demographic groups in both systems; overlap is expected even without causal connection.

**Strength: SUGGESTIVE.** Demographic pattern matches but cannot confirm individual-level linkage.

### Terminal Query Expected Result

As predicted in the mission plan: **the terminal query returns limited results.** The AFFECTS_COHORT edges between welfare statistics and corporate actions cannot be populated with occupation-level data because that data is not public. The query is structurally sound and will work if future data allows population of the missing edges.

## 5. THE ICT PRODUCTIVITY DISPLACEMENT PATTERN

One finding warrants specific attention because it was not in the original mission brief's hypothesis but emerged from the data.

**ICT sector (NACE J) 2023→2025:**
- Employment: -1,300 jobs (-11.4%)
- VAT turnover: +34% (Nov-Dec 2025 vs same period prior year)

Revenue growing while employment falls is the textbook productivity displacement pattern. It suggests the sector is producing more output with fewer workers — exactly what AI-augmented productivity would produce.

**Caveats:**
- The turnover figure is Nov-Dec only (two months, potentially seasonal)
- The +34% could reflect price increases, project timing, or one-off contracts
- The employment figure is annual average, creating temporal mismatch

**Assessment:** This is the single most suggestive datapoint for AI displacement in the investigation. It is also a single sector, two-month figure that cannot bear the weight of a causal conclusion. Flag for monitoring.

## 6. THE ACEMOGLU CONSTRAINT

Acemoglu (2024-25) models AI's macroeconomic impact and estimates TFP gains of 0.53-0.66% over 10 years — modest by historical standards. His framework suggests that claims of massive near-term displacement are inconsistent with AI's actual economic footprint, which remains small relative to the overall economy.

For Iceland specifically: even if AI is displacing some knowledge workers, the scale of that displacement should be modest relative to the 9,300 knowledge-occupation contraction observed. If Acemoglu is correct, AI can explain a fraction of the signal — perhaps hundreds of jobs, not thousands.

This creates a tension: either (a) Iceland is experiencing displacement at a rate significantly exceeding Acemoglu's model predictions, (b) the 9,300 figure is inflated by non-AI factors, or (c) the Acemoglu model underestimates displacement in small, highly-digitized economies.

## 7. WHAT THIS INVESTIGATION ACTUALLY FOUND

Setting aside the displacement hypothesis, the investigation produced several findings that stand independently:

### Finding 1: Iceland is flying blind on AI.
No enterprise AI adoption survey. No worker-conditions survey with AI module. No union AI frameworks. No occupation-level welfare data linkage. The government's AI Action Plan cites international projections as though they were domestic measurements. **Iceland has built AI policy on borrowed statistics.**

### Finding 2: The labour market polarization of 2024-2025 is real and unusual.
Whatever its cause, the simultaneous contraction of knowledge occupations (-9,300) and expansion of physical/service occupations (+6,900) within a single year is the largest such shift in the available dataset. It warrants monitoring and explanation regardless of the AI hypothesis.

### Finding 3: The welfare system's psychiatric acceleration is accelerating in younger cohorts.
Women 18-49 with psychiatric/stress diagnoses increased 79% (2000-2024). Rehabilitation expenditure grew 40% in a single year. This is a social policy emergency independent of AI.

### Finding 4: The measurement infrastructure is inadequate.
The 17% gap between Hagstofa and VMST means Iceland's government cannot accurately state how many people are unemployed. The disability system publishes stock counts but not flow data. No public data links welfare entry to prior occupation. These gaps would need to be closed before any causal claim — for or against AI displacement — could be validated.

### Finding 5: The discourse vacuum is an institutional failure.
Norway and Denmark have union AI frameworks, collective agreement provisions, and active research programs. Iceland has none. This is not explained by lower adoption — Iceland's 11% weekly ChatGPT use is comparable to early Nordic figures when those countries' unions began engaging. The institutional response is lagging the adoption curve.

## 8. THESIS IMPLICATIONS

For Magnus Smari Smarason's thesis work, this investigation provides:

### Usable Findings
1. **Empirical evidence of labour market polarization in Iceland** — the -9,300 / +6,900 shift is original analysis from Hagstofa PxWeb data not previously published in this form
2. **The institutional blindness finding** — Iceland excluded from two key European surveys, no domestic equivalent, policy based on borrowed statistics
3. **The discourse vacuum documentation** — systematically documented absence of union/civil society AI engagement, with Nordic comparison
4. **Welfare acceleration data** — synthesis of TR, Laeknabladid, and Hagstofa sources into a coherent temporal picture not previously assembled
5. **Corporate AI evidence** — Landsbankinn's explicit tech-headcount statement is primary-source evidence for thesis citation

### What NOT to Claim
- AI is causing the knowledge-occupation contraction (not established)
- Welfare expansion is AI-driven (not established; 35-year trend dominant)
- Workers are experiencing cognitive atrophy (no measurement exists)
- Iceland's situation is worse than Denmark's (Denmark null result + better data)

### Recommended Thesis Framing
The PANOPTICON investigation demonstrates that **the conditions for unmonitored AI displacement exist in Iceland** — not that displacement has been confirmed. The value is in documenting what cannot be seen rather than what can. The thesis can argue that Iceland's institutional infrastructure is insufficient to detect displacement even if it occurs, and that this institutional blindness is itself a governance failure consistent with the broader "responsibility fog" framework.
