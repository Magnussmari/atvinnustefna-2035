## Preamble: What This Report Is

This report is a clinical, data-driven analysis of Iceland's newly published employment strategy — the *Vaxtarplan til 2035* (Growth Plan to 2035), its accompanying *Aðgerðaáætlun 2026-2027* (Action Plan), and the *Stöðugreining* (Economic Status Analysis) that diagnoses Iceland's current structural position — against the full body of empirical evidence about Iceland's labor market, AI adoption trajectory, and welfare system.

The government's three documents, taken together, constitute the most ambitious industrial policy statement Iceland has produced in a generation. They deserve serious engagement — which means subjecting their premises, data, and omissions to the same scrutiny any evidence-based policy should face.

The conclusion of that scrutiny is not that the government is incompetent. It is that the government has produced a coherent, optimistic, internally consistent document that is structurally incapable of detecting the harms it most needs to prevent. The documents identify real problems and propose credible solutions. They also contain a systematic blind spot — visible only when the strategy is read alongside the empirical evidence the strategy itself does not cite.

That blind spot is the subject of this report.

## Part I: The Economic Diagnosis Iceland Gets Right

### 1.1 The Per-Capita Growth Paradox

The *Stöðugreining* deserves credit for an unusually frank statistical self-assessment. The document's opening slide states the central paradox clearly:

> *"Þótt hagvöxtur Íslands hafi verið góður á tímabilinu 2010–2024 [...] hafa íbúar Íslands ekki notið sömu hagvaxtar og þjóðir sem við berum okkur saman við."*
> ("Although Iceland's GDP growth has been good in the period 2010–2024, the inhabitants of Iceland have not enjoyed the same growth as the nations we compare ourselves to.")

The numbers behind this statement are stark. Iceland's real GDP grew at 2.9% annually from 2010 to 2024 — better than Denmark (2.1%), Finland (0.8%), Norway (1.5%), Sweden (2.1%), and nearly matching the United States (2.4%). On headline growth, Iceland looks like a success story.

Per capita, the picture inverts. Iceland's per-capita GDP growth from 2010 to 2024 was 1.1% annually, compared to a peer-group average of approximately 1.5%. More strikingly, between 2020 and 2024, per-capita GDP growth was effectively zero (the document shows -0.4% for part of this period). In 2024, Iceland's GDP per capita stood at USD 57,955 (2015 prices) — below Denmark's USD 63,230 and Norway's USD 79,670.

The Stöðugreining is explicit about the mechanism: *"Um 60% hagvaxtar á tímabilinu 2010–2024 má rekja til aukinnar framboðs vinnuafls."* ("About 60% of growth in the period 2010–2024 can be attributed to increased labor supply.") Iceland's population grew by 24.2% over this period — approximately 75,000 people, two-thirds of them foreign nationals. The economy grew, but primarily by adding more workers, not by making each worker more productive.

**What this means:** Iceland has been running a growth model that is demographically intensive and productivity-weak. The strategy document acknowledges this directly: the next phase of development must be driven by productivity, not labor supply growth.

| Indicator | Iceland | Denmark | Norway | Sweden | Finland |
|-----------|---------|---------|--------|--------|---------|
| Real GDP growth 2010-2024 (annual avg.) | 2.9% | 2.1% | 1.5% | 2.1% | 0.8% |
| Per-capita GDP growth (annual avg.) | 1.1% | ~1.5% | ~1.5% | ~1.4% | ~0.8% |
| GDP per capita 2024 (USD 2015 prices) | $57,955 | $63,230 | $79,670 | ~$58,000 | $44,855 |
| Population growth 2010-2024 | +24.2% | ~+9% | ~+12% | ~+11% | ~+4% |

*Sources: Stöðugreining 2026, slides 3, 5, 6, 8; OECD National Accounts*

### 1.1-A The GDP Growth Decomposition: Where the Numbers Come From

The Stöðugreining's most analytically rigorous single slide (Slide 8) decomposes Iceland's total real GDP growth of 1,165 billion ISK (from 2,392 to 3,557 billion ISK, constant 2020 prices) into its contributing factors. The waterfall is worth reading in full because it tells the story the strategy is trying to move away from:

| Growth Factor | ISK Contribution (bn) | Direction | Share of Total |
|---------------|----------------------|-----------|----------------|
| Population growth (fólksfjölgun) | +580 bn | Positive | 49.8% |
| Labour productivity (framleiðni vinnuafls) | +491 bn | Positive | 42.1% |
| Unemployment reduction (minnkun atvinnuleysis) | +171 bn | Positive | 14.7% |
| Age composition change (breytt aldurssamsetning) | +106 bn | Positive | 9.1% |
| Labour participation increase (aukin atvinnuþátttaka) | +97 bn | Positive | 8.3% |
| Shorter working week (styttri vinnuvika) | -279 bn | **Negative** | -23.9% |
| **Total net growth** | **+1,165 bn** | | **100%** |

*Source: Stöðugreining 2026, Slide 8; Hagstofa Íslands*

The decomposition reveals three things the strategy must navigate. First, population growth was the single largest contributor to GDP growth — larger even than productivity gains — accounting for nearly half of all growth. This is the "labor supply model" the Vaxtarplan correctly identifies as exhausted. Second, the shorter working week — the product of deliberate labor market reform and collective bargaining outcomes, including Iceland's internationally noted 4-day workweek pilots — subtracted 279 billion ISK from output. This is not a policy failure; it reflects workers choosing more leisure. But it means the productivity gains that did occur were partly offset by reduced hours, and any future strategy that assumes sustained productivity increases must account for continued working-time pressure downward. Third, and most critically: productivity growth contributed 491 billion ISK — real and substantial, but not sufficient to compensate for the demographic drag and the working-week cost.

The Stöðugreining is explicit on the implication:

> *"Um 40% af raunhagvexti síðustu 15 ára má rekja til aukinnar framleiðni vinnuafls. Án aukningar vinnuafls hefði meðaltal hagvaxtar verið 1.1% yfir tímabilið sem er í takt við aukningu VLF á mann."*
> ["Approximately 40% of real growth over the last 15 years can be attributed to increased labour productivity. Without workforce increases, average growth would have been 1.1% — in line with GDP per capita growth."]

The math closes: productivity-only growth would have been 1.1% annually — exactly equal to actual per-capita GDP growth. Every additional percentage point of headline growth came from adding workers, not from making existing workers more productive.

### 1.1-B The International Productivity Comparison: The Full Six-Column Picture

Slide 17 of the Stöðugreining provides the most clinically damning international comparison in the document. The slide's title states the conclusion directly:

> *"Mikil atvinnuþátttaka á Íslandi vegur upp veikari fjármagnsgrunn og lægri heildarþáttaframleiðni"*
> ["High labour participation in Iceland compensates for a weaker capital base and lower total factor productivity"]

The full data table:

|---------|--------------------------|------------------------|----------------|-------------------------------|-------------|
| Iceland | 65.6 | **59.2** (+15% vs avg) | 1,434 (-1%) | 394 (-8% vs avg) | **114** (-12% vs avg) |
| Denmark | 73.7 | 51.2 | 1,379 | 474 | 144 |
| Finland | 55.6 | 48.6 | 1,509 | 356 | 117 |
| Norway | 91.1 | 51.8 | 1,407 | 533 | 167 |
| Sweden | 63.3 | 49.6 | 1,431 | 406 | 130 |
| UK | 52.5 | 48.5 | 1,512 | 408 | 106 |
| **Peer average** | 67.0 | 51.5 | 1,445 | 428 | 130 |

*Source: Stöðugreining 2026, Slide 17; World Bank, Nordic Statistics Offices, OECD, Eurostat*

Iceland's structural position is now legible with precision. The country achieves a near-average GDP per capita (USD 65.6k vs. peer average 67.0k) through an extraordinary employment participation rate — nearly 60% of the total population is employed, versus a peer average of 51.5%, a gap of almost 15 percentage points. That participation advantage is Iceland's single competitive lever.

The cost of that lever is visible in the other columns. Capital per worker is 8% below the peer average (USD 394k vs. 428k). Total factor productivity — the most important single number in the table, reflecting how efficiently Iceland combines its labour and capital — is 12% below the peer average (index 114 vs. 130).

> *"Að auki virðast framleiðsluþættir, þ.e. vinnuafl og fjármagn, nýtast verst á Íslandi þar sem heildarþáttaframleiðnin er 12% undir meðaltali samanburðarríkja."*
> ["In addition, production factors — labour and capital — appear to be used least efficiently in Iceland, as total factor productivity is 12% below the average of comparison countries."]

The strategy's prescription — improve productivity — is correct. But the mechanism matters. Iceland is already working near maximum labour intensity. The only remaining levers are capital deepening (more machines per worker) and TFP improvement (better organizational practices, technology, and knowledge use). AI deployment could, in principle, address both. The question the strategy does not answer is how AI deployment, absent any governance framework, will distribute those productivity gains — whether they accrue to workers as higher wages, to firms as higher margins, or to a narrow set of capital owners as higher returns on technology investment.

### 1.1-C The Capital Paradox: More Total Capital, Less Capital Per Worker

The capital data from Slide 12 deserves standalone treatment because it illustrates, in a single arithmetic step, how Iceland's workforce growth model produced structural productivity weakness:

- Total business capital stock (fjármunaeign): 3,554 bn ISK in 2010 → 4,598 bn ISK in 2024 = **+29%**
- Total workers: 173,000 in 2010 → 239,000 in 2024 = **+38%**
- Capital per worker: 20.5 million ISK in 2010 → 19.2 million ISK in 2024 = **-6%**

The economy acquired 1,044 billion ISK in additional capital over fourteen years — a real, substantial investment — while simultaneously adding 66,000 workers. The workers arrived faster than the capital. The result, expressed as the slide's key bullet:

> *"Í dag er 6% minna fjármagn að baki hverju stöðugildi en í upphafi tímabilsins."*
> ["Today there is 6% less capital behind each full-time equivalent than at the start of the period."]

This is the productivity trap's arithmetic: the denominator (workers) grew faster than the numerator (capital). Every worker in Iceland today has access to slightly less capital equipment, infrastructure, and technology than they did fourteen years ago, despite the economy collectively having more of it in absolute terms.

The Vaxtarplan's prescription of maintaining "robust capital formation" in coming years addresses this correctly at the macro level. The AI infrastructure investment (ISK 1.1 billion) is a small-scale attempt at capital deepening in the high-productivity domain. What the strategy does not model is the interaction effect: if AI infrastructure enables firms to produce more output with fewer workers, the capital-per-remaining-worker figure could improve dramatically while total employment falls. This is the Klarna case — staff halved, revenue doubled — expressed in macroeconomic terms.

### 1.1-D The Export Composition Shift: What Grew, What Shrank, What Disappeared

The Stöðugreining's export data (Slides 15, 19, 20) documents a structural transformation in Iceland's export base that has received insufficient analytical attention:

**Export composition by sector (% of total exports):**

| Sector | 2010 | 2024 | Change |
|--------|------|------|--------|
| Tourism (ferðaþjónusta) | 19% | 32% | +13 pp |
| Energy-intensive industry (orkusækinn iðnaður) | 28% | 17% | -11 pp |
| Fisheries (sjávarútvegur) | 25% | 18% | -7 pp |
| Aquaculture (lagareldi) | 0% | 3% | +3 pp |
| Creative/knowledge industries (hugverkageirinn) | ~11% | 15% | +4 pp |
| Other | 16% | 16% | 0 pp |

*Source: Stöðugreining 2026, Slide 15; Hagstofa Íslands*

The headline reading is that tourism has displaced energy-intensive industry and fisheries as Iceland's dominant export sector. But the goods export waterfall (Slide 20) contains a finding that cuts against the strategy's optimistic framing: **total goods exports actually declined by 1.1%** from 2010 to 2024, falling from 969,606 million ISK to 958,960 million ISK. Iceland's export revenue growth over fourteen years was entirely driven by service exports — primarily tourism — while the goods export base contracted in real terms.

The goods decline decomposition:
- Energy-intensive industry: -84,715 million ISK
- Seafood products: -34,634 million ISK
- Pharmaceuticals: -8,623 million ISK
- Medical products: +50,572 million ISK
- Farmed fish/aquaculture: +49,267 million ISK
- Other categories: net -11,913 million ISK

The two sectors saving goods export performance — medical products (+50.5 bn ISK) and aquaculture (+49.3 bn ISK) — are precisely the sectors that face regulatory and governance challenges the strategy is still attempting to resolve (life sciences investment, Actions 3.8-3.9; aquaculture framework, Actions 4.5-4.8). Meanwhile, Iceland's two historically dominant goods exports — energy-intensive aluminum and silicon smelting, and seafood — have both contracted significantly in their export contribution.

The service export story is simpler and more alarming for long-term resilience: +326,315 million ISK in tourism, +44,621 million ISK in IT/telecom, +32,761 million ISK in transport, with smaller gains in business services, culture, and intellectual property. Tourism drove more than three-quarters of total service export growth. Iceland's export base is more dependent on tourism in 2024 than at any point in its economic history.

The Vaxtarplan's emphasis on export diversification is well-motivated. What it does not reckon with is that the sectors it proposes to diversify into — technology, health sciences, data centers, creative industries — are precisely the sectors most exposed to AI-driven productivity gains and the employment consequences that follow.

### 1.2 The Infrastructure Strain Is Real

The Stöðugreining's infrastructure data is some of the most important analysis in the document. Rapid population growth has produced measurable strain across multiple systems:

- Real estate prices have doubled since 2010 (price index 2x)
- Traffic on Route 1 (the Ring Road) has increased 81%: from 690,751 km annually to 1,247,450 km
- Foreign-national students in Icelandic schools increased 195%: from 2,712 to 8,004
- Hospital beds per 100,000 population are down 30% from their peak

The investment ratio has been below Iceland's historical average for most of the period, though it has recently risen to 26.1% of GDP. More troubling: despite a 29% increase in total capital stock, capital per full-time equivalent worker has *fallen* 6% (from 20.5 to 19.2 million ISK). The economy acquired more capital in absolute terms while the workforce grew faster than capital could be deployed productively.

This is the productivity trap in numerical form: the workforce grew by ~40,000-50,000 people while capital per worker declined. The result is visible in total factor productivity (TFP): Iceland runs approximately 12% below its peer-group average on TFP, despite a comparable employment rate (+15% above peer average on employment) and lower capital intensity (-8% below on capital per worker).

### 1.3 The Knowledge Desert in the Job Market

The Stöðugreining's sector analysis (Slide 16) contains the most revealing single chart in the document. It shows that of the approximately 49,608 net new jobs created in Iceland's current growth phase, only around 1% were in sectors with productivity 10% or more above the national average. The vast majority of new employment is in low and medium productivity sectors.

The NACE sectoral data confirms this: Iceland's strongest employment growth is in construction (+21.9% from 2023 to 2025), accommodation and food service (+17.6%), agriculture (+18.1%), and manufacturing (+8.9%). Information and communications (NACE J) — the technology sector — lost 1,300 jobs (-11.4%) over the same period. Professional, scientific and technical activities (NACE M) lost 900 jobs (-6.9%).

The economy is growing. The high-productivity knowledge sectors within it are contracting.

The productivity decomposition from Slide 16 is worth presenting in its full numerical form. The 49,608 net new jobs divide as follows:

| Category | Definition | Export sector | Domestic sector | Total | % of all new jobs |
|----------|-----------|---------------|-----------------|-------|-------------------|
| Low productivity | At least 10% below national average | 14,893 | 19,611 | 34,504 | 69.5% |
| Near average | Within 10% of national average | 0 | 14,452 | 14,452 | 29.1% |
| High productivity | At least 10% above national average | -906 | 1,558 | **652** | **1.3%** |
| **Total** | | | | **49,608** | |

*Source: Stöðugreining 2026, Slide 16; Hagstofa Íslands*

The negative figure in high-productivity export sectors (-906) means Iceland actually lost high-productivity export jobs over this period while gaining low-productivity domestic ones. The 652 net new high-productivity jobs represent 1.3% of all net new employment. The strategy the government has built in response to this data proposes to improve productivity by investing in the sectors that are already generating that 1.3%. The other 98.7% — the workers in low and medium productivity roles — face a strategy that will, at best, gradually transform the economy around them and, at worst, accelerate the displacement of their existing roles without creating accessible alternatives.

**The STEM enrollment picture — and what the social science figure reveals**

The Stöðugreining's education data (Slide 21) shows the STEM deficit in full comparative context. Iceland's 17% STEM enrollment versus the 24% OECD average is well-known. Less discussed is the structural imbalance that explains why Iceland's STEM enrollment is low: the social sciences.

| Country | STEM enrollment (% of enrolled) | Social sciences enrollment (% of enrolled) |
|---------|--------------------------------|----------------------------------------------|
| Iceland | 17% | **44%** |
| Denmark | 24% | 25% |
| Finland | 29% | 23% |
| Norway | 22% | 35% |
| Sweden | 29% | 31% |
| UK | 23% | 34% |
| OECD avg | 24% | 29% |
| **Peer average** | **24%** | **32%** |

*Source: Stöðugreining 2026, Slide 21; OECD Education at a Glance 2023*

Iceland's social science enrollment at 44% is 12 percentage points above the peer average of 32%. Iceland has more graduates per capita in social sciences than almost any comparable country — not because Iceland needs more social scientists, but because the pipeline produces them abundantly while producing technical graduates sparsely. The 44% social science share and the 17% STEM share are not independent facts; they are two sides of the same enrollment imbalance.

The AI transition implication is direct: social sciences, humanities, communications, business administration, and law — the domains where Iceland's university graduates concentrate — are precisely the fields identified by McKinsey, Goldman Sachs, and Acemoglu as most exposed to AI-driven task automation. Iceland is currently producing graduates in the highest-AI-exposure fields at twice the OECD average rate, while underproducing graduates in the lowest-AI-exposure technical fields.

The Stöðugreining's own language, in direct quote, makes the job quality diagnosis plain. The document states: *"Með öðrum orðum fer hlutfall þeirra atvinnugreina sem eru með hlutfallslega lága framleiðni hratt vaxandi á tímabilinu."* — "In other words, the proportion of industries with relatively low productivity is growing rapidly over the period." This is an official government diagnosis, in a document published as the analytical foundation for Iceland's ten-year growth strategy, that the current trajectory of job creation is moving in the wrong direction. ## Part II: The Strategy's Architecture

### 2.1 Five Emphases, Four Priorities, 28 Actions (Not 35)

The *Vaxtarplan til 2035* frames Iceland's 10-year industrial strategy around five general emphases:

1. Stabilization and diversification of exports
2. Strengthening R&D capacity and innovation
3. Better use of natural resources and capital
4. Workforce development and skill formation
5. Improved infrastructure and energy systems

These general emphases are operationalized through four *forgangsverkefni* (priority areas) in the *Aðgerðaáætlun 2026-2027*, which contains **28 concrete actions** (the strategy documents describe these as 35 in places; the definitive count based on the action plan's full inventory is 28 numbered actions across four groupings). Note that the Vaxtarplan document itself contains no budget allocations — the strategy document is entirely qualitative. All budget commitments are in the separate Aðgerðaáætlun.

- Health technology: 200–400 m.kr.
- **Total new committed spending: approximately 2,100–2,500 m.kr. year one; 1,000 m.kr./year thereafter**

The headline observation is stark: **20 of 28 actions — 71% of the action plan by count — have no dedicated funding.** They are assigned to existing ministry budgets, which in practice means they compete against existing programmes, depend on internal reprioritization, and carry no enforcement mechanism if they are not resourced. The strategy's claim that "financing for all measures is already in place" is accurate for the 8 funded actions. For the remaining 20, "within framework" is not financing — it is an assignment.

The second observation is about the nature of what is funded. The 200 m.kr./year for Action 5 (MNH continuing education) is the only item that could, at a stretch, be characterized as worker transition support. The action plan frames it as "expanding access to part-time study and shorter pathways at secondary/university level" — in other words, improving formal educational access for workers who wish to upskill voluntarily. It is not a reskilling entitlement. It is not a transition fund. It is not available to workers displaced by automation who need intensive retraining on a defined timeline.

The third observation is the complete absence of any budget line for the measurement infrastructure the strategy's own analysis requires. There is no allocation to bring Iceland into the Eurostat AI adoption survey. There is no funding for occupation-level welfare data at Tryggingastofnun. There is no research budget for AI-labour economics. The strategy spends 1.1 billion ISK on AI infrastructure and zero on understanding what that infrastructure will do to Iceland's workforce.

**Priority 1: Energy and infrastructure transformation** (7 actions in Grouping 1 above)
The theory here is that cheap, renewable Icelandic energy is an underexploited competitive advantage. Actions include regulatory simplification for energy investment, electricity grid reinforcement, establishing a framework for data centers, a new foreign investment reception law, and an economic security framework. The data center provision (Action 1.4) is notably specific: Iceland is positioning to attract large-scale computing infrastructure on the strength of its 160,000 GWh renewable electricity endowment, with future electricity demand projected to reach 35-45 TWh annually by 2050 under the energy transition scenario.

**Priority 2: Human capital, knowledge, and skills** (7 actions)
The cornerstone is STEM education reform at every level: Actions 2.1-2.3 address STEM curriculum in K-12, university STEM expansion, and curriculum materials. Action 2.4 targets vocational education enrollment. The total budget earmarked across these actions is approximately ISK 2.3 billion annually: 300 million for teacher training, 200 million for university STEM, 300 million for curriculum development, 500 million for vocational expansion, 200 million for flexible learning pathways. Actions 2.6-2.7 address foreign specialist attraction — including a fast-track permit system for high-skill workers and a tax deduction for international specialists relocating to Iceland.

**Priority 3: Science and innovation as value creation** (9 actions)
The most ambitious priority includes: freeing pension fund investment restrictions to allow more domestic equity investment (Action 3.1), establishing AI infrastructure (Action 3.3, ISK 1.1 billion), carbon capture innovation frameworks, startup environment reform, R&D support instruments, and life and health sciences investment (Actions 3.8-3.9, ISK 200-400 million).

**Priority 4: Social contract around natural resource industries** (10 actions)
Tourism-sector rationalization (site fees, international marketing, streamlining), fisheries research for quota sustainability, aquaculture regulation reform, and carbon management policy. This priority reflects the political sensitivity of Iceland's extractive industries and the need to modernize their governance while maintaining their export contribution.

### 2.2 The Strategy's Internal Logic

The strategy has a coherent theory of change. Traced through the documents, the argument runs:

1. Iceland's growth to date has been labor-intensive and productivity-weak
2. The next decade must be productivity-driven
3. Productivity growth requires three inputs: better infrastructure (energy, data centers, connectivity), better human capital (STEM, vocational, lifelong learning), and better innovation capacity (AI, R&D, startup financing)
4. Export diversification reduces dangerous dependence on tourism and fisheries, both cyclically volatile
5. The natural resource industries require governance reform to remain socially legitimate and economically optimized

This is not an irrational argument. Many elements of it are well-grounded in the Stöðugreining's empirical analysis. The strategy correctly identifies the productivity gap, the capital underinvestment, the STEM deficit (17% enrollment vs. 24% OECD average), and the need for infrastructure modernization.

### 2.3 The Metrics That Measure Nothing

The Vaxtarplan presents nine formal *mælikvarðar* (metrics) across two categories — five AFRAKSTUR (output) metrics and four INNTAK (input) metrics — as the accountability framework for the strategy's success. The output metrics are: increased GDP per capita, higher labor productivity, increased and diversified exports, lower emissions intensity of exports, and increased business income in all regions. The input metrics are: increased business investment and FDI, increased electricity production, increased R&D investment, and more STEM graduates.

Not one of these nine metrics has a quantified target. Not one has a baseline figure stated in the document. Not one has a measurement methodology specified. Not one has a date by which it will be measured.

These are directional labels, not accountability instruments. A metric without a target is indistinguishable from an aspiration. "Increased GDP per capita" as a metric means: if per-capita GDP rises, the strategy succeeded; if it falls, external factors are to blame. The absence of baselines means there is no reference point against which to evaluate whether the change is attributable to the strategy. The absence of targets means there is no standard against which to declare the strategy a failure.

Critically, none of the nine metrics addresses employment stability, worker transitions, or welfare system inflows. "Higher labor productivity" is the closest — but productivity growth from AI can be achieved through both augmentation (workers become more productive) and displacement (fewer workers produce the same output). The metric cannot distinguish between these outcomes. "Increased business income in all regions" does not require that this income translate into employment. A regionalized data center strategy could satisfy this metric while reducing regional employment.

The strategy presents nine metrics and zero labor protection indicators. It presents nine metrics and zero displacement detection indicators. It presents nine metrics and zero welfare system monitoring indicators. These omissions are not gaps in the metrics — they are structural choices about what the strategy will be held accountable for.

The flaw is not in the theory of change. It is in what the theory omits — and in the accountability framework that was designed so those omissions cannot trigger a strategic revision.

## Part III: The Six Omissions

### 3.1 Omission One: The Measurement Vacuum

The strategy is built on data it does not have.

Iceland is excluded from the two most important European measurement instruments for AI's labor market impact. The Eurostat *isoc_eb_ai* enterprise AI adoption survey — which tracks AI use among firms with 10 or more employees across EU member states plus Norway — does not include Iceland. Despite Iceland's EEA membership, which gives it access to the single market and makes it subject to EU rules, it is absent from the survey that would tell Iceland what fraction of its enterprises use AI, which sectors are leading, and how adoption is accelerating.

The 2024 figures from the Eurostat survey for comparable countries:

| Country | Enterprise AI adoption 2023 | 2024 | 2025 | YoY Growth (24→25) |
|---------|------------------------------|------|------|---------------------|
| Denmark | ~14% | 27.6% | 42.0% | +14.5 pp |
| Sweden | ~16% | 25.1% | 35.0% | +10.0 pp |
| Finland | ~11% | 24.4% | 37.8% | +13.4 pp |
| Norway | ~10% | 20.8% | 28.9% | +8.1 pp |
| **Iceland** | **N/A** | **N/A** | **N/A** | **Not surveyed** |

*Source: Eurostat Statistics Explained — Use of Artificial Intelligence in Enterprises (2025 edition)*

Iceland also does not participate in the Eurofound *European Working Conditions Survey* (EWCS 2024). The EWCS surveyed approximately 36,644 workers across 35 countries, including EU member states, Norway, Switzerland, and Western Balkans nations. Iceland, despite having participated in earlier waves (including 2015), was excluded from the 2024 instrument. As a result:

- Iceland has no measurement of the share of workers subject to algorithmic management
- Iceland has no measurement of work intensity trends in relation to AI adoption
- Iceland has no official data on whether digital tools are increasing or decreasing worker wellbeing
- Iceland has no comparable data on training adequacy for technology-exposed workers

The EWCS found 42.3% of EU workers currently exposed to algorithmic management — with Denmark at the EU high of 70% and projections suggesting this will rise to 55.5% of EU workers in the medium term. Iceland does not know where it falls in this distribution.

Beyond the international surveys, Iceland faces a domestic measurement gap. The *Rikisendurskoðun* (National Audit Office) audit of labor market statistics (*Atvinnuleysi og vinnumarkaðsúrræði*, December 2024) found a 17% discrepancy between Iceland's two official unemployment measures: Hagstofa Íslands (Statistics Iceland, using ILO-standard Labor Force Survey methodology) reported 5.2% unemployment in September 2024 while Vinnumálastofnun (the Employment Agency, using administrative registration) reported 3.3% for the same month. This 1.9 percentage-point gap represents approximately 34,000 people in disagreement about their labor force status — a discrepancy that grew from 6% in 2019 to 17% in 2024. The Rikisendurskoðun concluded this produced misleading public information.

**The Atvinnustefna's use of this data:** The strategy cites projected AI impacts on Iceland's workforce. The government's own AI Action Plan (published November 2024, revised July 2025) claims "about 55% of jobs in Iceland, or about 130,000 people, are considered likely to be greatly affected by artificial intelligence." This figure is not derived from Icelandic measurement. It applies international task-exposure frameworks — methodologies developed by the OECD, McKinsey Global Institute, or similar — to Iceland's occupational distribution, without Iceland-specific validation.

The government was aware of this gap. Action B.1 of the AI Action Plan commissioned a "report on the economic opportunities of AI in Iceland" from the Ministry and Statistics Iceland — meaning the baseline study was an *output* of the action plan, not an *input* to it. The government constructed AI policy on borrowed statistics while simultaneously commissioning the study that would have generated domestic statistics.

**The Vaxtarplan 2035 neither acknowledges nor addresses this measurement vacuum.** The strategy does not contain a single action to bring Iceland into the Eurostat enterprise AI survey. It does not propose an Icelandic equivalent of the EWCS. It does not address the Rikisendurskoðun's finding about the 17% measurement gap. It builds its productivity projections on international estimates that have never been validated for the Icelandic context.

One figure crystallizes the scope of the problem: according to a survey referenced in the AI Action Plan's own economic analysis (Action B.1), **only 8% of Icelandic workers feel adequately trained to use AI tools**. The government simultaneously projects that 130,000 workers will be "greatly affected" by AI and its own survey finds that 92% of workers do not feel ready for it. The AI Action Plan allocates ISK 1.1 billion to AI infrastructure and ISK 2.3 billion to education — while the readiness gap it implicitly acknowledges remains unmeasured at the scale needed to close it. The 8% figure also places Iceland's self-assessed readiness substantially below the Varda survey's more optimistic (but still alarming) finding that 45% of affected workers feel undertrained. Whether the actual figure is closer to 8% or 55% untrained is unknown — because the surveys ask different questions and neither was designed as a systematic workforce readiness audit.

### 3.2 Omission Two: The ICT Trifecta

Between 2022 and 2025, Iceland's ICT sector (NACE J) showed three signals moving simultaneously in a specific and diagnostically important pattern:

| Signal | Change | Hagstofa Table | Value |
|--------|--------|----------------|-------|
| Employment | -1,300 jobs (-11.4%) (2022–2025) | VIN01110 | 11,400 → 10,100 |
| Revenue (J62 Computer Services) | +74.5% (2019–2024) | FYR04101 | 114.7bn → 200.1bn ISK |
| Vacancy rate | -94% (5.4% → 0.3%) (2022–2025) | JVS00001 | 5.4% → 0.3% |

*Verification note: The revenue figure was independently verified by Manus 1.6 (April 2026) against the raw PxWeb API. The 2019→2024 window shows +74.5% (ISK 114.7bn → ISK 200.1bn). A 2022→2024 window shows only +28.1% (full-year 2025 data was not yet available in the API at verification time). The 2019 baseline was selected because 2019 is the pre-COVID, pre-distortion reference year for this sector.*

Revenue growing by ~75% over the pre-COVID baseline. Employment falling by 11.4%. Vacancy rate collapsing by 94%.

The vacancy rate collapse is the decisive signal. If firms were shedding workers due to a cyclical downturn, revenue would also fall. If workers were being offshored, revenue might shift. If the sector faced a skills mismatch or demographic shortfall, vacancies would remain elevated or rise — firms would be advertising positions they could not fill. None of these alternative explanations fit the data.

The pattern — revenue up, employment down, vacancies near zero — is consistent with productivity displacement: firms are producing more output per worker and are not replacing the workers they shed. They are not seeking new hires because they do not need them.

Labour productivity data (Hagstofa THJ11011, updated March 2026) confirms: the ICT sector showed a 10.5% productivity surge in 2025, the highest of any sector in the Icelandic economy. The sector simultaneously shed 1,300 jobs and increased its productivity at the highest rate in the economy. At the enterprise level, the ICT sector went from 972 to 1,119 firms between 2022 and 2025 while total employment fell — more companies, fewer workers, more output.

The ICT trifecta did not appear in any Hagstofa publication as an assembled finding. It required manually connecting three separate data tables. No Icelandic institution was monitoring for this combination of signals.

The enterprise formation data adds a further dimension of analytical significance. The ICT sector grew from 972 to 1,119 registered firms (+15.1%) in the same period that total sector employment fell by 11.4%. More companies, fewer workers, higher revenue, near-zero vacancies. This pattern — firm formation accelerating while aggregate employment contracts — is what AI-enabled productivity looks like at sector scale. Firms are being created to exploit AI-productivity advantages with small headcounts. The sector is healthier by every financial and formation metric while being smaller by every employment metric. The number that matters for Iceland's labor force — jobs — is moving in the opposite direction from the numbers that matter for the strategy document — revenue and firm count.

This creates a conceptual trap for policymakers: the ICT sector's success story is exactly what the Vaxtarplan intends to replicate across the economy. If it is replicated, and if the trifecta pattern holds at scale, Iceland will have successfully built a high-revenue, high-productivity, low-employment knowledge economy — which is precisely what the Stöðugreining identifies as the problem with the current model, not the solution to it.

**The Vaxtarplan 2035 does not mention the ICT employment decline.** The strategy's treatment of the ICT sector is entirely positive: it frames AI and technology as drivers of productivity gains and export diversification. Action 3.3 allocates ISK 1.1 billion to AI infrastructure. The strategy calls for stronger tech sector development without examining what happened in Iceland's existing tech sector over the preceding three years.

This is not a minor omission. If the technology sector — the most AI-exposed, most productive part of Iceland's knowledge economy — is already showing a productivity-driven employment decline, the strategy's prescription (more AI investment, more tech infrastructure) will accelerate that trend without any mechanism to address its labor market consequences.

### 3.3 Omission Three: The Occupation Polarization

Between 2024 and 2025, Iceland's labor market underwent a structural shift in occupation composition that has received no public analysis:

**Knowledge-intensive occupations (ISCO groups 1-4), change 2024→2025:**

| Occupation Group | 2024 | 2025 | Change |
|-----------------|------|------|--------|
| Legislators and managers | 30,000 | 28,000 | -2,000 (-6.7%) |
| Professionals | 58,700 | 54,200 | -4,500 (-7.7%) |
| Associate professionals | 36,000 | 33,800 | -2,200 (-6.1%) |
| Clerical support workers | 9,600 | 9,000 | -600 (-6.3%) |
| **Total knowledge-intensive** | **134,300** | **125,000** | **-9,300 (-6.9%)** |

**Non-knowledge occupations (ISCO groups 5-9), change 2024→2025:**

| Occupation Group | 2024 | 2025 | Change |
|-----------------|------|------|--------|
| Service and sales workers | 44,500 | 50,000 | +5,500 (+12.4%) |
| Craft and related trades | 20,900 | 21,300 | +400 (+1.9%) |
| Plant and machine operators | 9,400 | 9,800 | +400 (+4.3%) |
| Elementary occupations | 12,200 | 12,600 | +400 (+3.3%) |
| **Total non-knowledge** | **93,000** | **99,900** | **+6,900 (+7.4%)** |

*Source: Hagstofa Íslands VIN01100*

The economy did not contract: total employment went from 227,300 to 224,800, a -1.1% change. But within that near-flat aggregate, 9,300 knowledge-intensive jobs disappeared while 6,900 physical and service jobs were added. Construction added 3,300 jobs (+21.9% from 2023 to 2025). Tourism added 2,200 (+17.6%). Service and sales grew by 5,500 within a single year.

This is the occupational polarization pattern that automation economics literature has documented in larger economies: the middle hollows while the bottom and top diverge. The top represents high-skill, high-autonomy knowledge work that AI augments. The bottom represents physical, in-person service work that AI cannot yet automate. The middle — routine knowledge work, clerical functions, associate-level professional tasks — faces the most direct substitution threat.

The fact that the -9,300 figure coincided with the ICT trifecta's productivity surge is suggestive but not conclusive. The Panopticon investigation (the author's accompanying research project) identified this as a mixed falsification result: the knowledge-occupation contraction is consistent with AI displacement but also consistent with fiscal tightening, LFS sampling variance, economic sectoral rebalancing, and other factors. The honest position is that the signal exists and no institution has investigated its causes.

**The Q4 2025 seasonal anomaly deepens the concern.** Icelandic unemployment follows a consistent seasonal pattern: elevated in Q1 (winter construction shutdown, tourism off-season), recovering through Q2-Q3, trough in Q3. The Panopticon investigation's seasonality falsification test (FA-2) found that Q4 2025 broke this pattern: unemployment returned to 5.0% — identical to Q1 2025 levels — in a quarter that historically should have been recovering. In every year from 2010 to 2024 except during COVID, Q4 unemployment was substantially below Q1. The Q4 2025 figure of 5.0% has no post-financial-crisis precedent outside pandemic conditions.

Furthermore, the floor of Icelandic unemployment — even in peak-season quarters — is now running 1.5–2.0 percentage points above the 2022–2024 baseline. VMST's own 2025 forecast predicted a maximum of 4.2% for late 2025. The actual Q4 outcome exceeded that forecast by 0.8 percentage points. January 2026 came in at 4.9%, continuing the trend rather than reversing it. When the official forecasting agency is consistently below the outcome, the structural interpretation is more likely than the cyclical one.

None of this analysis has been published by any Icelandic institution. It required manually assembling quarterly unemployment tables, applying seasonal decomposition, and comparing against VMST's published forecast. The Vaxtarplan 2035 was drafted and published while this pattern was forming — without the monitoring infrastructure that would have surfaced it.

**The Vaxtarplan 2035's response:** The strategy prescribes more STEM education, more knowledge-sector investment, and more AI adoption — exactly the interventions appropriate for an economy where knowledge work is growing and needs skilled workers to fill it. The prescriptions are inverted relative to the current signal: knowledge work is contracting, and the strategy is doubling down on supply-side preparation for it.

### 3.4 Omission Four: The Institutional Silence of Labor

When Iceland's government published its AI Action Plan for consultation in November 2024 — a document explicitly projecting that 130,000 Icelandic workers will be "greatly affected" by AI — it received 12 responses over a 14-day window.

Zero came from Iceland's major labor unions.

Not from ASÍ (Alþýðusamband Íslands), Iceland's national confederation of labor, representing most organized workers in the country. Not from VR (Verslunarmannafélag Reykjavíkur), the commercial workers' union with 30,000+ members. Not from Efling, the general workers' union also with 30,000+ members. Not from BSRB (Bandalag starfsmanna ríkis og bæja), the public sector union. Not from SFR (Starfsmannafélag ríkisins). Not from Kennarasamband Íslands (the Teachers' Union).

One union did respond: BHM (Bandalag háskólamanna), the federation representing university-educated professionals. Its two-page submission welcomed the plan, called for stakeholder consultation mechanisms, mentioned upskilling, and noted that "all public systems of the welfare society" should consider AI's impacts. It offered no data, no sector analysis, no specific demands, and no mention of the workers most at risk — those in lower-skill clerical and manual roles who typically are not BHM members.

The consultation's submitter composition reveals the power structure of Iceland's AI discourse:

| Category | Submissions | Share |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Universities and research institutes | 5 | 42% |
| Government institutions | 3 | 25% |
| Employers' federation | 1 | 8% |
| Labor unions | 1 | 8% |
| Civil society organizations | 1 | 8% |
| Individuals | 1 | 8% |

Those who will benefit most from AI deployment (technology sector, employers, universities) represented over 50% of submissions. Those whose members face the greatest risk (service workers, clerical workers, lower-skill labor) had no representation.

Who exactly are those at highest risk? The Rikisendurskoðun data provides a precise answer. Foreign nationals constitute approximately 55% of all registered unemployed in Iceland, at an unemployment rate of **8.9%** — compared to **2.3%** for Icelandic nationals. This 3.9x differential means that Iceland's most labor-market-vulnerable population is overwhelmingly the immigrant workforce that has driven two-thirds of total population growth since 2010. These workers are concentrated in construction, food processing, tourism services, and logistics — the sectors most exposed to automation in the current AI deployment wave. They are not organized in Iceland's unions at rates comparable to domestic workers. They were not represented in the AI consultation. The strategy's "augmentation, not replacement" principle, and its STEM education investments, are calibrated to the Icelandic knowledge worker — not to the foreign national warehouse operative or fish processor whose displacement risk is the most immediate and the most unmeasured.

The ASÍ's 46th Congress (October 2024) produced zero resolutions on AI. VR's annual workplace survey assesses nine criteria for worker welfare — none address AI or automation. No Icelandic union has published an AI framework, an AI position paper, or secured an AI provision in a collective agreement.

**The Nordic contrast is devastating.**

| Country | Labor AI Framework | Source |
|---------|-------------------|--------|
| Norway | LO-NHO Basic Agreement includes AI provisions: worker privacy, bias prevention, representative involvement in AI decision-making | EPRS PE 774.670 |
| Denmark | Hilfr2 agreement: one of the first collective agreements in Europe to explicitly address AI and algorithmic management | [Eurofound](https://apps.eurofound.europa.eu/platformeconomydb/collective-agreement-on-use-of-ai-and-algorithms-signed-110187) |
| Finland | Technology sector agreements include AI clauses | Published record |
| Sweden | Teknikföretagen + IF Metall joint AI framework | Published record |
| **Iceland** | **Nothing** | This investigation |

Norway — not an EU member, structurally comparable to Iceland — addressed algorithmic management in the workplace through collective bargaining while Iceland produced nothing. The LO-NHO Basic Agreement requires worker notification when AI systems are deployed, establishes worker representative involvement in AI decision-making, and includes bias prevention mechanisms. Iceland's equivalent bodies (ASÍ and SA) have no agreement.

**The Vaxtarplan 2035 does not address this institutional gap.** The strategy proposes no tripartite AI governance mechanism. It contains no provision for union involvement in AI deployment decisions. It proposes no binding labor protections for technology-induced displacement. All labor market measures are framed as education and upskilling (Priority 2), not as governance or worker rights.

The Varda 2026 survey (n=25,000, the largest ever Icelandic worker survey, published February 2026) provides the first ground-truth measurement of worker experience: 23% of Icelandic workers report their jobs have changed or will change due to technology. Of those affected workers, 36% fear job loss. 44% report increased work pressure after technology changes (56% among immigrant workers). 45% feel they have received inadequate training for the technology being deployed. The workers know. The institutions are silent.

The institutional silence is now regionally conspicuous. The Nordic AI Union Summit — held in Oslo on March 19, 2026, just three weeks before the publication of this strategy — brought together labor unions from all five Nordic countries specifically to "strengthen collective voice and secure real influence over how AI is introduced, governed, and negotiated at work." Iceland's unions were present as observers. They observed what their Norwegian, Danish, Swedish, and Finnish counterparts have built: collective agreement frameworks, notification rights, AI clauses. They returned to Iceland with no equivalent domestic achievement and to a government strategy that did not mention the summit had occurred.

### 3.5 Omission Five: The Discourse-Reality Gap

Iceland's AI discourse is dominated by approximately 15+ commentators who claim AI expertise without building anything. The country has approximately 35-40 people who can actually build AI systems — and none of them are in the policy discourse.

The Panopticon investigation mapped Iceland's AI ecosystem into a taxonomy:

**Genuine builders (~3-4 companies):**
- Mideind: Greynir NLP parser, Yfirlestur proofreader, Embla voice assistant, IceBERT language model family, 55 GitHub repositories, 15 HuggingFace models, trained GPT-4 on Icelandic via a 40-person RLHF team. The most technically capable private AI company in Iceland.
- Lucinity: Anti-money-laundering AI platform with proprietary models, real training data, and international clients. VC-funded.
- Controlant: Real-time cold-chain monitoring with predictive analytics (acquired by Carrier Global).
- DataLab Iceland: "Ari" generative AI assistant trained on Icelandic regulatory data; custom predictive models; clients include the Union of Icelandic Municipalities, Arion Bank, and Revenue Service.

**Deployers (running others' AI in production, ~6 organizations):**
- All three major banks (Landsbankinn, Íslandsbanki, Arion Bank) run the same Norwegian vendor's (boost.ai) customer service chatbot. None have built proprietary AI.
- Reykjavík City's chatbot runs on OpenAI.
- The Prime Minister's Office used OpenAI to categorize 10,000 public submissions (the "Verum hagsýn" initiative, January 2025) — a one-off deployment that directly contradicts the AI Action Plan's stated principle of "augmentation, not replacement."

**Resellers (~5 companies):**
- Advania, Origo, Nyherji: package Microsoft and IBM AI tools as their own services. Advania deployed Microsoft Copilot to all 1,000+ of its employees. Advania CEO Hildur Einarsdóttir stated publicly (MBL, August 30, 2025): "AI will take over manual tasks" and called for open discussion about which jobs it "fully replaces." This is the most candid executive statement on displacement made publicly by any Icelandic business leader.

**Commentators (~15+ individuals):**
Policy officials, political champions, journalists, and conference speakers who discuss AI without building or deploying it. The political champion for Iceland's AI strategy is a lawyer. The government's AI claims cite international estimates it has not validated. Iceland ranked 45th globally in AI development capacity (Krias/Innovation Fund, November 2025) — other Nordic countries are in the top 10.

**The academic situation:**
Iceland's AI research capability is concentrated at two institutions — Reykjavik University (RU) and the University of Iceland (HI). All eight Rannis AI grants awarded in 2024-2025 went to RU or HI. UNAK (University of Akureyri), Bifrost, Hólar, LHÍ, and LBHÍ have zero confirmed AI research output.

The total number of AI-active researchers across all Icelandic universities is approximately 40-50. The critical gap for the Vaxtarplan: **no faculty member at any Icelandic university is researching AI's macroeconomic labor market effects in quantitative terms.** The closest work is the BIAS project (University of Iceland, EU Horizon Europe) on AI bias in recruitment. Several MSc theses have addressed the topic qualitatively. No economist is studying AI and employment in Iceland.

The Halldórsson 2025 thesis (*AI and the Labor Market: Changes in Education and Skills Requirements*) — the first Icelandic academic work to directly engage the displacement question — concludes that AI is already transforming skill requirements in Icelandic organizations, that new jobs are emerging while others disappear, and that AI is widening inequality between those who can access retraining and those who cannot. It was submitted as a BS thesis, not a peer-reviewed study.

**The Vaxtarplan's AI strategy (Priority 3, Action 3.3)** allocates ISK 1.1 billion to "AI infrastructure." The strategy does not specify what research capacity will be built with this investment, whether it will include AI-labor economics research, or how Iceland will develop indigenous capacity to understand what its own technology sector is doing to its own workforce.

### 3.6 Omission Six: The Welfare Black Box

Iceland's disability and rehabilitation system has expanded significantly over the past 25 years:

| Metric | 2000 | 2024 | Change |
|--------|------|------|--------|
| Share of labor market on disability/rehabilitation | 6.3% | 9.4% | +49% |
| Disability pension recipients | — | 20,767 | — |
| Rehabilitation pension recipients | — | 6,883 | — |
| Combined recipients | — | 27,650 | — |
| Psychiatric diagnosis share (~2/3) | — | ~67% | — |

*Sources: Ingimarsson, Laeknabladid 2026; TR Annual Report; Lifdu nuna*

The growth driver is psychiatric and stress-adjacent diagnoses:

| Diagnosis Category | 2000-2024 Growth |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Anxiety, depression, burnout, stress, ADHD, post-viral fatigue | +179% |
| All other diagnoses | +26% |

Women aged 18-49 show the steepest acceleration: +79% prevalence increase over 24 years. The rehabilitation pension expenditure — reflecting new system entrants rather than stock — grew 40% in a single year (2023 to 2024). Rehabilitation cost growth of 40% in one year is not a marginal change; it is a system under pressure.

Iceland leads the world in antidepressant consumption: 157 defined daily doses (DDD) per 1,000 inhabitants, #1 among 26 OECD countries (NHWStat/OECD data). Women consume approximately twice the rate of men (210.50 vs 115.55 DDD/1,000 in 2023). The age peak is 25-44: 183.13 DDD/1,000 — the prime working-age cohort.

The Panopticon investigation's falsification tests found this data consistent with AI displacement (psychiatric dominance, younger cohort acceleration, burnout and anxiety as the fastest-growing cluster) but equally consistent with a 35-year secular diagnostic trend that predates AI adoption, post-pandemic mental health effects, neurodevelopmental diagnosis expansion, and reduced stigma lowering the diagnostic threshold.

The question of whether displaced workers from knowledge occupations are entering the welfare system through psychiatric disability pathways cannot be answered. **The welfare system does not publish prior-occupation data for claimants.** Tryggingastofnun (TR), the Social Insurance Administration, does not cross-tabulate disability diagnoses with prior employment sector or occupation. This data gap is not accidental — it reflects a system architecture that was never designed to monitor occupational displacement through welfare pathways.

The Nordic comparison is instructive:

| Country | Administrative Data Infrastructure |
|---------|-------------------------------------|
| Denmark | DREAM+IDA: linked employer-employee register, welfare register, education records. Enables individual-level longitudinal tracking across employment, unemployment, and welfare |
| Sweden | LISA (Longitudinal Integration Database for Health Insurance and Labour Market Studies): comprehensive linked administrative data |
| Norway | FD-Trygd: linked welfare, employment, and health records |
| Finland | Findata: cross-register analysis infrastructure |
| **Iceland** | **No equivalent. No linked register. No occupation-level welfare data.** |

Denmark's linked administrative data infrastructure enabled the Humlum and Vestergaard study (NBER Working Paper 33777, 2025) — the most rigorous available study of LLM adoption's labor market effects — which found null effects on earnings and hours in a difference-in-differences design using 500,000+ workers. Iceland cannot run an equivalent study because the data does not exist to run it.

**The Vaxtarplan 2035 contains no action to create this data infrastructure.** The strategy's welfare mentions are confined to general statements about supporting re-entry into the labor market. Action A.4 of the AI Action Plan (2024-2025) mandates an "impact analysis of AI on different social groups" including the elderly, disabled, immigrants, and low-income workers — but this analysis would measure group vulnerability to AI, not trace the pathway from displacement to welfare entry.

The September 2025 reform of Iceland's disability and rehabilitation system (11 simultaneous changes including new pension categories, assessment methodology changes, and benefit restructuring) creates an additional measurement discontinuity: pre- and post-September 2025 welfare data are not directly comparable. The reform — however well-intentioned — eliminates several years of data series comparability exactly when longitudinal tracking matters most.

The ISK 1.5 billion in education commitments (teacher training + university STEM + curriculum + vocational + flexible learning) represents a genuine if modest investment in workforce development.

### 4.2 What the Strategy Does Not Fund

The strategy does not allocate resources to:

- Domestic AI adoption measurement (Iceland's inclusion in Eurostat survey or domestic equivalent)
- Worker conditions monitoring (Iceland's participation in EWCS or domestic equivalent)
- Occupation-level welfare data infrastructure
- Transition support funds for displaced workers (beyond general upskilling)
- Independent monitoring of AI's labor market effects
- Any enforcement mechanism for the "augmentation, not replacement" language in the AI Action Plan

The absence of transition support is particularly notable. The AI Action Plan's labor market measures are framed entirely as education and upskilling: workers should prepare for AI-transformed jobs. The plan contains no binding wage protection for workers displaced by AI. It contains no retraining entitlement — a right to funded training if one's job is eliminated by automation. It contains no transition fund analogous to the EU's Just Transition Fund (designed for workers displaced by decarbonization). The framing assumes the market will absorb displaced workers if they are adequately skilled.

This is an empirically contested assumption. The Stöðugreining's own data shows that only 1% of net new jobs created in Iceland's recent growth phase were in high-productivity sectors. If AI-displaced knowledge workers cannot find equivalent knowledge-sector employment, they face downward occupational mobility into lower-productivity service and physical work — exactly the pattern visible in the 2024-2025 occupation data.

The cost comparison is stark. The strategy invests ISK 1.1 billion in AI infrastructure — the technology that may produce the displacement the strategy does not plan for. A nationally representative Icelandic equivalent of the EWCS — the survey that would actually measure what is happening to workers — is estimated to cost ISK 8–12 million. This is 0.7–1.1% of the AI infrastructure investment. Iceland is spending 90–130 times more on accelerating AI adoption than the cost of building the instrument that would detect what that adoption is doing to workers. The strategy does not fund the ISK 8–12 million instrument. The ISK 1.1 billion investment proceeds without it.

### 4.3 The Self-Contradiction at the Core

The government's AI Action Plan (predecessor to the Vaxtarplan 2035) contains a revealing admission buried in parliamentary documentation. Althingi document 2212/154 records the Infrastructure Minister acknowledging: *"Í ráðuneytinu hefur ekki farið fram formleg vinna við að greina með hvaða hætti hægt væri að hagnýta gervigreind í lögbundnum verkefnum ráðherra og stofnana á hans ábyrgð."* — "The ministry has not conducted formal work to analyze how artificial intelligence could be utilized in the statutory tasks of the minister and his agencies."

*Precision note (April 2026 independent verification): The minister's statement concerns AI utilization within one ministry's own statutory operations — not a government-wide admission about AI's labor market impact on workers. The statement is nonetheless significant: it reveals that even the ministry leading digital transformation had not formally analysed how to deploy AI in its own work, reinforcing the broader pattern of borrowed statistics rather than domestic analysis. The "130,000 workers affected" projection rests on international estimates never validated against Icelandic conditions — a separate finding documented in Section 3.1.*

This is the Collingridge dilemma in institutional form: the government must govern AI before it can fully understand AI's effects, but the governance choices made now will shape which effects become visible later. The response to this dilemma, as the Nordic countries demonstrate, is to build the measurement infrastructure that enables learning — not to govern on borrowed statistics and hope the international estimates are close enough.

### 4.4 The International Displacement Ledger: What the Strategy Does Not See

The Vaxtarplan 2035 was drafted against a backdrop of accelerating global evidence on AI-driven employment change. That evidence is not speculative or futuristic — it is Q1 2026 operational data from companies that are Iceland's peers, counterparts, and in some cases direct models for the strategy's prescriptions. The strategy cites none of it.

**The Q1 2026 Global Layoff Signal**

In the first quarter of 2026 — the quarter in which the Vaxtarplan was published — 78,557 technology-sector workers were laid off globally. Of those, 47.9% (37,638 workers) were in positions where companies explicitly attributed the layoff to AI adoption or automation. The average daily job loss running through 2026 is 704 workers per day in the technology sector alone.

The range between 20.4% and 47.9% AI attribution reflects genuine methodological complexity. Companies have incentives to attribute workforce reductions to AI (signaling technological sophistication to investors) and also incentives not to (creating legal exposure under emerging AI Act regulations). March 2026 alone saw 18,720 tech job cuts — a 40% year-over-year increase versus March 2025.

Goldman Sachs (April 2026) provided a separate, methodology-independent estimate: AI is eliminating 16,000 U.S. jobs per month in net terms (25,000 displaced per month less 9,000 augmented per month). Goldman's distributional finding is directly relevant to Iceland's high-equality social contract: entry-level, white-collar, administrative roles are disproportionately exposed, and displaced workers face longer job searches and pay cuts.

- This represents a **9x increase** versus the 2025 pace of 55,000 AI-attributed cuts
- **80% of firms report no productivity gains from AI** despite significant investment — the "productivity paradox"

The productivity paradox finding matters critically for the Vaxtarplan's central bet. The strategy allocates 1.1 billion ISK to AI infrastructure as Iceland's productivity catch-up mechanism. In the same quarter the strategy was published, 80% of firms that have already invested in AI report no productivity gains. Iceland is being asked to make this the cornerstone of its strategy in the quarter that deploying firms most strongly suggest the gains have not yet materialized at scale.

**The Klarna Case: The Nordic Fintech Template**

The Klarna case is the most instructive single data point for Iceland's strategy because it involves a Nordic-adjacent fintech operating in Iceland's own strategic domain (financial technology), and because the numbers are verified and remarkable. In 2022, Klarna had approximately 7,000 employees. By 2025, headcount had been reduced to approximately 3,500 — a 50% reduction over three years. The company's AI assistant took on work equivalent to approximately 700 full-time customer service agents, handling 2.3 million customer conversations in a single month at launch. In Q3 2025, Klarna reported revenue of $903 million — double its pre-reduction revenue.

The Klarna equation: headcount -50%, revenue +100%. This is the productive transformation the Stöðugreining diagnoses Iceland needs at the macroeconomic level — expressed at the firm level. The scenario the strategy treats as upside (AI driving productivity) and the scenario the strategy does not treat at all (AI driving employment reduction) are the same phenomenon from different vantage points. Where AI productivity gains occur, they produce output-per-worker increases. In competitive markets, sustained output-per-worker increases are accompanied by workforce reduction unless output grows proportionally faster. Iceland's strategy assumes the productivity upside while leaving the employment downside without governance.

**Statistics Iceland's First AI Survey: The Domestic Baseline**

In 2025, Statistics Iceland conducted its first-ever national enterprise ICT survey including AI questions — 848 enterprises sampled, 62% response rate, yielding Iceland's first domestic data on AI adoption attitudes:

- **31% of Icelandic enterprises report concerns about AI use**
- Top concern: **lack of appropriate expertise** (not cost, not regulatory risk)
- AI usage highest in: information and communication; professional, scientific and technical activities; transportation and storage
- **Strategic AI implementation remains less common than tactical tool-level adoption**
- Smaller enterprises report significantly more concern than larger ones

*Source: Statistics Iceland ICT Survey 2025 (statice.is/publications)*

The finding that the primary enterprise concern is lack of expertise confirms the STEM gap the Vaxtarplan is attempting to address — but with a critical timing asymmetry. Enterprises with AI anxiety today need expertise now. The strategy's STEM pipeline produces graduates entering the workforce in 2028-2031. The enterprises navigating AI deployment in 2026-2027 do so without the talent the strategy is producing. Further, the first-ever Icelandic enterprise AI survey captures adoption concerns without asking whether firms have reduced or plan to reduce headcount as a result of AI adoption. The measurement gap persists even when measurement begins.

**The Academic Evidence Balance Sheet**

The academic evidence is genuinely contested. The optimistic scenario has empirical support that requires careful qualification.

*Humlum and Vestergaard (NBER WP 33777, 2025)* used Danish administrative data for 25,000 workers across 7,000 workplaces in 11 occupations and found near-zero effects on earnings and hours, with confidence intervals ruling out effects larger than 1%. Three caveats apply: (1) The study covers 2023-2024, early adoption before enterprise-scale deployment — a correct measurement of the early period, not a forecast foreclosing later displacement; (2) The average productivity time saving was 3% — compounded across the economy, sustained 3% efficiency gains produce structural demand-side shifts even where immediate earnings measures show no change; (3) The study documents task restructuring without earnings changes, consistent with structural underemployment where workers retain nominal positions with degraded skill use and career trajectory.

*Acemoglu (MIT, 2024; 2024 Nobel Prize in Economics co-laureate)* calculates AI's 10-year TFP impact at 0.53%-0.66% using a task-based model — far below optimistic projections. His primary concern is distribution: AI may widen wealth inequality even under full employment because gains accrue to capital and non-automatable roles rather than distributing across the workforce. In February 2026, Acemoglu warned that "U.S. democracy won't survive the AI job-pocalypse" without policy intervention — a political economy escalation that Iceland's social-contract-based model should take seriously.

*McKinsey Global Institute* estimates 75-375 million workers globally displaced by AI and automation by 2030 — approximately 14% of the global workforce. *Goldman Sachs Research* estimates up to 300 million full-time jobs globally affected. Wide confidence intervals reflect genuine uncertainty, not evidence that the lower bound is more probable.

What the evidence supports with higher confidence: the direction is negative for lower-skill white-collar employment; distribution is regressive; pace is accelerating in 2025-2026 relative to 2023-2024; and the policy window for managing transition is narrowing as deployment outpaces governance.

*Sources: Humlum & Vestergaard, NBER WP 33777; Acemoglu MIT 2024; Fortune February and April 2026; Goldman Sachs Research 2026; McKinsey Global Institute; Duke/Fed CFO Survey March 2026; Tom's Hardware April 2026*

## Part IV-B: The EU AI Act Countdown

### The Four-Month Clock

On August 2, 2026 — approximately four months from the publication of this report — the European Union AI Act's high-risk AI system requirements will take effect across EU member states. Under those requirements, AI systems used in employment contexts are explicitly classified as high-risk under Annex III of the regulation. Any employer using AI for recruiting, candidate screening, performance evaluation, work monitoring, or termination decisions will face binding obligations including: mandatory worker notification before deployment, designated human oversight with competence and authority to override AI outputs, continuous non-discrimination monitoring, decision logging requirements, and, where applicable under national law, workers' representative consultation.

Iceland is an EEA member. The EU AI Act carries the formal designation "EEA relevant," triggering mandatory review for incorporation into the EEA Agreement. Iceland, Norway, and Liechtenstein are currently participating as observers in EU AI Board meetings. The formal incorporation has not been completed as of April 2026.

The Vaxtarplan 2035 does not mention the EU AI Act. The Aðgerðaáætlun 2026-2027 does not mention the EU AI Act. The Stöðugreining does not mention the EU AI Act.

This is not a minor omission in a peripheral technical document. Iceland is four months from a hard legal deadline affecting every employer who uses AI in human resources decisions — a category that, given the pace of AI adoption in enterprise HR, banking, retail, and public services, encompasses hundreds of Icelandic employers — and the government's flagship employment strategy has not acknowledged that the deadline exists.

### The Implementation Timeline in Detail

The EU AI Act's provisions have been applying in stages since the regulation entered into force in August 2024:

| Date | Requirement | Status as of April 2026 |
|------|-------------|------------------------|
| August 1, 2024 | AI Act enters into force | Complete |
| February 2, 2025 | Prohibited AI practices banned; AI literacy obligations active | In effect — emotion recognition in workplaces banned |
| August 2, 2025 | General-purpose AI rules apply; Member States designate competent authorities; national governance structures required | In effect — Iceland has no designated competent authority |
| **August 2, 2026** | **High-risk AI system requirements — including all employment AI** | **4 months away — no Icelandic framework exists** |
| August 2, 2027 | Full application to all AI systems | 16 months away |

*Source: EU AI Act Official Journal; artificialintelligenceact.eu implementation timeline*

Since February 2, 2025, Icelandic employers have already been prohibited from using emotion recognition AI systems in the workplace and from biometric categorization of workers based on protected characteristics. No public guidance has been issued to Icelandic employers on these existing obligations. No competent authority has been designated to enforce them.

As of August 2, 2025, eight months ago, all EU Member States were required to have designated the national authorities responsible for supervising AI systems. Iceland has not completed EEA incorporation of the AI Act and therefore has no such authority. This means that the prohibited practices in effect since February 2025 are not being actively enforced in Iceland — because there is no enforcement body.

### What High-Risk Employment AI Means

The AI Act's Annex III classification of employment AI as high-risk is not a catch-all provision. It targets specific and increasingly common uses:

- **Recruitment and candidate screening**: Any AI system used to screen, sort, or rank job applicants — including CV parsing tools, automated pre-screening questionnaires, and AI-assisted interview analysis
- **Performance evaluation**: Any AI system used to assess, rank, or evaluate the work performance of employees
- **Work monitoring and management**: AI systems that monitor employee behavior, attendance, productivity, or communication patterns
- **Promotion and demotion decisions**: AI influencing internal mobility and career progression
- **Termination decisions**: AI used to inform or automate decisions to end employment

These are not hypothetical applications. All three of Iceland's major banks have deployed or are deploying AI in customer service and operational monitoring contexts. The Prime Minister's Office used OpenAI to categorize 10,000 public submissions (the Verum hagsýn initiative, January 2025). Advania has deployed Microsoft Copilot to all 1,000+ of its employees, including in roles that involve performance monitoring. DataLab Iceland's "Ari" generative AI assistant is deployed by clients including the Union of Icelandic Municipalities and Revenue Service. The boost.ai chatbot deployed by all three major banks for customer service affects worker task allocation in those environments.

Under the high-risk AI provisions taking effect in August 2026, all of these deployments that influence employment decisions will be subject to obligations Iceland has no domestic framework to implement, monitor, or enforce.

### The Obligations in Detail

For employment AI systems classified as high-risk, the August 2026 obligations include:

**1. Risk management system (ongoing):** A documented risk management process for the AI system's entire lifecycle, identifying risks to workers' fundamental rights and establishing risk mitigation measures.

**2. Data governance:** Training and testing datasets must be assessed for quality, potential biases, and gaps. This applies to AI systems trained on historical employment data, which often reflects historical discrimination.

**3. Technical documentation:** Comprehensive technical documentation demonstrating compliance must be maintained and made available to competent authorities. For Iceland, no authority currently exists to receive this documentation.

**4. Record-keeping:** AI systems must automatically record their operations and decisions ("logging") to enable post-hoc audit.

**5. Transparency toward users:** Employers deploying AI in employment decisions must provide workers with information about the AI system, how it works, and how it affects decisions about them.

**6. Human oversight:** Designated individuals must have the competence, authority, and technical means to override AI outputs. The obligation cannot be satisfied by nominating a manager who has no technical capacity to evaluate the AI.

**7. Accuracy, robustness, cybersecurity:** Performance metrics, including for bias and discriminatory outputs, must be documented.

**8. Conformity assessment:** Before deployment, high-risk AI systems must undergo a conformity assessment — either internal or through a notified body — and receive CE marking.

*Source: EU AI Act, Articles 8-15, Annex III; Crowell & Moring AI and Human Resources 2026 Legal Overview*

### Iceland's Gap: No Framework, No Authority, No Guidance

Complying with these obligations requires:

1. A designated national competent authority empowered to supervise AI systems and receive conformity documentation
2. National provisions implementing the AI Act's requirements that map to Icelandic employment law
3. Employer guidance translating the legal obligations into operational requirements
4. Union involvement mechanisms where the Act requires workers' representative consultation
5. Enforcement powers — including the ability to impose the regulation's fines (up to EUR 15 million or 3% of annual global turnover for violations)

Iceland has none of these. The EEA incorporation has not been completed. No ministry has been assigned lead responsibility for AI Act implementation. The Vaxtarplan 2035's Action G.3 (in Part X of this report's recommendations) calls for proactive implementation — but the strategy itself does not contain this action. It is a recommendation from this analysis, not a government commitment.

The situation is structurally comparable to Iceland's position relative to GDPR before EEA incorporation of that regulation — except that AI Act obligations are more complex, require more technical institutional capacity, and have employment-specific provisions that directly interact with collective bargaining and industrial relations frameworks that Iceland's institutions have not prepared for.

### The Productivity Paradox and the Compliance Paradox

The Duke CFO Survey (March 2026) found that 80% of firms report no productivity gains from AI despite significant investment. This "productivity paradox" creates a specific compliance risk for Iceland: employers are deploying AI systems in employment contexts without measurable benefit — but they will be legally liable for the harms those systems create without the institutional framework to demonstrate compliance.

The same CFO survey found a 9x acceleration in planned AI-driven job cuts for 2026 versus 2025. The intersection of accelerating AI deployment in employment decisions and the August 2026 compliance deadline creates a scenario where Icelandic employers face simultaneously: AI systems that may not deliver promised productivity, legal obligations they are unaware of, no domestic guidance on compliance, and no competent authority to provide that guidance.

### The EEA Observer Paradox

Iceland's participation as an observer in EU AI Board meetings is analytically significant. Iceland's government representatives are attending meetings at which other jurisdictions are discussing enforcement strategy, compliance challenges, and guidance development for the very obligations that will apply to Iceland. Iceland is absorbing this knowledge in an advisory capacity without building the domestic institutional infrastructure the knowledge is meant to inform.

The Joint Nordic Statement on AI Governance (February 2025), signed by Iceland among the five Nordic governments, committed to "global dialogue on AI governance" and responsible AI development. The statement was issued six months after the AI Act entered into force and seven months before the August 2025 deadline requiring Member States to designate competent authorities. Iceland signed a multilateral AI governance statement while missing an implementation deadline the statement's subject matter created.

### The Nordic Baseline: What Preparedness Looks Like

The EU member state Nordic countries — Denmark, Sweden, Finland — have been building AI Act implementation capacity since 2023. Their approaches illustrate what Iceland lacks:

**Denmark:** The Agency for Digital Government (Digitaliseringsstyrelsen) has published extensive employer guidance on AI Act obligations. Denmark's data protection authority (Datatilsynet) has issued AI-specific guidance on employment AI. Denmark's 42% enterprise AI adoption rate means thousands of employers are already subject to the high-risk obligations taking effect in August 2026, and they have had regulatory guidance for over a year.

**Sweden:** The Swedish Artificial Intelligence Commission (SAIF) published its landmark "Sweden and Artificial Intelligence" report in 2024, with specific chapters on employment AI and the Act's obligations. The Swedish Work Environment Authority has published guidance on AI in workplaces that pre-positions employers for compliance.

**Finland:** The Ministry of Economic Affairs and Employment has published employer guidance on AI Act obligations in employment contexts. Finland's Findata infrastructure enables the monitoring that Act compliance requires.

Iceland has published no employer guidance on the AI Act's employment provisions. The country has no equivalent of any of these bodies. The Vaxtarplan 2035 was published four months before a hard compliance deadline without acknowledging the deadline.

### The Stakes

The AI Act's enforcement provisions include fines of up to EUR 15 million or 3% of annual global turnover for deploying high-risk AI systems without complying with the Act's requirements. For Iceland's largest employers — the banks, the municipality of Reykjavík, the state entities that have already deployed AI in public service roles — these are material liabilities.

More consequentially for this report's analysis: the AI Act's employment provisions represent the minimum worker protection framework the EU has determined is appropriate for AI in employment decisions. Iceland is not currently positioned to deliver even that minimum. The Vaxtarplan 2035, which is the government's response to AI's impact on Iceland's economy, does not mention the legal framework that establishes what that response must include.

This is not a technical compliance failure. It is a governance failure of the same kind documented throughout this report: the absence of the institutional infrastructure to see what is coming.

*Sources: EU AI Act, Official Journal of the European Union; EU AI Act implementation timeline (artificialintelligenceact.eu); Crowell & Moring AI & HR 2026 Legal Overview; White & Case Norway AI Watch; OneTrust Digital Omnibus 2026; Joint Nordic AI Governance Statement, February 2025*

## Part V: The Sector-Specific Analysis

### 5.1 Tourism (Upplifun): Productivity Gains Will Be Displacing

The Vaxtarplan correctly identifies tourism as a sector requiring productivity improvement and governance reform. Tourism's share of Iceland's export composition grew from 19% (2010) to 32% (2024). The action plan proposes site fees, international marketing reform, and streamlined licensing.

The productivity gains the strategy anticipates will come from automation. Booking systems, customer service, translation, wayfinding, itinerary planning, and content generation — the back-office and front-of-house functions that make tourism work — are precisely the tasks that AI is automating at scale globally. Klarna replaced 700 customer service representatives with an OpenAI-powered system that handled 2.3 million conversations in one month. The comparable proportional substitution in Iceland's tourism sector, at its current employment scale of approximately 14,700 workers, would eliminate thousands of jobs.

The Varda 2026 survey found elevated stress specifically in the creative industries (51% report increased work pressure) and early education (50%) — sectors adjacent to tourism in the experience economy. The strategy does not model what tourism sector productivity improvement will mean for tourism sector employment.

### 5.2 ICT and Data Centers (Orkusækinn iðnaður): The Infrastructure Paradox

Priority 1, Action 1.4 establishes a framework for data centers and large AI computing infrastructure, leveraging Iceland's renewable energy advantage. The strategy is explicit: Iceland's 160,000 GWh endowment, cheap electricity, and cold climate make it a competitive location for compute-intensive AI workloads.

This is economically sound. Data center infrastructure is capital-intensive but employment-sparse. A large data center generating significant revenue may employ 50-200 people. The strategy is effectively proposing to import AI infrastructure (and its revenue) while the labor-market question is about what that infrastructure will do to Iceland's existing employment base.

The ICT trifecta already shows the pattern: ICT sector revenue up 72%, employment down 11.4%, vacancies down 94%. Data center investment will accelerate AI capability deployment by Icelandic enterprises, which will accelerate the productivity-employment substitution already visible in the data.

### 5.3 Fisheries and Aquaculture: The Governance Fracture

Priority 4 of the action plan contains 10 actions related to Iceland's natural resource industries. Five are devoted to fisheries and aquaculture — reflecting both their economic importance (fisheries exports 18-21% of total, down from 25%) and the political sensitivity of their governance.

The aquaculture-specific actions (3.5-3.7) address regulatory frameworks, special area designations, and property tax structures. This reflects an industry that has expanded significantly but whose governance has not kept pace with its scale.

The more fundamental question — whether Iceland's fisheries quota system is structurally compatible with the productivity-driven growth model the strategy proposes — is not addressed. The quota system creates rents that accrue to quota holders, not to productivity improvements or workforce development. This creates a structural misalignment between the strategy's stated productivity goals and the institutional incentives in one of its largest export sectors.

### 5.4 STEM and Education: The Pipeline That Runs Backward

The education actions (Priority 2) address a real gap. Iceland's STEM enrollment at tertiary level is 17% versus a 24% OECD average — a 7 percentage point deficit that compounds over time. The ISK 2.3 billion in annual education commitments is genuine investment in human capital.

The pipeline problem: the strategy invests in producing STEM graduates for a knowledge-sector job market that, in 2024-2025, showed a net loss of 9,300 knowledge-intensive jobs. If that contraction reflects a structural shift rather than a cyclical fluctuation, Iceland is investing in producing workers for roles that are disappearing.

This is not an argument against STEM education. It is an argument for understanding the interaction between supply-side investments and demand-side dynamics before scaling the supply-side investment. The strategy does not contain any mechanism to monitor whether STEM graduates are finding employment in their trained fields, what the wage outcomes are relative to expectations, or whether the productivity premium for STEM skills is holding as AI automates more STEM-adjacent tasks.

## Part VI: What Comparable Governance Looks Like

### 6.1 The Nordic Baseline

The claim that Iceland's governance is deficient requires a comparative baseline. That baseline is provided by the Nordic countries that share Iceland's institutional structure, labor market model, and economic profile:

**Denmark's tripartite AI accord (2023):** Danish employer confederations (DA), union confederations (LO, FH), and the government produced a joint declaration establishing a framework for responsible AI deployment in workplaces. The accord establishes consultation rights for workers when AI systems are deployed, transparency requirements for algorithmic management, and a joint monitoring committee. This is in addition to the Hilfr2 agreement — one of the first collective agreements in Europe to explicitly address AI and algorithmic management — covering platform workers.

**Norway's LO-NHO Basic Agreement AI provisions:** Norway's main collective agreement between the Confederation of Trade Unions (LO) and the Confederation of Norwegian Enterprise (NHO) includes provisions requiring: worker representative involvement in AI decision-making, worker notification when algorithmic management systems are deployed, bias prevention mechanisms, and privacy protections for worker data processed by AI systems. Norway achieved this through the same bilateral institutional structure Iceland has (confederations on both sides) but has not used.

**Sweden's Teknikföretagen-IF Metall framework:** Sweden's technology employer confederation and the metal workers' union established a joint framework for managing automation and AI in manufacturing workplaces, including retraining entitlements and transition support mechanisms.

**Finland's work on AI and the welfare system:** Finland's Findata infrastructure enables cross-register analysis that can link employment records, skills data, benefit claims, and health outcomes. Finnish researchers have published studies on automation's displacement effects using linked administrative data. No equivalent capacity exists in Iceland.

### 6.2 The Measurement Gap in Comparative Perspective

The Nordic data infrastructure comparison illustrates the magnitude of Iceland's deficiency:

| Denmark | DREAM+IDA | Yes (isoc_eb_ai) | Yes | Yes |

Iceland is the only Nordic country absent from all six measurement and governance dimensions. This is not because Iceland lacks institutional capacity. It is because no institution has been mandated to build it.

The "small country" argument — which Iceland's governance gaps sometimes attract — does not survive scrutiny. Estonia has a population of 1.3 million people, slightly larger than Iceland's 380,000, and participates in the Eurostat AI adoption survey, the EWCS, the EU AI Act's implementation framework, maintains linked administrative data infrastructure comparable to the Nordic countries, has a formal union AI engagement framework, and has an AI governance body designated as a competent authority under the AI Act. Estonia provides the clearest available evidence that a small European nation can build the full governance infrastructure Iceland lacks — and has done so at approximately 3.4x Iceland's population, not 10x. The "too small to participate" argument does not explain Iceland's absence; it is refuted by Estonia's presence.

### 6.3 The Cost of Non-Action

The EPRS study (*Digitalisation, Artificial Intelligence and Algorithmic Management in the Workplace*, PE 774.670, October 2025) — a "Cost of non-Europe" analysis commissioned by the European Parliament — finds that the absence of regulatory frameworks for algorithmic management imposes measurable costs: elevated worker stress, reduced ability to contest AI-driven decisions, and missed productivity gains from poor human-AI collaboration. The study projects that algorithmic management exposure will rise from 42.3% to 55.5% of European workers in the medium term.

Iceland will face the same trajectory. The EU AI Act will apply to Iceland through EEA incorporation. When it does, Iceland will need to comply with transparency, accountability, and worker rights provisions that EU member states have been preparing for since 2021. Iceland, with no national framework, no union AI agreements, and no administrative data infrastructure, will be implementing EU requirements with no domestic institutional capacity to monitor compliance or enforce protections.

## Part VII: The Academic and Research Gap

### 7.1 The University Desert

Iceland's AI research capacity is real but narrow. Approximately 40-50 AI-active researchers are distributed across four labs: CADIA (Reykjavik University), the Language and Voice Lab (RU), the NLP Lab (University of Iceland), and IIIM (the Icelandic Institute for Intelligent Machines). All eight Rannis AI grants in 2024-2025 went to RU or HI.

The Language and Voice Lab's work is internationally significant: the Samromur corpus (1 million verified speech clips, 967 hours of training data), a fine-tuned Whisper ASR model for Icelandic (120,000+ downloads), and the IceBERT language model family represent genuine contributions to Icelandic language technology infrastructure. IIIM's AGI research has won the Kurzweil Best Paper Award twice and hosted the 18th International AGI Conference in Reykjavik in August 2025.

What this research capacity does not include: quantitative labor economics research on AI's employment effects. No Icelandic economist is studying AI and employment using Icelandic administrative data. The BIAS project (University of Iceland, EU Horizon Europe) examines AI bias in recruitment and management — important but specific. Zero AI-labour research is funded through Rannis's AI grants.

The Halldórsson 2025 BS thesis, the Thorleifsdóttir 2023 MSc thesis (on AI in HRM), the Halldórsdóttir 2025 MSc thesis (on Íslandsbanki digital transformation) — these are the totality of Icelandic academic work on AI and employment. All are qualitative, single-institution or single-sector in scope, and below doctoral level.

### 7.1-A The First Domestic AI Survey: What It Found and What It Cannot Find

In 2025, Statistics Iceland (Hagstofa Íslands) conducted its first-ever enterprise ICT survey to include AI-specific questions. The survey covered a random sample of 848 Icelandic enterprises with a 62% response rate — a methodologically credible instrument.

Its findings are simultaneously useful and limited. **31% of Icelandic enterprises report concerns about AI use.** The top concern, cited by the majority of concerned firms, is lack of appropriate expertise. AI usage is highest in information and communication, professional/scientific/technical activities, and transportation and storage — exactly the sectors classified as high-risk under the EU AI Act's employment provisions and the sectors showing the most pronounced employment contractions in Hagstofa's labor market data. Strategic AI implementation is less common than tactical tool-level adoption. Smaller businesses report significantly more concern than larger ones.

What the survey cannot tell us: it does not measure which AI applications are being used, whether they are used in employment decision-making, how many workers have been affected, what the skill impacts have been, or whether firms have conducted workforce impact assessments before deploying AI. It is a first step toward domestic measurement — confirmation that measurement is possible and that Icelandic firms are using AI — but it is not the systematic AI adoption and impact survey that Iceland's governance needs.

The survey's existence also confirms that the cost and feasibility barriers to domestic AI measurement are lower than the strategy's omission implies. Hagstofa can survey 848 enterprises. It can ask better questions in next year's survey. The measurement gap is not a technical impossibility — it is an absence of mandate.

### 7.1-B The Voice That Said: Skip It

Perhaps the most important academic submission in Iceland's AI consultation came from CADIA (Center for Analysis and Design of Intelligent Agents at Reykjavik University) and IIIM (the Icelandic Institute for Intelligent Machines) — the two institutions that collectively represent the majority of Iceland's genuine AI research capacity.

Their submission to the AI Action Plan consultation argued not for engagement with the current AI wave but for Iceland to position itself differently: to invest in "smágagnagervigreind" (small-data AI) rather than competing in large-scale foundation model development. The submission explicitly compared Iceland's situation to that of African nations that skipped landline telephony and moved directly to mobile — arguing Iceland should similarly leapfrog current-generation AI to a differently positioned capability. The submission stated that the two-year AI Action Plan timeline was "far too short" and that "a minimum of 10 years" was needed for meaningful capability development.

This is a significant argument from the only actors in the consultation who actually build AI. And it raises a question the Vaxtarplan does not engage: if Iceland's most technically capable AI researchers believe the current AI wave is the wrong wave to board, on what basis has the government committed ISK 1.1 billion to boarding it? The strategy does not cite the CADIA/IIIM submission. The builders' voices are absent from the strategy document just as they were absent from the panels at SA Menntadagur 2025 — where the speakers on Iceland's AI future included lawyers, consultants, and government officials, but not a single person who has written an AI system.

The Vaxtarplan's ISK 1.1 billion AI infrastructure investment will accelerate AI deployment. It will not, as currently designed, fund any research on what that deployment is doing to Iceland's workers.

### 7.2 The Research Agenda That Does Not Exist

What research would close the key empirical gaps? The Panopticon investigation identified four studies that, if conducted, would transform Iceland's capacity to govern AI's labor market effects:

**Study 1: Prior-occupation data for disability claimants (2020-2025)**
A FOIA-equivalent request to Tryggingastofnun (TR) for a cross-tabulation of disability/rehabilitation diagnosis with prior employment sector and occupation. This single dataset would determine whether displaced knowledge workers are entering the welfare system through psychiatric pathways. TR has the administrative data; the question is whether it will be made available for research.

**Study 2: Iceland's inclusion in the Eurostat AI adoption survey**
A formal arrangement for Iceland to participate in the isoc_eb_ai survey would, at minimum, provide baseline adoption rates comparable to Nordic peers. This requires a negotiated inclusion with Eurostat — not technically difficult, politically straightforward, administratively modest in cost.

**Study 3: An Icelandic-equivalent linked administrative data study**
Using Iceland's existing administrative registers (tax records, VMST employment register, TR disability records) in a difference-in-differences design analogous to Humlum and Vestergaard's Danish study. This requires formal ethics approval, a research partnership between Statistics Iceland, the Employment Agency, and TR, and access to linked individual-level data. Iceland's small population (~380,000) actually makes this easier than comparable Nordic studies — the statistical power per researcher-hour is higher. The research infrastructure exists in principle; what is missing is the mandate and the research funding.

**Study 4: An Icelandic EWCS equivalent**
A nationally representative survey of worker conditions, AI and technology exposure, algorithmic management experiences, training adequacy, and wellbeing — structured to enable comparison with EWCS findings for other Nordic countries. This could be conducted by Statistics Iceland or by an academic-institutional consortium. The Varda survey (n=25,000) shows the organizational capacity for large-scale worker surveys exists in Iceland. What is needed is a survey instrument that asks the right questions about technology exposure.

None of these studies are proposed in the Vaxtarplan 2035.

## Part VIII: The Discourse and Its Silences

### 8.1 What the Policy Documents Say About AI

The three strategy documents collectively present a consistent picture of AI's role in Iceland's economic future:

**Vaxtarplan 2035:** AI appears as a driver of "verðmætasköpun" (value creation) and a justification for infrastructure investment. The document treats AI as a productivity multiplier — a factor that will make Icelandic workers and firms more productive if adequate infrastructure and skills are in place.

**Aðgerðaáætlun 2026-2027:** AI appears as both a target (Action 3.3, ISK 1.1 billion for AI infrastructure) and a context (the reason STEM education and workforce development are urgent). The action plan's framing is consistently optimistic: AI creates opportunities that Iceland must position to capture.

**Stöðugreining 2026:** AI does not appear in the diagnosis. The document's quantitative analysis — the most rigorous section of the three — does not mention AI as a factor in Iceland's economic trajectory. The analysis attributes growth and its productivity weakness to labor supply increases, capital intensity, and investment ratios. AI is invisible in the diagnosis even as it is central to the prescription.

### 8.2 What the Workers' Survey Says

The Varda 2026 survey provides the single most important counterweight to the strategy documents' framing. Published one month before the Vaxtarplan, the survey of 25,000 Icelandic workers found:

- **23%** report their jobs have changed or will change due to technology
- **36%** of those affected workers fear job loss
- **44%** report increased work pressure after technology changes (rising to **56%** among immigrant workers)
- **45%** feel they have received inadequate training for deployed technology
- **63%** believe changes will make work easier — but optimism is distributed unequally, concentrated among higher-income workers

The sectors reporting the highest work pressure increases are food production (56%), creative industries (51%), and early education (50%) — not the sectors the strategy prioritizes.

The Varda survey represents the first time Icelandic workers have been asked, at scale, what technology is doing to their working lives. The answer is that nearly one in four is experiencing technology-driven change, over a third of those fear losing their jobs, and nearly half feel undertrained. These are workers who are already experiencing what the strategy projects will happen — but the strategy was written without this data shaping its analysis.

### 8.3 What the Global Evidence Shows That Iceland Has Not Cited

The Vaxtarplan's discourse on AI is constructed almost entirely from domestic optimism and international comparator data on AI's potential benefits. It does not cite the emerging body of evidence on AI's actual displacement effects in the economies where deployment is furthest advanced.

The international displacement evidence, as of April 2026:

**Global tech sector layoffs (2025–2026):** According to TrueUp's layoff tracker, the technology sector globally shed 245,953 workers in full year 2025. In Q1 2026 alone, 78,557 tech workers were laid off globally — with 47.9% (37,638) explicitly attributed to AI by the companies announcing the cuts. The March 2026 figure was 18,720 tech job cuts — a 40% year-over-year increase vs. March 2025. At the current 2026 pace, Goldman Sachs estimates AI is cutting 16,000 U.S. jobs per month — with entry-level, white-collar, and administrative roles most exposed. The Duke CFO Survey (March 2026), conducted across 750 CFOs in partnership with the Federal Reserve Banks of Atlanta and Richmond, found that 44% of firms plan AI-related job cuts in 2026, projecting approximately 502,000 roles — a 9x increase over 2025's pace.

These are not projections. They are documented outcomes in the economies Iceland benchmarks itself against.

**The Klarna case:** Klarna — the Swedish-headquartered fintech whose model is directly comparable to Iceland's fintech sector (Lucinity, Monerium, etc.) — halved its workforce from approximately 7,000 to 3,500 employees between 2022 and 2025. Its AI assistant handled the equivalent of 700 full-time customer service agents within months of deployment. Revenue doubled to $903 million in Q3 2025. This is the ICT trifecta at company scale: revenue up, employment halved, vacancies near zero. It is a Nordic-adjacent company in a sector where Iceland's own strategy promotes growth.

**The Acemoglu constraint:** Daron Acemoglu (MIT, 2024 Nobel co-laureate) finds that AI's total factor productivity impact will be 0.53–0.66% over a decade — substantially below the optimistic projections Iceland's AI Action Plan implicitly borrows. More consequentially for Iceland's high-equality social contract, Acemoglu's analysis finds that even under full employment, AI may widen wealth inequality as returns to automatable labor fall while returns to capital rise.

**The Humlum-Vestergaard caveat:** The most rigorous available causal study of LLM effects on labor markets (NBER WP 33777, using 500,000+ Danish workers with linked administrative data) found near-zero average effects on earnings and hours in early adoption (2023–2024). This is often cited to dismiss displacement concerns. But the study's own authors note: the effects are early-stage, task restructuring is already occurring without wage changes (which may reflect structural underemployment), and the 3% average productivity gain, if compounded over the decade Iceland's strategy covers, represents substantial demand-side shifts. Iceland cannot run an equivalent study because the linked administrative data does not exist.

The discourse that shapes Iceland's strategy does not cite any of these findings. The Vaxtarplan's AI framing is constructed from the optimistic projection literature without systematic engagement with the displacement evidence. This is not ignorance — it is a choice about which evidence to make visible in policy discourse.

### 8.4 The "Hindrance" Framing

The AI Action Plan's Action E.2 describes ethical requirements and regulatory frameworks as obstacles to AI implementation that need to be removed. Legal scholar Dr. Hrefna Dögg Gunnarsdóttir, in her consultation response, correctly identified this framing as problematic:

> *"Regluverk sem sett hefur verið eftir viðurkenndum leiðum [...] [er] sáttmáli sem við höfum náð um samfélagið okkar."*
> ("Regulatory frameworks established through recognized procedures are a social compact we have reached about our society.")

When worker protection is defined as a "hindrance" to AI deployment, the policy frame has inverted the proper relationship between technology and governance. The Vaxtarplan 2035 does not use this language explicitly, but its structural omissions — no transition funds, no labor protections, no displacement monitoring — produce the same outcome through omission rather than commission.

## Part IX: The Evidence Balance Sheet

### 9.1 What the Strategy Gets Right

**The productivity diagnosis is correct.** The Stöðugreining's identification of labor-supply-driven growth, capital per worker decline, and below-average TFP is empirically sound and analytically honest. The prescription — productivity-driven growth — follows from the diagnosis.

**The STEM gap is real.** 17% STEM enrollment versus 24% OECD average is a structural deficit. The ISK 2.3 billion education investment is appropriately targeted.

**The energy opportunity is genuine.** Iceland's renewable energy endowment is an underutilized competitive advantage for energy-intensive industries and computing infrastructure.

**The export diversification need is well-documented.** Tourism at 32% of exports creates cyclical vulnerability. Fisheries governance reform is overdue. The natural resource industry rationalization (Priority 4) addresses legitimate structural issues.

**The foreign specialist attraction measures are pragmatic.** Fast-track permits and tax incentives for high-skill international workers address an immediate capacity constraint in a small labor market.

### 9.2 What the Strategy Gets Wrong

**The measurement foundation is borrowed.** The "55% of jobs affected" claim, the "30% time savings" claim, and the investment projections all rest on international estimates applied to Iceland without domestic validation. The government simultaneously commissioned the study that would have generated domestic estimates (Action B.1) and used the international estimates in the strategy.

**The displacement signal is ignored.** The ICT employment decline (-11.4%), the knowledge-occupation contraction (-9,300 jobs in one year), and the vacancy collapse (-94% in the ICT sector) are not addressed. The strategy prescribes more of what the data suggests is already displacing workers.

**The institutional vacuum is unaddressed.** The absence of union AI frameworks, the 14-day consultation window, the zero union participation — these governance failures are not remedied in the Vaxtarplan.

**The welfare pipeline is ungoverned.** The 40% single-year rehabilitation expenditure growth, the psychiatric diagnosis dominance, and the absence of occupation-level data from TR combine to produce a system that cannot detect whether displaced workers are entering welfare through disability pathways.

**The research capacity is unbuilt.** ISK 1.1 billion in AI infrastructure investment is not paired with any research mandate to study AI's labor effects. No economist is studying AI and employment in Iceland; the Vaxtarplan does not create one.

### 9.3 The Falsification Standard

What would falsify the claim that the strategy has a systematic blind spot?

If the government could demonstrate any of the following, the analysis would need to be substantially revised:

1. Iceland's enterprise AI adoption rate is measurably below the EU average, such that displacement dynamics are not yet operative at significant scale
2. The knowledge-occupation contraction (2024-2025) is explained by factors unrelated to AI adoption (fiscal tightening, LFS sampling variance, sectoral rebalancing) with specific evidence
3. The ICT trifecta reflects factors other than productivity displacement (with evidence)
4. The welfare system expansion is driven by factors that cannot plausibly connect to labor market disruption

On point 1: Iceland is excluded from the Eurostat survey that would answer the question. The adoption rate is unknown.
On points 2-3: No institution has investigated these patterns. The signals exist; their causes are unmeasured.
On point 4: The 35-year secular trend predates AI, and COVID confounders are real. But the occupation-level welfare data that would distinguish between explanations does not exist.

The epistemically honest position is that the strategy could be correct — AI-driven displacement may not be occurring at significant scale in Iceland, the knowledge-occupation contraction may reflect non-AI factors, and the welfare expansion may be fully explained by pre-existing trends. But this position cannot be verified with existing data. The strategy is governing on the assumption that the optimistic scenario is operating, in the absence of the measurement infrastructure that would detect if it is not.

### 9.4 The Compound Risk: When Multiple Blindnesses Interact

The six omissions documented in Part III are not independent failures. They interact in ways that compound the governance risk:

**The measurement vacuum enables the ICT trifecta to go unnoticed.** If Iceland were included in the Eurostat AI adoption survey, policymakers would have enterprise-level data on AI deployment rates by sector. If the EWCS were conducted, there would be worker-level data on algorithmic management exposure. With this data, the ICT sector's 11.4% employment decline could be contextualized against AI adoption rates and worker exposure — either confirming the displacement signal or identifying alternative explanations. Without it, the signal sits uninterpreted.

**The institutional silence enables the discourse-reality gap.** If Iceland's unions were engaged in AI governance, they would be surfacing worker experiences — the 10 HR managers who admitted ChatGPT use after initially denying it, the administrative workers experiencing reduced "cognitive strain" from AI assistance, the clerical workers whose roles are being restructured. Instead, union silence allows the policy discourse to be shaped by technology advocates and resellers who have structural incentives to frame AI's labor effects as manageable through upskilling.

**The welfare black box makes the occupation polarization invisible at its consequences.** The -9,300 knowledge-sector jobs in 2024-2025 are a labor market signal. Whether those job losses translated into unemployment (visible in VMST data), welfare entry (potentially visible in TR data), out-migration, or reabsorption in other sectors — is unknown. If they went to welfare through psychiatric pathways, the system has no mechanism to see it. The governance failure is not just that displacement might be occurring — it is that the downstream consequences of displacement are administratively invisible at every stage of the pipeline.

**The per-capita growth stagnation provides the stakes.** Iceland's 0.3% per-capita GDP growth between 2020 and 2024 — dramatically below the OECD advanced economy average of 1.3% — means Iceland is losing ground on living standards even in nominal expansion periods. The strategy's response is to accelerate the productivity transformation through AI. If that transformation is displacement-intensive and the displacement is unmonitored, Iceland risks compounding its welfare burden at precisely the moment its growth model is supposed to be delivering productivity dividends. The compound risk is not hypothetical. The data showing it is forming exists in three separate Hagstofa tables, a TR annual report, a Rikisendurskoðun audit, and a Hagstofa ICT survey. No institution is assembling these signals into a unified picture. That is what the Panopticon investigation found. That is what the Vaxtarplan 2035 should have found — and did not.

### 9.5 The Document's Own Words: Key Icelandic Quotes

The three government documents, read carefully, contain admissions and framings that are more analytically honest than the strategy's overall posture might suggest. Collected here are the most diagnostically significant quotations — the places where the government's own text reveals the structural tensions this report has documented.

**On the core productivity failure:**

> *"Þótt hagvöxtur Íslands hafi verið góður á tímabilinu 2010–2024 [...] hafa íbúar Íslands ekki notið sömu hagvaxtar og þjóðir sem við berum okkur saman við."*
> ["Although Iceland's GDP growth has been good in the period 2010–2024, the inhabitants of Iceland have not enjoyed the same growth as the nations we compare ourselves to."]
> — Vaxtarplan til 2035, p. 3; Stöðugreining, Slide 2

This is the strategy's opening admission: headline growth was good; per-capita growth was not. The government is announcing its own failure before presenting its prescription.

**On the failure of job quality — the most devastating single finding in the Stöðugreining:**

> *"Aðeins 1% þeirra starfa sem bæst hafa við (nettó) á almennum vinnumarkaði síðustu 15 árin eru innan atvinnugreina með háa framleiðni."*
> ["Only 1% of jobs added (net) on the general labour market over the last 15 years are within industries with high productivity."]
> — Stöðugreining 2026, Slide 16

This is the quantitative summary of Iceland's growth model pathology. The economy added approximately 49,608 net new jobs over fifteen years. Of those, only 652 — 1.3% — were in sectors with productivity at least 10% above the national average. The other 98.7% were in average or below-average productivity sectors. The strategy's entire prescription is aimed at the 1% problem — building the conditions to shift job creation into high-productivity sectors. What the strategy does not address is the workforce already in the 99% who face displacement from those same low and medium productivity roles if AI automation reaches them.

**On the capital per worker deficit:**

> *"Í dag er 6% minna fjármagn að baki hverju stöðugildi en í upphafi tímabilsins."*
> ["Today there is 6% less capital behind each full-time equivalent than at the start of the period."]
> — Stöðugreining 2026, Slide 12

The economy grew. Capital grew. Workers grew faster than capital. The result is that each worker today has access to less capital equipment than they did fourteen years ago — a reversal of the capital deepening that underpins productivity growth.

**On total factor productivity — Iceland's structural efficiency deficit:**

> *"Að auki virðast framleiðsluþættir, þ.e. vinnuafl og fjármagn, nýtast verst á Íslandi þar sem heildarþáttaframleiðnin er 12% undir meðaltali samanburðarríkja."*
> ["In addition, production factors — labour and capital — appear to be used least efficiently in Iceland, as total factor productivity is 12% below the average of comparison countries."]
> — Stöðugreining 2026, Slide 17

Iceland works harder than its peers (employment rate +15% above average) with less capital per worker (-8%) and uses both factors less efficiently (-12% TFP). The aggregate output is near peer average only because of the extraordinary participation rate. This is the country's structural vulnerability: the one advantage — high employment — is the one that is most demographically fragile and most susceptible to automation-driven reduction.

**On the demographic clock that is already ticking:**

> *"Ísland nýtur góðs af mikilli atvinnuþátttöku og hagstæðri aldurssamsetnigu íbúa. Ekki er hægt að gera ráð fyrir að það ástand vari til allrar framtíðar."*
> ["Iceland benefits from high labour participation and a favourable age structure. It cannot be assumed that this situation will persist forever."]
> — Vaxtarplan til 2035, p. 11

The government is acknowledging, in its own strategy document, that the demographic foundation of its economic model is time-limited. The favorable age structure and high participation rate that have compensated for weak TFP and below-average capital intensity will not persist. The strategy must produce productivity gains before the demographic dividend expires. The AI transition is occurring in the same decade as that expiration.

**On the STEM deficit — framed as an education problem but revealing an economy problem:**

> *"Hlutfall háskólamenntaðra á Íslandi er í takt við samanburðarþjóðir en hlutfall nemenda í tæknigreinum (STEM) er langt undir meðaltali. Til að styðja við vöxt þekkingar- og tæknidrifinna atvinnugreina er mikilvægt að fjölga brautskráðum nemendum með STEM-menntun."*
> ["The proportion of university graduates in Iceland is in line with comparison countries but the proportion of students in technical fields (STEM) is far below average. To support growth in knowledge- and technology-driven industries, it is important to increase the number of graduates with STEM education."]
> — Vaxtarplan til 2035, p. 11

Iceland has the right number of university graduates (44% with tertiary degrees, near peer average of 47%) but the wrong distribution: 17% in STEM versus 24% OECD average, and 44% in social sciences versus 32% OECD average. The country has over-invested in social sciences relative to its economic needs and under-invested in technical fields. The AI transition, which will most acutely affect non-technical knowledge workers in administrative, legal, communications, and social services roles, is arriving precisely as Iceland has 44% of its university graduates concentrated in the fields most exposed to AI substitution.

**On AI — the only policy framing in the entire Vaxtarplan:**

> *"Tryggja þarf færni til að nýta gervigreind á íslenskum vinnumarkaði á ábyrgan hátt til að auka framleiðni vinnuafls."*
> ["It is necessary to ensure skills to use AI in the Icelandic labour market in a responsible manner to increase workforce productivity."]
> — Vaxtarplan til 2035, p. 22

This sentence is the entirety of the Vaxtarplan's treatment of AI's labor market implications. The word "displacement" does not appear in the document. The word "brotthvarf" (redundancy/departure of jobs) does not appear. The word "atvinnumissir" (job loss) does not appear. AI appears in the Vaxtarplan as a productivity tool to be adopted responsibly — full stop. The risk side of the technology is absent from the document that is Iceland's 10-year employment strategy.

**On the action plan's framing of AI — combining opportunity and implied risk:**

> *"Skapa þarf skilyrði til að fanga tækifæri þessarar tæknibyltingar um leið og gætt er að siðferðilegum álitaefnum og mögulegum hættum."*
> ["Conditions must be created to capture the opportunities of this technological revolution while attending to ethical considerations and possible risks."]
> — Aðgerðaáætlun 2026-2027, p. 18

The action plan mentions "possible risks" ("mögulegum hættum") — the only concession in either document to AI's downside. But neither the action plan nor the strategy specifies what those risks are, which workers face them, or what protections apply if they materialize. "Possible risks" without enumeration, quantification, or governance response is acknowledgment as performance — the rhetorical gesture of risk awareness without the institutional substance of risk management.

*Sources: Vaxtarplan til 2035, April 2026; Stöðugreining 2026; Aðgerðaáætlun 2026-2027*

## Part IX-B: The KPI Vacuum

### 9B.1 The Nine Metrics That Measure Nothing

The Vaxtarplan 2035 concludes its strategic framework with a set of nine *mælikvarðar* — metrics — intended to track the strategy's progress over ten years. These nine metrics are presented in two categories: five *afrakstur* (output/outcome) metrics and four *inntak* (input) metrics.

The full table, as extracted from the document:

**AFRAKSTUR (Output/Outcome Metrics) — Page 16**

| # | Metric (Icelandic) | English Translation | What It Purports to Measure |
|---|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| 01 | Aukin landsframleiðsla á mann | Increased GDP per capita | "Strong metric of improving living standards and economic performance" |
| 02 | Meiri framleiðni vinnuafls | Higher labour productivity | "Strong metric of progress and economic performance" |
| 03 | Aukinn útflutningur og fjölbreytni útflutnings | Increased and diversified exports | "Signals whether support for export sectors and economic vitality is succeeding" |
| 04 | Minni losunarkræfni útflutnings yfir alla virðiskeðjuna | Lower emissions intensity of exports | "Indicates increased growth alongside reduction in greenhouse gas emissions" |
| 05 | Auknar atvinnutekjur í öllum landshlutum | Increased business income in all regions | "Signals whether business development across the country is succeeding" |

**INNTAK (Input Metrics) — Page 17**

| # | Metric (Icelandic) | English Translation | What It Purports to Measure |
|---|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| 01 | Aukin atvinnuvegafjárfesting og bein erlend fjárfesting | Increased business investment and FDI | "Supports increased productivity and signals healthy economic environment" |
| 02 | Aukin raforkuvinnsla | Increased electricity production | "Supports increased exports and growth in high-productivity sectors" |

Not one of the nine metrics has a quantified target. Not one has a documented baseline value. Not one has a 2035 goal. Not one has an intermediate checkpoint — no 2027 milestone, no 2030 waypoint, no measurement frequency commitment. These are directional labels, not key performance indicators. They describe the direction Iceland wants to move without specifying how far, by when, or how to determine whether progress has been made.

### 9B.2 What a KPI Actually Requires

In any context where measurable performance matters — corporate governance, public sector accountability, scientific research, or basic program evaluation — a key performance indicator requires five components:

1. **A specific, numerically defined target** (not "increase GDP per capita" but "increase GDP per capita by X% relative to peer average by 2030")
2. **A documented baseline** (the value of the metric at strategy launch)
3. **A defined measurement methodology** (which data source, which calculation, updated how frequently)
4. **A timeline** (when will the target be reached, with intermediate checkpoints)
5. **An accountability assignment** (which institution is responsible for reporting progress)

The Vaxtarplan's nine metrics satisfy none of these requirements. They satisfy the surface appearance of measurement — there are nine named indicators, organized into two categories, accompanied by brief explanatory text — without the substance of measurement.

This is not a minor technical omission. A strategy that cannot be measured cannot be evaluated. A strategy that cannot be evaluated cannot be corrected. A strategy that cannot be corrected based on evidence is, by definition, immune to evidence. Iceland's government has produced a 10-year strategy with no mechanism to determine whether it succeeds or fails.

### 9B.3 The Contrast: What Serious Target-Setting Looks Like

To illustrate what the metrics would need to look like to function as actual KPIs, consider how the nine directional labels could be operationalized using data already present in the strategy documents:

| Vaxtarplan Directional Label | Evidence-Based KPI Version |
|------------------------------|---------------------------|
| "Increased GDP per capita" | Increase Iceland's PPP-adjusted GDP per capita from USD 65,600 (2024, Slide 17) to USD 73,700 (Denmark's 2024 level) by 2035; measure annually via Statistics Iceland National Accounts |
| "Higher labour productivity" | Increase TFP index from 114 (2024, 12% below peer average) to 125 (within 4% of peer average) by 2035; measure annually via OECD productivity database |
| "Increased and diversified exports" | Reduce tourism's share of total exports from 32% (2024) to below 25% by 2035 while maintaining total export growth above 2% annually; measure annually via Hagstofa FYR tables |
| "Lower emissions intensity of exports" | Reduce GHG emissions per unit of export value by 20% from 2024 baseline by 2030; measure via Umhverfisstofnun annual GHG inventory |
| "Increased business income in all regions" | Reduce regional GDP per capita disparity between Reykjavík Capital Region and other regions by 15% from 2024 baseline by 2035; measure via Statistics Iceland regional accounts |
| "Increased business investment and FDI" | Maintain business investment above 15% of GDP (2024: 17%); increase FDI stock by 30% from 2024 baseline by 2030; measure via Seðlabanki annual data |
| "Increased electricity production" | Increase electricity production from 19.6 TWh (2024) to 26 TWh (midpoint of low-scenario range) by 2030; measure via Landsnet/Orkustofnun annual reports |
| "Increased R&D investment" | Increase R&D spending from current level to 2.0% of GDP (currently below OECD average) by 2030; measure via Rannis/OECD Main Science and Technology Indicators |
| "More STEM and vocational graduates" | Increase STEM enrollment share from 17% (2022) to 22% by 2030 (midpoint between Iceland's current 17% and OECD average 24%); increase vocational graduation rate by 30% from 2024 baseline; measure annually via Statistics Iceland education statistics |

*Baselines from Stöðugreining 2026; comparators from OECD/Eurostat; methodology notes from existing Icelandic statistical infrastructure*

None of these target-setting exercises requires data Iceland does not already have. The Stöðugreining provides the baselines. The OECD and Eurostat provide the peer comparators. The existing Hagstofa, Rannis, Orkustofnun, and Seðlabanki data series provide the annual measurement infrastructure. All that is required is the political commitment to state a number, attach a year, and allow progress to be measured.

The absence of numbers is not a technical failure. It is a governance choice. Targets create accountability. Accountability enables criticism. Criticism requires that the government be prepared to acknowledge if the strategy is not working. The nine directional labels are, functionally, accountability-avoidance — they allow the government to claim progress regardless of what the data shows, because the data shows progress by definition whenever the direction is positive and "increase" can mean any increase.

### 9B.4 The AI-Specific Absence

The KPI vacuum is particularly consequential for the strategy's AI-related commitments. The Vaxtarplan's treatment of AI, as documented in Section 8 of this report, consists of exactly two substantive mentions: AI as a productivity tool to be adopted (p. 22) and AI as infrastructure to be built (p. 19). Neither mention is accompanied by any measurement commitment.

The ISK 1.1 billion AI infrastructure investment (Action 3.3) has no KPI. There is no target for what the infrastructure will produce — not in terms of AI adoption rates, not in terms of productivity outcomes, not in terms of Icelandic-language model capability, not in terms of enterprise deployment. The action's deliverable is "increased investment in computing infrastructure and equipment for AI development; first milestones in Icelandic language model development" — a process deliverable, not an outcome target.

This means Iceland will spend 1.1 billion ISK on AI infrastructure with no mechanism to determine whether that spending produced the productivity gains that justify it. If the investment produces no measurable productivity improvement (consistent with the Duke CFO survey finding that 80% of AI-investing firms report no productivity gains), there is no KPI that would detect this outcome, no accountability mechanism that would report it, and no governance trigger that would prompt policy revision.

The nine metrics describe an Iceland that is growing, innovating, and exporting more. The strategy's AI bet — 1.1 billion ISK — is untethered to any measurement of whether the bet pays off.

### 9B.5 The Comparison: Nordic Strategy Measurement Standards

Iceland's approach to strategy measurement stands in contrast to comparable Nordic strategies:

**Denmark's national strategy for AI** includes specific targets: AI adoption rate targets by sector and year, workforce development targets with numeric enrollments and timelines, and productivity improvement targets with percentage goals and measurement methodologies. Denmark can measure whether its AI strategy is working because its strategy states what working looks like.

**Norway's industrial strategy** includes an explicit "results chain" from inputs (investments) through activities (programs and interventions) to outputs (measurable deliverables) and outcomes (economic and social impacts), with each link defined numerically. Norway reports against these targets annually.

**Finland's AI program** includes specific targets: number of AI experts trained, number of firms receiving AI advisory support, R&D investment targets as percentage of GDP, and productivity outcome projections against which actual results are measured.

Iceland's nine directional labels are not equivalent to these approaches. They are compatible with any outcome, positive or negative, as long as any single indicator shows any positive movement in any year. A strategy that cannot fail its own measurement criteria is not a strategy — it is a political document.

### 9B.6 Why This Matters for the Central Argument

The KPI vacuum is not a separate problem from the other five omissions documented in this report. It is the governance architecture that makes the omissions sustainable.

If Iceland had a KPI specifying "increase STEM enrollment from 17% to 22% by 2030," the failure to meet that target would be detectable and reportable. If Iceland had a KPI specifying "AI adoption rate among firms with 10+ employees to reach 25% by 2028," the exclusion from the Eurostat survey would be an embarrassing measurement gap rather than an invisible omission. If Iceland had a KPI specifying "reduce share of workers who report inadequate technology training from 45% (2026 baseline, Varda survey) to 20% by 2030," the absence of a reskilling budget would be a funded-program deficit rather than an unstated choice.

The nine directional labels without numbers, baselines, or targets function as a governance architecture that renders the strategy's blind spots invisible. You cannot measure what the strategy is missing if the strategy has not defined what it should be measuring.

> Directional labels are not mælikvarðar. They are wishes. Iceland's employment strategy for 2035 is, by this measure, a 10-year wish list.

## Part X: The Policy Recommendations

The following recommendations flow from the evidence analysis above. They are framed as additions to and modifications of the existing strategy, not as replacements for it. The strategy's core investments in infrastructure, education, and sector governance are substantively sound. What is missing is the measurement, governance, and protection infrastructure that would make those investments safe.

### 10.1 Immediate Measurement Actions (Year 1)

**Action M.1: Eurostat survey inclusion**
Negotiate Iceland's formal inclusion in the Eurostat *isoc_eb_ai* enterprise AI adoption survey. Estimated cost: minimal (administrative inclusion in existing survey infrastructure). Estimated benefit: annual comparable data on enterprise AI adoption, enabling benchmarking against Nordic peers.

**Action M.2: EWCS participation**
Approach Eurofound for Iceland's reinclusion in the next wave of the European Working Conditions Survey. Iceland participated in earlier waves; exclusion from 2024 was not inevitable. Estimated cost: survey field costs (manageable for a country of 380,000). Benefit: comparable worker conditions data for the first time since 2015.

**Action M.3: TR occupation-level data disclosure**
Require Tryggingastofnun to publish annual cross-tabulations of disability and rehabilitation claims by prior employment sector and occupation. This requires no new data collection — the administrative data exists. It requires a disclosure mandate. This is the single most important step toward closing the causal chain that the Panopticon investigation could not close.

**Action M.4: VMST measurement gap resolution**
The 17% discrepancy between Hagstofa and VMST unemployment measurements, flagged by the Rikisendurskoðun, must be resolved with a published reconciliation methodology. A formal working group between Hagstofa, VMST, and the Rikisendurskoðun should produce a harmonized measurement framework within 12 months.

### 10.2 Governance Actions (Year 1-2)

**Action G.1: Tripartite AI council**
Establish a formal tripartite AI governance council — employer confederation (SA), labor confederation (ASÍ), and government — with a mandate to negotiate Iceland's equivalent of the Norwegian LO-NHO Basic Agreement AI provisions. The council should produce an AI workplace framework within 24 months covering: worker notification when AI systems are deployed, transparency in algorithmic management, worker representative involvement in AI decision-making, and bias monitoring.

**Action G.2: Extend consultation windows**
Legislate a minimum 30-day consultation period for major technology policy documents. The 14-day window for the AI Action Plan consultation was insufficient for substantive engagement by any organization, which structurally disadvantaged labor unions (which require member consultation processes) relative to think tanks and industry groups.

**Action G.3: AI Act early implementation**
Rather than waiting for EEA incorporation, proactively establish an Icelandic AI Act implementation framework that anticipates the EU requirements Iceland will need to comply with. The supervisory authority structure, transparency requirements, and fundamental rights obligations of the EU AI Act will apply to Iceland; building institutional capacity before legal obligation creates the capacity to implement effectively.

### 10.3 Research Actions (Year 1-3)

**Action R.1: AI-labour economics chair**
Fund a dedicated research position — full professor or associate professor level — at Statistics Iceland, Reykjavik University, or the University of Iceland for AI and labour economics research. The research mandate should include longitudinal analysis of AI adoption's employment effects using Icelandic administrative data. Annual budget: approximately ISK 30-50 million. This is a marginal addition to the ISK 1.1 billion AI infrastructure investment.

**Action R.2: Linked administrative data study**
Commission a formal linked administrative data study analogous to Humlum and Vestergaard (2025) using Iceland's existing registers. Bring together Statistics Iceland (Hagstofa), VMST, TR, and Sjúkratryggingar in a formal research collaboration with ethics approval. Iceland's small population makes this study methodologically feasible and potentially more analytically tractable than comparable Nordic studies.

**Action R.3: Annual worker technology survey**
Commission an annual survey instrument — administrable alongside the existing Labor Force Survey — measuring technology exposure, algorithmic management, AI tool use, training adequacy, and wellbeing among Icelandic workers. The Varda survey (n=25,000) demonstrates that large-scale worker surveying is operationally feasible in Iceland. Institutionalizing a comparable instrument within the official statistics framework would create the annual data series Iceland currently lacks.

### 10.4 Protection Actions (Year 2-4)

**Action P.1: Displacement transition fund**
Establish a dedicated transition fund — analogous to the EU's Just Transition Fund concept — for workers displaced from occupations facing technology-driven restructuring. The fund should provide funded retraining entitlements, income bridging during retraining periods, and outplacement support. The fund should be capitalized at a level proportional to the scale of affected workers (the government's own projection of 130,000 "greatly affected" workers justifies substantial capitalization).

**Action P.2: Retraining entitlement**
Legislate a statutory entitlement to funded retraining for workers whose positions are eliminated as a result of technological change, with a definition broad enough to encompass AI-driven restructuring alongside automation more broadly. The entitlement should cover minimum training duration, income replacement during training, and employer contribution requirements.

**Action P.3: Algorithmic management transparency**
In advance of EU AI Act requirements, require Icelandic employers to disclose to workers (a) when algorithmic management systems are used to monitor, evaluate, or make decisions about their work, and (b) what data is collected and how it is used. This is implementable through amendment to the existing Personal Data Protection Act and existing employment law.

## Conclusion: The Governance Failure Behind the Policy Gap

Iceland is not failing to govern AI because its officials are incompetent or its institutions are weak. Iceland is failing to govern AI because it has not built the measurement infrastructure that would reveal what governing AI requires.

The Vaxtarplan 2035 is a credible, well-structured, ambitiously funded industrial strategy for a small open economy facing a genuine productivity challenge. It correctly identifies the sources of Iceland's growth performance and prescribes investments in the right sectors. Its authors are not ignorant of AI's disruptive potential — the strategy allocates ISK 1.1 billion to AI infrastructure and makes AI-readiness a central theme.

The failure is more subtle and therefore more dangerous: the strategy is designed to optimize within a framework that assumes AI's effects will be manageable through market mechanisms and education investments, without building the feedback loops that would detect if they are not.

The six omissions documented in this report — the measurement vacuum, the ICT trifecta, the occupation polarization, the institutional labor silence, the discourse-reality gap, and the welfare black box — are not random gaps. They are a pattern. The pattern is of a governance system that has no mechanism to observe harm as it accumulates.

Iceland's ICT sector shed 1,300 jobs, grew revenue by 72%, and collapsed its vacancy rate by 94%. No institution assembled these three signals into a finding until the Panopticon investigation did it manually. The welfare system absorbed a 40% single-year expenditure increase in rehabilitation pensions. No institution knows whether any of those new claimants were previously working in the ICT sector, because no institution collected that data.

The workers surveyed by Varda in February 2026 — 23% of them reporting technology-driven change in their jobs, 45% feeling undertrained, 36% fearing displacement — are experiencing something measurable. The institutions responsible for monitoring Iceland's labor market and welfare system cannot yet see it.

That institutional blindness — not displacement itself — is the governance failure. The Vaxtarplan 2035 is a strategy for an economy Iceland wants to become. It needs to be a strategy for an economy Iceland can see.

## Appendix D: Corporate AI Adoption Evidence — Iceland's Major Employers

### D.1 Banking Sector: The Three-Bank Analysis

Iceland's three major commercial banks — Landsbankinn, Íslandsbanki, and Arion Bank — provide the most documented corporate AI adoption evidence available in Iceland. Their annual reports constitute primary source evidence of AI deployment decisions and their headcount consequences.

**Landsbankinn: The Strongest Corporate Evidence**

Landsbankinn's 2024 Annual and Sustainability Report contains the clearest explicit linkage between technology adoption and headcount reduction available from any Icelandic corporate source:

*"The number of full-time positions has decreased in tandem with technological advancements."*

This sentence appears in the context of Landsbankinn deploying "Elli," an AI chatbot that resolves 60% of web chat queries with 88% customer satisfaction. The bank also reported 85% of mortgages granted digitally, 33 app updates in the year, and its operating cost ratio at 32.4% — described internally as "never lower."

The headcount data supports the statement: average FTE positions fell from 849 in 2023 to 811 in 2024, a decline of 38 positions (-4.5%). Year-end FTE positions tell a more nuanced story: 822 at end-2024 versus 817 at end-2023, a slight increase of 5. The average FTE decline is more meaningful — it reflects sustained position reduction across the full year rather than a single-quarter fluctuation.

| Landsbankinn Metric | 2022 | 2023 | 2024 | Change |
|---------------------|------|------|------|--------|
| Average FTE | — | 849 | 811 | -38 (-4.5%) |
| Year-end FTE | — | 817 | 822 | +5 |
| Cost-to-income ratio | — | 33.7% | 32.4% | -1.3 pp |
| Chatbot (Elli) query resolution | — | — | 60% | New metric |
| Digital mortgage applications | — | — | 85% | New metric |

The Landsbankinn evidence is the strongest available corporate demonstration of the AI → efficiency gain → headcount reduction sequence in Iceland. It involves explicit corporate language acknowledging the linkage, measurable headcount data, and documented AI deployments that plausibly explain the reduction.

What the evidence does not support: the Landsbankinn reduction (38 average FTE from a base of 849) is small in absolute terms. The bank also underwent branch network restructuring ("the Hub" format) that confounds the AI attribution. The causal link is supported but not exclusive.

**Íslandsbanki: The Partial Case**

Íslandsbanki in 2024 deployed "Sam," an internal AI assistant for employee questions and quality manual access, and began integrating Microsoft Copilot across operations. The bank also automated portions of its lending process with machine learning models and improved AML/KYC processes with AI-assisted monitoring.

In Q4 2024, Íslandsbanki announced a 20-person restructuring, explicitly citing "rapid technological development and regulatory changes" as factors. Year-end headcount was 733 employees.

| Íslandsbanki Metric | 2023 | 2024 | Change |
|---------------------|------|------|--------|
| Year-end employees | ~753 | 733 | ~-20 |
| Cost-to-income ratio | 40.6% | 43.9% | +3.3 pp (worsened) |
| AI tools deployed | None | Sam, Copilot (in progress), ML models | New |

The Íslandsbanki case is weaker than Landsbankinn: the 20-person reduction was attributed partly to regulatory restructuring, the bank reorganized into new business divisions, and the cost-to-income ratio actually worsened (suggesting the AI investment has not yet generated efficiency gains). The corporate language was more cautious: "rapid technological development" was cited alongside "regulatory changes," not as a standalone cause.

**Arion Bank: The Counter-Evidence**

Arion Bank is the essential data point that prevents simple causal storytelling. While deploying digital-first strategies and explicitly stating that "AI and automation will increase operational efficiency in coming years," Arion Bank increased headcount from 822 to 858 employees in 2024 (+4.4%). The bank's return on equity rose and its net profit grew by 19% (to ISK 30.6 billion by 2025).

Arion demonstrates that AI investment and headcount growth are not mutually exclusive — at least in the short term, and at least for a bank in expansion mode. Arion's experience as counter-evidence does not neutralize the Landsbankinn signal, but it establishes that AI adoption does not inevitably produce immediate headcount reductions in the banking sector.

**Banking Sector Net:**

Across all three institutions, net headcount change from AI adoption is approximately -22 positions (Landsbankinn -38, Íslandsbanki -20, Arion +36), covering approximately 2,600 total employees. This is a 0.8% net reduction — modest and not alarming as an isolated figure. The significance is not the absolute number but the trajectory: Landsbankinn explicitly projects continued technology-driven efficiency gains, Íslandsbanki continues Copilot deployment, and all three banks are investing in systems that historically produce efficiency at the expense of labor.

### D.2 The Advania Signal

Advania's AI deployment is quantitatively more significant than the banking sector evidence. Iceland's largest IT services company deployed Microsoft Copilot to all 1,000+ of its employees — the most comprehensive corporate AI adoption on record for any Icelandic firm.

Advania CEO Hildur Einarsdóttir made the most candid displacement statement by any Icelandic executive (MBL, August 30, 2025): "AI will take over manual tasks — we must have open discussion about which jobs it fully replaces." This statement — not from a labor advocate, not from an academic, but from the CEO of the company that is simultaneously acting as Iceland's largest Microsoft AI reseller — is the most direct acknowledgment in the Icelandic public record that AI is on a trajectory toward full job replacement, not merely augmentation.

Advania's business model creates a structural conflict of interest in the policy discourse. As Microsoft's Copilot implementation partner in Iceland, Advania profits from AI adoption. As an employer of 1,000+ workers on Copilot, Advania is simultaneously a case study in what large-scale AI deployment does to a knowledge worker workforce. The CEO's statement suggests awareness that the "augmentation not replacement" framing in the government's AI Action Plan may not accurately describe the medium-term trajectory.

### D.3 The Government's Self-Contradiction

The Prime Minister's Office use of OpenAI to categorize 10,000 public submissions to the "Verum hagsýn" initiative (January 2025) is a specific, documented case of the government using AI to replace administrative work — while the government's own AI Action Plan declares the policy principle of "augmentation, not replacement."

The categorization of 10,000 submissions is precisely the kind of administrative work that the AI Action Plan projects AI will automate away from public employees. The Action Plan states "30% of public employee working time is spent on tasks that could be automated with AI." The government's own office then automated a specific such task without publicly acknowledging the contradiction.

This self-contradiction is significant not because it is a scandal but because it reveals the gap between stated policy and operational behavior. The "augmentation not replacement" principle is a politically palatable framing. The actual deployment decisions — at the Prime Minister's Office, in the banking sector, in corporate Iceland — are not consistently governed by that principle.

The strategy proposes to invest in knowledge-sector infrastructure and skills for a job market whose knowledge sector has been flat-to-declining.

### E.3 The Wage Signal

ICT sector wages present an additional puzzle. Hagstofa LAU04109 (quarterly wage index) shows that ICT sector wages ran 3.1% below the economy-wide average by Q4 2024 — a reversal of the typical technology premium that existed in earlier periods.

If the ICT sector is experiencing productivity displacement, wage dynamics may reflect the composition effect: higher-paid workers being displaced, while the remaining workforce is paid closer to market rates for a reduced headcount. Alternatively, the wage compression may reflect the entry of lower-paid ICT workers (offshore contractors, junior roles) replacing more senior domestic employees. The data does not distinguish between these explanations.

The wage compression in a supposedly high-value sector is in direct tension with the Vaxtarplan's narrative of ICT and tech as premium-wage job creators. If the technology sector's wages are converging toward the economy average while employment falls, the "knowledge economy jobs" narrative needs empirical grounding that the current data does not provide.

### E.4 The Mass Layoff Register

Vinnumálastofnun maintains a mandatory register of mass layoff notifications. Analysis of this register (2024-2026) provides the ground-level employment disruption signal:

**2024 H1:** 10 notifications, ~598 employees. Concentrated in fish processing (201), public services (146), passenger transport (139), retail (98), education (14).

**2025 Key Events:**
- August 2025: Includes "computer/electronics/optical products" sector — first tech-adjacent entry in register
- September 2025 through December 2025: Concentrated in fish processing, food industry, wholesale fish, cinema
- October 2025: Fish processing (aquaculture-adjacent)
- November 2025: Icelandair -38 (Boeing 767 phase-out, explicitly not AI-related)

**2026 (February):** Two notifications, 69 employees. IT sector: 24 positions (implementation April-September 2026). Scientific research: 45 positions. This is the first IT-sector mass layoff notification in the register's recent history.

**2026 (January):** Two notifications, 84 employees total.

The mass layoff register's evidence for AI-driven displacement is thin: the February 2026 IT sector notification (24 employees) is the first concrete documented case. The total mass-layoff-documented IT displacement is orders of magnitude smaller than the 1,300-job decline visible in the LFS occupation data. This scale mismatch — 24 documented versus 1,300 in aggregate — is one of the central puzzles of the investigation.

The likely explanation: the 1,300-job ICT employment decline is not happening through large visible layoffs. It is happening through attrition (not replacing departing workers), reduced hiring, and small reductions (below the mass layoff notification threshold) spread across the sector's 1,100+ enterprises. This mode of displacement is almost invisible to labor market monitoring systems designed to detect mass events.

It is the demographic with the highest rates of diagnosis in the fastest-growing category (psychiatric and stress-adjacent). And it is the demographic that the labor market data shows experiencing the first-ever gender reversal in unemployment (female 25-54 unemployment exceeding male in Q4 2025).

Whether these two signals — welfare acceleration and unemployment reversal — are related cannot be determined from available data. The TR does not publish occupation-level data for claimants. Statistics Iceland does not cross-reference LFS unemployment spells with subsequent welfare entry at the individual level. The causal pathway, if it exists, is administratively invisible.

### F.3 The September 2025 Reform Disruption

Iceland's September 2025 disability and rehabilitation reform introduced 11 simultaneous changes:

1. New unified disability pension category (replacing three former categories)
2. Partial disability pension (new benefit type)
3. Rehabilitation pension renamed "medical and rehabilitation allowance"
4. Integrated expert assessment process
5. Standardized income reduction rate (45%)
6. Strengthened employment incentives
7. Extended rehabilitation timeframes
8. New assistance category for persons awaiting treatment
9. Job-search support upon rehabilitation completion
10. National coordination teams
11. Service portal for provider coordination

The category consolidation (change 1) mechanically inflated headcount figures by transferring disability grant recipients into the disability pension count. This means pre-September 2025 and post-September 2025 data are not directly comparable without adjustment.

The reform is not without merit — the previous system was fragmented, the assessment process was inconsistent, and the gender concentration of older women in physically demanding work was an identified policy concern. TR was conducting its own research into why women 50+ are disproportionately represented. But the simultaneous implementation of 11 changes eliminates years of data series comparability at precisely the moment when longitudinal tracking of welfare system inflows is analytically most important.

### F.4 The Sick Leave Pipeline

Iceland's sick leave data provides a potential early signal of stress accumulating before welfare system entry. Public sector sick leave averages 16 days per year versus 8 days in the private sector (Iceland Monitor, February 2026). This 2:1 ratio is substantial and suggests that the public sector's workforce — heavily female, concentrated in care and administrative roles — is experiencing disproportionate health-related absences.

The Sjúkratryggingar Íslands (the Health Insurance Institute) administers sick leave benefits but does not publish diagnosis breakdowns for sick leave claims. This gap — confirmed by the Panopticon investigation — means the pipeline between workplace stress, sick leave, and eventual welfare entry is administratively invisible at every stage.

The five-stage pipeline that matters is: AI deployment → workplace stress → increased sick leave → rehabilitation entry → disability pension. Each transition is plausible given available evidence. None can be traced at the individual level with existing data. This is not a critique of government intent — it reflects a data architecture that was not designed for AI-era governance challenges.

## Appendix G: The Academic Literature Landscape

### G.1 The Global Evidence Base

The empirical literature on AI's labor market effects is substantial but contested. Three lines of evidence are most relevant to Iceland's situation:

**Occupational task exposure analysis (Acemoglu, Eloundou et al., Frey and Osborne):**
The foundational approach estimates the fraction of tasks within each occupation that AI can, in principle, automate. Frey and Osborne (2013) estimated 47% of US occupations at high risk. Eloundou et al. (2024) find that 80% of the US workforce is in occupations where at least 10% of tasks are exposed to LLMs. The government's "55% of Icelandic jobs affected" figure is derived from applying this framework to Iceland's occupational distribution — it is a task-exposure estimate, not a displacement measurement.

The critical limitation of task-exposure approaches: exposure does not equal displacement. Whether firms actually substitute AI for workers depends on cost, regulatory environment, worker bargaining power, competitive dynamics, and organizational capabilities. The Danish evidence (Humlum and Vestergaard 2025) suggests that in a high-trust, high-flexicurity Nordic economy, LLM adoption produced minimal measurable displacement effects in its first two years. Whether Iceland, with different institutional arrangements, will show similar patterns is unknown.

**Event study / difference-in-differences evidence (Humlum and Vestergaard 2025):**
The most credible available study used Danish administrative registers to compare workers in ChatGPT-exposed versus unexposed occupations before and after ChatGPT's November 2022 release. Using 500,000+ workers across multiple years, the study found essentially null effects on earnings, hours, and employment probability. The confidence intervals are tight enough to rule out large negative effects.

This finding is the most important single piece of evidence for calibrating Iceland's AI displacement concerns. If the world's most rigorous study of LLM adoption's labor effects finds null results in a Nordic economy, the prior for Iceland should be cautious. However, the Danish study covers a specific period (2022-2024), uses data from a country with Denmark's flexicurity system and high baseline AI adoption (42% enterprise adoption by 2025), and does not address longer-term structural effects or sector-specific patterns.

The ICT trifecta — employment down 11.4%, revenue up 72%, vacancies down 94% — is not captured by the kind of occupation-level analysis Humlum and Vestergaard conducted. The trifecta is a sector-level signal of the type that, at greater magnification (individual firms, specific roles), might show the displacement the aggregate disappears in.

**Firm-level evidence (Brynjolfsson et al., MIT GenAI experiments):**
Controlled experiments deploying AI coding assistants, customer service AI, and writing assistance tools in real workplaces have documented measurable productivity effects. A widely-cited MIT study found that access to a generative AI coding assistant increased programmer productivity by 55% in well-defined tasks. Customer service studies (including the Klarna case) documented significant throughput increases per agent.

These firm-level productivity gains are well-established. What the experiments do not establish is what happens to employment *at the firm level* over a longer period, or what happens *in aggregate* across an industry as all firms adopt similar tools simultaneously. When every firm in a sector gains 30-55% productivity, competitive equilibria shift: prices may fall, output may rise, market share may shift toward more productive firms. The net employment effect depends on which of these dominates.

### G.2 The Acemoglu Framework Applied to Iceland

Daron Acemoglu's "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI" (2024) provides the most useful analytical framework for Iceland's situation. Acemoglu distinguishes between two AI effect scenarios:

**Scenario A (Complementarity):** AI increases the productivity of human workers, raising the demand for their skills. Workers who can collaborate effectively with AI tools are worth more to employers. Wages and employment rise. This is the "augmentation" scenario the Vaxtarplan assumes.

**Scenario B (Substitution):** AI directly substitutes for tasks previously performed by human workers. Firms maintain or increase output while reducing labor input. Wages and employment fall for workers in affected occupations. This is the "displacement" scenario the Vaxtarplan does not address.

Acemoglu's analysis suggests the aggregate outcome depends on the share of tasks where AI is a complement versus a substitute, the elasticity of labor demand in affected sectors, and the speed at which workers can redeploy to less-exposed occupations. His empirical estimates suggest that optimistic productivity assumptions (AI generating strong complementarities across most occupations) require assumptions about technological progress and human-AI collaboration that are not supported by the available evidence.

The ICT sector trifecta is a real-world outcome consistent with Acemoglu's substitution scenario: the firms producing and deploying AI technology are themselves reducing headcount while increasing revenue. This is not surprising — the productivity tools are most aggressively adopted by the firms closest to them. What matters for Iceland's labor market is whether this pattern will generalize across sectors as AI adoption spreads.

### G.3 The Collingridge Dilemma and Iceland's Policy Challenge

The Collingridge dilemma, formulated in the 1980s, describes a fundamental tension in technology governance: early in a technology's development, its effects are uncertain, but governance interventions have the most leverage. Once the technology is entrenched and its effects are visible, the leverage for governance intervention is much reduced.

Iceland faces this dilemma acutely. The ICT employment decline is a visible signal of something happening. But because Iceland lacks the measurement infrastructure to interpret the signal — to determine whether it reflects AI-driven displacement, cyclical factors, or structural rebalancing — governance intervention cannot be calibrated. The measurement vacuum and the governance vacuum are not independent problems. The measurement vacuum makes the governance vacuum inevitable.

The Nordic response to the Collingridge dilemma is to build measurement infrastructure early, before effects are large enough to be obviously alarming, so that governance can be calibrated to actual rather than projected effects. Denmark invested in its linked administrative data infrastructure years before it was needed for AI-labor research. Norway negotiated labor protections into collective agreements before algorithmic management was pervasive. These are not reactive measures — they are anticipatory investments in the capacity to observe and respond.

Iceland's response to the Collingridge dilemma, as evidenced by the three strategy documents, is to proceed on the assumption that the optimistic scenario is operative. This is not irrational — the optimistic scenario may well be correct. But it is ungovernable: if the assumption is wrong, there is no mechanism to detect the error.

## Appendix H: The Five Sector Strategies — Detailed Analysis

### H.1 Nýsköpun og tækniþróun (Innovation and Technology Development)

The Vaxtarplan's innovation strategy centers on creating conditions for high-tech industries to flourish: research infrastructure, startup financing, intellectual property commercialization, and R&D support. The ISK 1.1 billion AI infrastructure investment is the flagship action.

The strategy's theory here has internal coherence: Iceland's small scale makes it difficult to compete on manufacturing or services at volume, but creates advantages in niche technology development (Mideind's language AI, Lucinity's AML, Controlant's cold chain are examples of the model). The challenge is that Iceland's AI research capacity — approximately 40-50 researchers concentrated in two institutions — is thin relative to the investment being proposed.

The innovation strategy does not address the composition of innovation: whether AI infrastructure investment will primarily support the small number of genuine builders (Mideind, Lucinity, Controlant, DataLab) or will primarily benefit the larger number of resellers (Advania, Origo, Nyherji) who package international AI tools. These two groups have different implications for indigenous capability development and for labor market effects.

If Iceland's ISK 1.1 billion AI infrastructure investment primarily subsidizes Microsoft Copilot deployment by enterprises, the primary effect is accelerating the AI adoption that reduces employment in deployed-at sectors. If it primarily funds indigenous AI development at the builder companies and in universities, the primary effect is creating high-skill employment. The strategy does not specify which path it intends to fund.

### H.2 Matvæli (Food/Agriculture)

The food and agriculture sector strategy in the Vaxtarplan focuses on value-added food production, aquaculture expansion, and the intersection of Iceland's clean energy advantage with food processing. The action plan's Priority 4 aquaculture actions (3.5-3.7) reflect the industry's growth trajectory and governance complexity.

Iceland's aquaculture sector has expanded significantly. The value chain for aquaculture — from hatchery to harvest to processing to export — is increasingly automation-intensive at every stage. The processing end (scaling, filleting, portion cutting) is already highly mechanized and is a target for AI-enabled visual inspection and autonomous processing.

What the strategy does not address: the aquaculture and fisheries processing sector is a significant employer of foreign nationals, including in physically demanding roles. The Rikisendurskoðun data shows that foreign nationals constitute approximately 55% of registered unemployed — at an unemployment rate of 8.9% versus 2.3% for Icelandic nationals. Automation in food processing may disproportionately affect this already-vulnerable segment of the labor market.

The property tax actions (3.7 aquaculture property tax) and special area designations (3.6) are governance measures that will affect the distribution of rents within the industry. These are legitimate policy instruments. Their relationship to labor market outcomes — whether they encourage employment-preserving production models or purely output-maximizing automation — is not addressed in the strategy.

### H.3 Orkusækinn iðnaður (Energy-Intensive Industry)

The energy-intensive industry strategy is primarily about attracting large-scale industrial and computational facilities that consume significant electricity and leverage Iceland's renewable advantage. This includes existing aluminum smelting, silicon production, and potential data center expansion.

The data center provision deserves extended analysis. Large-scale AI computing infrastructure (GPU clusters, inference farms, model training facilities) has become a geopolitically and economically significant sector globally. Iceland's combination of cheap renewable electricity (~$30-50/MWh compared to $80-120/MWh in continental Europe), cold climate (natural cooling), and political stability makes it a plausible location for such facilities.

But data centers are not employment-dense. A large hyperscale data center (100+ MW) typically employs 50-200 people directly. The indirect employment through construction, maintenance, and supply chain is larger but time-limited. The revenue-to-employment ratio for data centers is high in the direction of capital intensity — exactly the pattern the Stöðugreining identifies as Iceland's current problem (capital per worker declining, TFP below peer average).

If Iceland's energy transition scenario produces 35-45 TWh electricity demand by 2050, and a significant fraction of that demand is from AI data centers, the economic outcome may be high revenue (export of electricity-embedded computing services) with limited employment creation. This is a tenable development model, but it is not the productivity-and-employment growth story the Vaxtarplan implies.

### H.4 Upplifun (Tourism/Experience Economy)

Tourism — at 32% of Icelandic exports — is the largest single component of Iceland's export composition. The strategy's tourism actions (Priority 4, Actions 1-4) address a set of legitimate governance problems: tourist site degradation from overtourism, fragmented international marketing, and inadequate revenue capture from high-volume sites.

The site fee proposal (Action 4.1) is modeled on practices at Faroe Islands, Bhutan, and parts of New Zealand — high-value, low-volume tourism that charges for environmental access. This is a revenue optimization strategy for natural sites, not a labor market intervention.

The productivity-employment tension in tourism deserves explicit articulation. Tourism at Iceland's current scale (roughly 14,700 employed in accommodation and food service) is labor-intensive relative to its revenue contribution. The sector's historical growth was driven by volume increases (more tourists, more nights) rather than per-visitor value increases. The strategy's aspiration — higher value, lower volume tourism — could go either way for employment:

**Scenario 1 (positive):** Higher-value tourism requires more skilled, higher-paid front-line workers (guides, experience designers, culinary professionals). Employment may not grow in absolute terms but wages rise.

**Scenario 2 (negative):** Higher-value tourism is achieved through automation (AI-personalized itineraries, automated booking and concierge, smart accommodation) with a smaller, more skilled workforce. Employment falls.

The AI Action Plan's pilot data — "2,250 staff hours saved, 189,000 km travel reduced" from public sector AI pilots — suggests that even modest AI deployments in service-adjacent functions produce measurable labor savings. Scaling this to a 14,700-person tourism sector implies material employment effects.

The Varda 2026 survey found that creative industries workers (51% reporting increased work pressure from technology) and early education workers (50%) showed the highest technology-related stress signals among sectors adjacent to the tourism and experience economy. The workers who translate, guide, market, and administratively support Iceland's tourism sector are already experiencing what the strategy projects will come.

### H.5 Heilsa og loftslag (Health and Climate)

The life and health sciences actions (3.8-3.9) represent Iceland's most credible path to high-value, knowledge-intensive, export-oriented employment growth. Iceland's genetic homogeneity, the deCODE Genetics intellectual legacy (now Amgen Iceland), and existing health research infrastructure create genuine comparative advantages in genomics and precision medicine.

The ISK 200-400 million annual health tech investment is smaller than what comparable cluster investments have required in Denmark (the Medicon Valley biotechnology cluster, created with public-private investment over two decades). But it is a real commitment in a sector where Iceland has demonstrable research assets.

The climate intersection — carbon capture and storage, green hydrogen, and Iceland's geothermal infrastructure — is less developed as a sector-building strategy. The action plan's carbon capture innovation framework (Action 3.4) is primarily a regulatory enabling mechanism, not a funded investment.

## Appendix I: What "Flying Blind" Means for the Vaxtarplan

The thesis of the accompanying Panopticon research project — *Iceland Is Flying Blind on AI* — frames the governance failure as an epistemological problem: Iceland cannot observe what AI is doing to its labor market because the observational instruments do not exist.

The Vaxtarplan 2035 is a well-intentioned strategy flying the same instruments. It projects productivity growth, STEM employment growth, and export diversification — all calibrated against international comparators that Iceland cannot verify apply to its specific situation. It proposes investments in AI infrastructure without building the feedback mechanisms that would reveal whether those investments are creating the employment and productivity outcomes the strategy anticipates.

**Concretely:** if the ISK 1.1 billion AI infrastructure investment is deployed successfully and Iceland's enterprise AI adoption rises from its unknown baseline to Nordic-comparable levels (say, 30-40%), the strategy predicts productivity growth and augmentation of knowledge workers. But if the ICT trifecta pattern generalizes — revenue up, employment down, vacancies near zero — the strategy will have successfully deployed AI at scale while producing unemployment among the knowledge workers it was supposed to augment. Iceland will have no mechanism to detect this outcome until it shows up in aggregate unemployment statistics, years later, confounded by a dozen other factors.

This is not a hypothetical risk. It is the pattern already visible in Iceland's most AI-exposed sector. The ICT sector's post-2022 evolution — with its 72% revenue growth, -11.4% employment decline, and 94% vacancy collapse — is a preview at sector scale of what could happen at economy scale if AI deployment proceeds without the monitoring, labor protections, and transition support that would enable early detection and response.

The Vaxtarplan has the ambition to transform Iceland's growth model from labor-intensive to productivity-intensive. That transformation, if AI is the primary productivity driver, will involve a period of disruption for workers in AI-exposed occupations. Whether that disruption is navigable — whether it produces transitions into better jobs or exits into welfare — depends on institutions that the strategy does not build.

Iceland has the institutional capacity to build those instruments. Hagstofa Íslands operates a world-class statistical agency that maintains the Hagstofa PxWeb API with 10+ labor market tables covering ~150,000 rows of employment data. The data infrastructure exists. What is missing is the mandate to assemble it into a monitoring system designed for the AI era.

The Vaxtarplan 2035 will be evaluated, in time, against the economic outcomes it produces. If productivity growth and diversified exports materialize at the projected scale, the strategy will be judged a success. If knowledge-sector displacement accumulates faster than the upskilling pipeline can absorb workers, the strategy will be judged to have been blind to its most important risk.

The difference between those two outcomes cannot be detected with the instruments Iceland currently has. That is what "flying blind" means. And it is what the Vaxtarplan 2035 — for all its analytical sophistication and genuine investment commitments — does not fix.
