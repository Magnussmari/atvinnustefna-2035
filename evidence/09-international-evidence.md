## 4. ACADEMIC STUDIES (2025–2026)

### 4.1 Humlum & Vestergaard (2025) — NBER WP 33777

**Full citation**: Anders Humlum and Emilie Vestergaard, "Large Language Models, Small Labor Market Effects," NBER Working Paper No. 33777 (2025).

**Methodology**: Linked large-scale adoption surveys to Danish administrative labor records. Covered 25,000 workers across 7,000 workplaces in 11 occupations (accountants, customer support, financial advisors, HR professionals, IT support, journalists, legal professionals, marketing, office clerks, software developers, teachers).

**Key findings**:
- **No significant impact on earnings or recorded hours in any occupation**
- Confidence intervals rule out effects larger than **1%**
- Null results hold even for intensive users, early adopters, workplaces with substantial AI investment
- Average productivity time savings: **3%** — with weak wage pass-through
- Adoption is linked to **occupational switching and task restructuring** but without net changes in hours or earnings

**Interpretation for report**: This is the most rigorous causal study available on LLM labor market effects using administrative data. The "small effects" finding is often cited to minimize displacement concerns. However, three caveats apply:
1. The study covers **early adoption (2023-2024)** — the displacement is likely lagged
2. Task restructuring without earnings changes may reflect **structural underemployment** rather than stability
3. The 3% productivity gain, if sustained and compounded across the economy, represents substantial long-run demand-side shifts

**Sources**: [NBER WP 33777](https://www.nber.org/papers/w33777) | [Full PDF](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w33777/w33777.pdf) | [SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5250742)

### 4.2 Acemoglu — "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI"

**Author**: Daron Acemoglu (MIT; 2024 Nobel Prize in Economics co-laureate)

**Published**: 2024, widely cited through 2025–2026

**Key findings**:
- AI TFP impact: **0.53%–0.66% over a decade** — substantially below optimistic projections
- Uses task-based model: macroeconomic consequences governed by Hulten's theorem (fraction of tasks affected × average cost savings)
- AI job displacement will be "substantial, but gradual" — room for retraining if policy acts
- Major concern is **income distribution**, not just total employment — AI may widen wealth inequality even under full employment
- Policy recommendation: Encourage AI regulations promoting **augmentation** and discouraging excessive displacement

**February 2026**: Acemoglu publicly warned that "U.S. democracy won't survive the AI job-pocalypse" without policy intervention, amplifying the political urgency of the analysis.

**Sources**: [MIT Economics paper](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/2024-04/The%20Simple%20Macroeconomics%20of%20AI.pdf) | [Fortune profile February 2026](https://fortune.com/2026/02/22/who-is-daron-acemoglu-nobel-laureate-ai-job-layoffs-economic-inequality-donald-trump/)

**Relevance to Iceland**: Acemoglu's distribution concern is directly relevant to Iceland's high-equality social contract. Even if aggregate employment holds, AI could degrade wage growth for workers in automatable roles — a risk invisible to policies focused on unemployment rates alone.

**Finding**: AI is eliminating **16,000 U.S. jobs per month**, with Gen Z (entry-level workers) disproportionately affected.

**Methodology**: Analysis of 40 years of data on technological disruption "scarring" effects.

**Key data points**:

- Entry-level, white-collar, administrative roles most exposed: data entry, customer service, legal support, billing

- Long-term: AI automation will displace roughly **6–7% of the U.S. workforce** (~11 million workers)

## 5. EU AI ACT — EEA/ICELAND IMPLEMENTATION TIMELINE

### Implementation Milestones

| Date | Requirement | Status |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **August 1, 2024** | AI Act entered into force | Complete |
| **February 2, 2025** | Prohibited AI practices banned; AI literacy obligations active | **In effect** |
| **August 2, 2025** | General-purpose AI rules apply; national governance structures required; Member States designate competent authorities | **In effect** |
| **August 2, 2026** | High-risk AI system requirements apply — including all **employment-related AI** (recruiting, screening, performance evaluation, termination) | **Approaching — 4 months away** |
| **August 2, 2027** | Full application to all AI systems | Forthcoming |

### Workplace and Employment Provisions (High-Risk, from August 2026)

AI used in employment contexts is **explicitly classified as high-risk** under Annex III of the AI Act. Obligations include:

- **Worker notification**: Employers must inform workers before deploying high-risk AI in the workplace
- **Human oversight**: Designated individuals must have competence, authority, and technical ability to intervene and override AI outputs
- **Non-discrimination monitoring**: Continuous monitoring for discriminatory AI outputs required
- **Logging and transparency**: Decision logs must be maintained
- **Workers' representative consultation**: Where applicable under national law

Prohibited (since February 2025): Emotion recognition in workplaces; biometric categorization of workers based on protected characteristics.

### EEA Applicability to Iceland

The EU AI Act carries the designation **"EEA relevant"**, triggering mandatory review for incorporation into the EEA Agreement.

- Iceland, Norway, and Liechtenstein are **participating as observers** in EU AI Board meetings
- The AI Act is expected to **become Icelandic law** via the EEA Agreement, with no standalone Icelandic AI legislation anticipated
- **No formal incorporation decision has yet been made** as of April 2026 — this creates a regulatory gap
- Norway's situation is identical: EEA incorporation expected but not yet formalized

**Sources**: [EU AI Act Implementation Timeline](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/implementation-timeline/) | [Ogletree: Initial Obligations February 2025](https://ogletree.com/insights-resources/blog-posts/eu-publishes-groundbreaking-ai-act-initial-obligations-set-to-take-effect-on-february-2-2025/) | [Crowell & Moring: AI & HR 2026 Legal Overview](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/artificial-intelligence-and-human-resources-in-the-eu-a-2026-legal-overview) | [White & Case: Norway AI Watch](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-our-thinking/ai-watch-global-regulatory-tracker-norway) | [OneTrust: Digital Omnibus 2026](https://www.onetrust.com/blog/how-the-eu-digital-omnibus-reshapes-ai-act-timelines-and-governance-in-2026/)

**Relevance to Iceland**: August 2, 2026 is a hard deadline — just 4 months from the date of this report. Iceland's employment strategy should be actively preparing for AI employment compliance obligations, but this legislative timeline is absent from the current strategy document. Employers using AI in HR processes will face legal obligations well before any policy framework is in place.

### National Strategies

All five Nordic countries (Denmark, Finland, Sweden, Norway, and Iceland) have developed national AI strategies. The Nordic model's distinctive features — high union density, flat organizational hierarchies, co-determination, and high social trust — are being cited as both **assets** (easier collective negotiation on AI governance) and **vulnerabilities** (assumptions of worker buy-in may mask displacement until late in the cycle).

**Key theme emerging in 2026**: Nordic AI governance is moving from strategy documents toward **collective agreement and co-determination frameworks**. The Nordic AI Union Summit specifically targets the negotiation gap — the absence of AI clauses in labor agreements.

### Denmark — Closest Comparator

Denmark's 42% enterprise AI adoption rate (highest in EU, +14.5pp in one year) has created acute labor market pressures. Denmark's flexicurity model is being tested: rapid redeployment assumes new jobs exist; if AI displaces faster than new roles emerge, flexicurity's passive income support becomes the dominant mechanism, which is fiscally expensive at scale.

**Relevance to Iceland**: Iceland has historically benchmarked against Denmark for labor market policy. Denmark's adoption rate is where Iceland is heading. Iceland's employment strategy needs to incorporate the Danish experience, including the early evidence from Humlum & Vestergaard (WP 33777) which specifically studied Danish workers.

## METHODOLOGICAL NOTES

1. **Attribution complexity**: "AI-driven layoffs" is a contested category. Companies have incentives to cite AI for workforce reductions that may have other causes (overhiring in 2021-2022, macroeconomic correction, trade tariff uncertainty in 2026). The range of 20.4% (strict explicit attribution) to 47.9% (broad AI-cited) should be presented as a confidence interval, not a point estimate.

2. **Lag effects**: Humlum & Vestergaard's near-zero labor market effects (2025) may reflect early adoption phase. Most economists expect displacement effects to intensify 2026–2028 as models improve and enterprise deployment matures.

3. **Iceland's position**: Small open economies with high human capital (like Iceland) historically experience technology transitions through **redeployment** rather than mass unemployment. However, this redeployment requires active policy intervention — which is precisely what the current Atvinnustefna does not provide.

4. **Sectoral exposure**: Iceland's economy is concentrated in tourism (AI-exposed at managerial/support level), fisheries (partial AI exposure), financial services (high AI exposure), and creative industries (high AI exposure). A sectoral displacement risk matrix would strengthen the report.
