# Research Synthesis: Atvinnustefna Íslands 2035

**Date:** 2026-04-10
**Method:** 7-sector parallel research + adversarial review

---

## Cross-Sector Dependencies

1. **Document analysis (Sector 2) validates Sector 1 claims:** The blog draft claimed AI is mentioned only as positive. Text extraction CONFIRMED: 3 positive mentions, 0 negative, 0 risk-related terms.

2. **Institutional reports (Sector 3) provide the normative framework:** Every major international institution (IMF, WEF, OECD, ILO, Goldman Sachs, McKinsey) has published AI employment risk assessments. Iceland has none.

3. **Iceland vulnerability data (Sector 4) quantifies domestic risk:** 28.7% of workforce in public administration — the sector most exposed to AI automation per IBM, Microsoft models. Tourism support roles also exposed per Klarna model.

4. **Reskilling data (Sector 6) exposes the policy gap:** At $24,800/worker (WEF), reskilling even 10% of Iceland's workforce would cost ~$585M. The policy mentions neither the need nor the budget.

## Cross-Sector Conflicts

1. **Magnitude dispute:** Q1 2026 layoff data varies by source:
   - Tom's Hardware/Nikkei: ~78,557 (47.9% AI)
   - Challenger: 52,050 tech sector YTD (40% increase, but AI "only" 13% of all cuts)
   - These are different measurement scopes — Challenger tracks all US layoffs, not just tech

2. **Net impact debate:** WEF says +78M net jobs globally; Goldman Sachs projects +7% GDP. But the TRANSITION cost is the issue, not the net outcome. Both can be true: net positive AND devastating transition.

3. **"AI washing" challenge:** Andreessen, Altman, and even Benioff himself suggest companies use AI as excuse. This weakens the raw numbers but strengthens the structural argument — even if 50% of "AI layoffs" are overstated, 50% are real.

## Reinforcements (Convergent Findings)

- ALL six international institutions agree AI will significantly disrupt employment
- ALL agree reskilling/retraining is essential
- ALL recommend proactive government policy
- The OECD, of which Iceland is a member, specifically identifies 27% of jobs at high risk
- The ILO specifically flags gender inequality in AI impact (women more exposed)
- Iceland's own data shows 38% of women work in public sector — the sector most at risk

## Adversarial Findings (Honest Oracle)

**Strongest counterargument:** The "Luddite Fallacy" — every prior technology panic was wrong. David Autor (MIT) argues AI could REBUILD middle-class jobs if used as decision support, not replacement. This is intellectually serious and must be addressed.

**Response:** The blog addresses this head-on in Section VI. The Luddite Fallacy argument concerns NET long-term outcomes. The critique of Atvinnustefna concerns the ABSENCE of any analysis — not a prediction of doom. Even an optimistic government should have a risk assessment.

**Second strongest counterargument:** Iceland has structural advantages — small population, high education, strong social safety net, union coverage.

**Response:** Also addressed in Section VI. These advantages are real but don't excuse the absence of planning. A fire department doesn't skip the pre-incident plan because the building has sprinklers.

## What The Evidence Says AGAINST This Analysis

1. Overall US/global unemployment remains low despite AI deployment
2. Many "AI layoffs" may be relabeling of normal business cycles
3. Small, agile economies may adapt faster than large ones
4. The 47.9% figure specifically is from Nikkei Asia — one source, widely recited
5. WEF's net positive projection (+78M) undermines doom narratives
6. Iceland's tourism core product (nature/experience) is not automatable
7. IBM's example actually shows AI leading to MORE total employment
8. The NBER CFO study calls 502,000 cuts "not the doomsday scenario" (0.4% of workforce)
